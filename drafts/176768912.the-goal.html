But I have a theory about why this might work more broadly, and why mainstream psychology structurally cannot discover this even if I’m right.]

The Problem: Gold-Standard SAD Treatment Kinda Sucks

The gold-standard treatment for social anxiety disorder is Cognitive Behavioral Therapy combined with Exposure and Response Prevention (CBT+ERP). Meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials show approximately 40% remission rates, of which roughly half are maintained long-term.

That’s a 20% success rate for the best treatment we have.

Guys. That’s dogshit.

For comparison: exposure therapy for specific phobias has remission rates around 60-90%. OCD responds to ERP at similar rates. We have genuinely effective treatments for other anxiety disorders. Social anxiety is the weird outlier where our best therapeutic technology just... doesn’t work that well.

Why?

Why CBT+ERP Fails: The Response Prevention Problem

I laid out in a previous post why exposure therapy for social anxiety is fundamentally harder than for other anxiety disorders. The short version:

Real exposure and response prevention for social anxiety would require banning all impression management behaviors. Not just the obvious external safety behaviors like avoiding eye contact or clutching drinks, but the internal ones too: planning conversation topics, post-event rumination about whether you said something stupid, trying to figure out what someone wants to hear before you say it.

In practice, therapists attempt to ban a very small handful of safety behaviors such as drink-clutching or staying silent in conversation. And probably this helps on the margin! But it’s not the same as banning every semblance of effort you’re making at stopping people from thinking poorly of you.

Why Academia Cannot Study This Even In Principle

Let’s say, hypothetically, I’m right about everything. 100% correct. The intervention that actually works for social anxiety requires people to accept the risk of minor social rejections and alienations as the non-negotiable core of the therapeutic mechanism.

How does this knowledge make it into the therapy textbooks?

The answer is, of course, that it doesn’t. It can’t. Because IRBs explicitly prohibit research that risks social harm to participants. Any intervention that requires participants to risk looking weird, being rejected, or alienating others in their main social groups as the core mechanism of action—that’s not getting IRB approval. Ever.

So what happens instead? Researchers do “social mishap studies” where patients intentionally spill drinks or pause awkwardly during presentations. Tiny, controlled fuck-ups that demonstrate perfectionistic standards aren’t necessary. Which is great! But also severely limited, because the real anxieties people have are stuff like “what if I express a political opinion someone hates” or “what if I ask someone out and it’s weird forever” or “what if I join this friend group and they all secretly wish I hadn’t.” Sometimes those fears are accurate and come true!

Academic psychology, in its current form, cannot study interventions for those fears. Not because researchers don’t want to, necessarily, but because the institutional structure prohibits it.

If I’m right, then effective treatment for social anxiety exists in a permanent blind spot of academic research and always will.

If you want a vision of the future, imagine a boot with “20% long-term remission” written on its sole, stamping on a human face, forever.

The Dating Anxiety Gap

There’s another structural problem: academic psychology is deeply uninterested in dating anxiety as a research focus.

This makes sense from their perspective. It’s a reputationally risky subfield— too close to PUA territory, too tied up with gender politics, too easy to get cancelled over. New academics will avoid it (career suicide) even as established academics already have their research directions set.

The field focuses on social anxiety disorder, specifically the severe cases where someone can’t function in society. Which is fair! Those people need help more urgently.

But this leaves a huge gap: subclinical social/romantic anxiety that makes people miserable but not dysfunctional enough to qualify for treatment. Guys who can hold jobs and have friends but freeze up around women. People who can make small talk but can’t form deep connections. The enormous population of anxious nerds who are functional but unhappy. Nobody’s studying them or building interventions for them.

The Metis Problem: Good Practitioners Can’t Scale

Meanwhile, there ARE people who seem to know how to teach this stuff. Dating coaches like the woman who ran my workshop (though it must be said that her success rates were also not great). Therapists like Chris Lakin who do deep 1:1 work. The Fully Known guys with their… physical activation techniques? I’m still not quite sure how the Fully Known guys model this stuff.

They have real metis: practical craft knowledge that clearly works when implemented by skilled practitioners. But it’s all 1:1. Workshops with 10 guys, hour-long therapy sessions, direct observation and real-time adjustment.

This is great for the people who can access it and afford it. But it doesn’t scale and it will never scale. There aren’t enough skilled practitioners and there never will be.

Why I Think There’s An Opportunity

All of this suggests there’s massive low-hanging fruit in this domain.

We know from MCT that banning rumination/threat-monitoring helps with anxiety. I strongly suspect from my own experience (and now a very small handful of Slutcon attendees) that aggressive goal-replacement can work. I believe the theoretical mechanisms are sound.

But nobody’s systematizing it into a transmissible protocol because:

  • Academic researchers can’t study the most effective interventions

  • Skilled practitioners are doing 1:1 work that can’t scale

  • The field isn’t interested in the subclinical dating anxiety population

That means someone working outside these constraints— say, some random guy with a blog who isn’t bound by IRBs or professional reputation management— might be able to make real progress.

What Success Would Look Like

Let me be clear about what I’m trying to build:

NOT: One weird trick that instantly cures social anxiety.

NOT: Some kind of, idk, guru thing where people need ongoing access to me personally.

YES: A set of blog posts or lectures that gives subclinically anxious people adequate mental scaffolding to have a >20% probability of slowly bootstrapping themselves out of their anxiety over 3-5 months

The model is something like: you attend a workshop or read a series of posts, you get the core concepts and protocols, and then you do the integration work yourself.

This was what worked for me. One workshop, months of integration. Nobody held my hand through it, but I had the right conceptual toolkit and in the end that was enough. The question is: can this be packaged well enough that it reliably works for others?

Why This Might Actually Be Possible

CBT homework is already done independently. Therapists give instructions and send people off to execute them. The transmission mechanism for therapeutic techniques is ALREADY “here are some concepts and protocols, go implement them.”

So there’s no obvious reason well-designed content couldn’t replicate the key parts. Especially if I:

  • Make the protocols as concrete and actionable as possible

  • Provide clear decision frameworks for ambiguous situations

  • Build in troubleshooting for common failure modes

The Slutcon results suggest the core mechanism works (at least short-term) when people actually execute on it. The main unknowns are:

  • How well does this transfer outside permissive, high-motivation contexts like Slutcon?

  • Does it stick long-term or do people regress?

  • What are the failure modes and how do we address them?

I’m planning follow-ups to start answering these questions.

The Lofty Part

My actual goal (I know this sounds insane) is to figure out an order to put words in such that reading those words will one-shot social/romantic anxiety for a significant subset of the anxious-nerd population.

This is ambitious, given that I might be a crank who got lucky once. But also: if I’m right about the structural barriers, nobody else is positioned to try this. Academic researchers can’t study it. Practitioners can’t scale it. The field isn’t focused on it.

Worth a shot!
