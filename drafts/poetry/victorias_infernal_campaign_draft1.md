# Victoria's Infernal Campaign
## A Parliamentary Inquiry in Ottava Rima

### I. The Scene

The House convened; the benches full for war,
Since sixty thousand British souls lay dead,
And all the stores of England gone before
To furnish Hell with jam and toast and bread.
The PM rose, that statesman grave and hoar,
And bowed his head and mourned the valiant dead:
"The late campaign was much to be deplored—
But field affairs? Lord Ashworth held the sword."

### II. The War Minister

Lord Ashworth rose, his dignity intact:
"The men were well equipped for tropic climes,
In khaki drill with extra socks well-packed—
What more could any office do at such times?
The fault lay with the Foreign Office's tact:
Lord Pemberton had sworn, through several mimes,
That Hell was not so hot as all supposed—
The question, then: why had he so deposed?"

### III. The Foreign Secretary

Lord Pemberton stood up with wounded pride:
"My sources were impeccable and sound!
A vicar down in Shropshire prophesied
That Hell possessed a pleasant, temperate ground.
Besides, the men reached Hell in proper stride—
The fault was in the landing, I'll be bound:
The Admiralty picked the beachhead spot,
And marched them straight into a lake—quite hot."

### IV. The Admiralty

Lord Blackwood of the Admiralty rose,
His face gone red with patriotic rage:
"The Navy did its part, as everyone knows!
We sailed them there across the Stygian stage.
We asked for funds for fire-resistant bows,
For asbestos gangplanks—all the latest gauge—
But Finch, the Chancellor, refused the cost.
They disembarked from wood. In flames. They're lost."

### V. The Chancellor

Sir Reginald stood up, his eyebrows arched:
"The budget was sufficient for the task!
For any normal campaign, nothing starched—
I funded what was reasonable to ask.
But Hartley at Colonial Office marched
Forty-seven clerks behind each mask,
With filing cabinets, tea-sets, rubber stamps—
All melted now in subterranean camps."

### VI. The Colonial Secretary

Lord Hartley rose, affronted and austere:
"The civil service would have done its part
Had General Featherstone not shown such fear
Of following instructions from the start!
His orders were sublimely, simply clear:
Establish a perimeter with art,
Accept the devil's swift capitulation—
Instead he stopped for tea. Mid-conflagration."

### VII. The General's Dispatch

A clerk stood up to read the General's last,
Retrieved from charred remains beside the flag:
"The men showed spirit, though the flames came fast,
Their discipline I could not help but brag.
The fault lay with the troops—their courage passed—
They tended, when on fire, to scream and drag
Themselves about in quite unseemly ways.
Request reinforcements. Signed: Featherstone. P.S. Send bays."

### VIII. The Resolution

The House relaxed. The dead could not object.
They voted to censure Featherstone's remains.
His widow would receive no pension check,
His portrait would be stricken from the frames.
The matter closed with dignity erect—
When suddenly a footman brought in chains
A note: "Her Majesty enquires of you:
When shall the second expedition's crew?"
