# Saltbush Bill's Merino

'Twas Saltbush Bill, with his travelling sheep, was moving them south by east,
When he camped at a run that was owned by a most peculiar beast—
Not a beast in the literal sense, of course, but a man of obsessive mind,
Called Doddery Davis of Doddery Downs, the worst of his tedious kind.

'Tis strange how often the men out back will take to some curious craft,
Some ruling passion to keep their thoughts away from the overdraft;
And Davis was mad for the merino breed, for the bloodline pure and true,
And he'd talk of fleece and of Spanish stock till your face was turning blue.

"The merino," he'd say, "is the noblest sheep that was ever bred in Spain,
And a man could lose his head for the crime of smuggling one out again!"
(A bloke from Doobendoo once swore his grandfather's cousin knew
A man beheaded in Seville for the crime of smuggling out a ewe;
But I've searched the records thoroughly, and I cannot find the case,
And anyway the Spanish used the garrotte—not the ace,
I mean the axe—you see my point; the facts are getting hazy,
And that's the way with Doobendoo; the locals there are crazy.)

Now Bill had passed through a month before and had seen old Davis's pride:
The champion ram called Doddery Prince, with a fleece two inches wide—
I should say two FEET, of course, not inches—that's a slip;
I'm prone to errors when I write; they sometimes make me trip.
The ram could barely stand at all; he wobbled in the heat,
And Bill had thought, "There's a wager here that's waiting to be beat."

So Bill he baited his trap with care and he said to Davis straight:
"I've heard you breed the finest sheep this side of the Doubloon Gate,
But a drover down in Doobendoo—a man I thought a liar—
Said your full-blood champions couldn't stand a shearer's fire.
He said they'd keel like salted slugs the moment they were shorn—
I told him that was nonsense, that no such sheep were born!"

(Now whether Bill believed this drover, or invented him from scratch,
Is a question I can't answer, and it doesn't matter much;
The point is that old Davis rose like a bream upon the hook,
And swallowed down the tale entire with a most indignant look.)

"A lie!" he roared, "A slanderous lie! My sheep can stand the test!
I'll bet you a tenner," Davis cried, "my ram against your best—
We'll shear them both and race them too, and prove who's got the heart!"
And this was just what Saltbush Bill had schemed for from the start.

They brought the ram—a creature huge, with fleece so deep and wide
The wool grew down across his eyes, his legs were lost inside.
(I'm told he once walked into a fence, but that's got nowt to do
With anything—I mention it because it fills a line or two.)
"His sire was Doddery King the Fourth, his dam was Doddery Queen!"
(I'd say they could have tried a bit; the pattern's not too keen.)

Bill fetched a ewe so plain and lean she barely caught the eye,
A crossbred thing with a Roman nose and a somewhat weary sigh.
(I should clarify: the ewe wasn't Bill's in the strictest sense—
She'd wandered from a neighbouring run and jumped the boundary fence;
But a sheep in a drover's mob, I'm told, is a drover's sheep by right,
At least that's what Bill told me once, and I didn't care to fight.)

They called a shearer—one called Mick—who shore the great ram first:
Eighteen pounds of wool came off! Old Davis nearly burst
With pride to see the massive fleece piled high upon the floor—
"Now beat that with your mongrel ewe! I'll bet she's barely four!"
Mick shore the ewe: six pounds she gave, and Davis laughed aloud:
"Eighteen to six! My Prince has won before the race!" he crowed.
But Bill just smiled and said, "The bet was shearing AND the race,
So set them at the quarter-mile, and let them set the pace."

They lined them up, the naked ram and the crossbred, lean and shorn,
And Davis shouted, "Go!"—but Prince just stood there, looking worn;
He wobbled once, he wobbled twice, then sat down in the dust,
While Bill's crossbred went trotting off as though she simply must
Get back to her companions in the mob beside the gate—
She finished in a minute flat; Prince hadn't left the plate.

"My sheep has won," said Saltbush Bill, "the terms were what you said:
We'd shear them both and race them too—your Prince can't leave his bed.
I'll take that tenner, if you please, and ride on out of here,
And give your man from Doobendoo my compliments sincere."
Old Davis paid without a word; his face was grim and red,
And not another syllable on racing bets he said.

The passing drover through those parts who stops at Davis's run
Will hear a lecture on the fleece before the day is done;
But if you ask about the ram, the famous Doddery Prince,
Old Davis turns a shade of red and hasn't spoken since
Of pedigrees or racing bets or bloodlines pure and true—
He just says, "Get a sheep that lives," and pours another brew.
