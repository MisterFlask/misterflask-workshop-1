# The Mile That Would Not End

A runner set out on a day
As fair as days can be,
To run a single simple mile
And then come home for tea.

The first two hundred yards went well
(As first two hundred may),
But then the road, which should have turned,
Continued straight away.

The elm he'd passed a moment back
Was now an elm again,
And then a second time an elm—
The same elm, that was plain.

His left knee made a formal plea,
His right knee filed a brief,
His lungs declared a wholesale strike,
And both shins howled with grief.

"Just to that tree," the runner gasped,
"Just to that leaning fence—"
But trees and fences would not stay
And offered no defence.

He passed a sign that read "HALFWAY"
And took a ragged breath,
Then passed one reading "STILL HALFWAY"
And one that just read "DEATH."

"Why do I run?" he asked the road.
The road did not reply.
"Why does ANYONE run?" he wailed.
The road just let him by.

A sofa shimmered in the road
And whispered "Sit on me"—
He lunged, and caught a squat hydrant,
Weeping, by the knee.

His watch insisted, cool and small,
Two minutes had gone by.
"Impossible!" the runner cried.
"It's been at LEAST July."

He gritted what was left of him
And surged—if surge he could—
In form less like a man who runs
Than a plank of wood.

And then he stopped. The mile was done.
(Or had the mile stopped him?)
He stood before his own front gate,
Destroyed in every limb.

He told the road, "I'll see you soon."
The road expressed no view.
The road had heard this all before.
The road already knew.
