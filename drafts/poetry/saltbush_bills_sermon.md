# Saltbush Bill's Sermon

'Twas Saltbush Bill, with his travelling sheep, came down by the One Tree Flat;
He watered them slow at the sandy bend, and he stopped to have a chat.
A small bush chapel stood by the road, all weathered and worn of face,
Where sinners gathered for Sunday prayers and the hope of saving grace.

A preacher came to the One Tree Flat, a man of fearsome name,
Who spoke of hellfire and brimstone hot until the sinners came.
He preached so loud that the bush fowls fled, and the stockmen held their breath,
For he painted sin with a lurid brush and he prophesied their death.

'Tis strange how the wandering preachers come to ply their saving trade,
Where the regular clergy fear to tread, and the bishop never strayed.
They find good souls in the dusty west, all wanting a word from God,
And the wandering preacher reaps his fee from the frightened and the awed.

This preacher's trick was to call them out, each sinner by name and deed;
He'd shame them there in the public eye and he'd make their spirits bleed.
They had to stand and confess their sins, or be branded a godless soul,
And the local folk all trembled so, for the preacher kept the roll.

Now Saltbush Bill stepped in one night, to see what it was about;
He sat up the back on a packing crate, a stranger and the odd man out.
The preacher spotted him sitting there, and he pointed a bony hand:
"You there in the shadows, sinner man, rise up and confess where you stand!"

The congregation turned as one, to see what Bill would say;
They reckoned he would stammer out, or bolt and run away.
But Saltbush Bill just grinned and stood, and he stretched out good and slow,
Said he: "Well Reverend, fair enough, I've sins that you should know."

"I sailed the China seas," he said, "and I plundered many a ship;
I sank a dozen merchantmen, and I gave them all the slip.
I robbed an English admiral, and I stole a Spanish queen,
And the crimes I worked upon the waves were the worst you've ever seen."

"I poisoned me a rajah once, and I pinched his ruby throne;
I strangled seven mystic monks and I ground their sacred bone.
I stole the jewels of the British Crown and I hawked them in Bombay,
And I lit a fire in the Tower of London just to pass the day."

Now a man may boast of the things he's done, and he'll likely tell it true,
For a drover's word is his sacred bond, and he'll keep it through and through.
But confession time is a different game, when the parson's on the prod,
And a clever man can admit to much, without offending God.

"I sold my soul to the Devil twice," said Bill with a knowing wink;
"Once down in Hell and once on Earth before you could even blink.
I raised the dead in a Cairo tomb, I cursed a holy shrine,
And I drank the blood of a vampire bat and I mixed it with altar wine."

A snicker ran through the wooden pews, for they caught the drover's game;
No bushman born had done such deeds, or owned to half the same.
The laughter spread like a bushfire lit, it roared from row to row,
And the preacher stood there red as brick while his power began to go.

He slammed his Bible on the rail, he stormed out through the door;
He cursed the lot of them to Hell, and swore he'd preach no more.
But Saltbush Bill just scratched his head, surprised at all the fuss:
Said he: "I only told the truth, so what's the bother thus?"

So Saltbush Bill moved on next day, with his travelling mob in tow;
The locals bought him drinks for weeks, for striking such a blow.
And the chapel stands there still today, but no preacher comes to call,
For they say that Saltbush Bill's confessed the greatest sins of all.
