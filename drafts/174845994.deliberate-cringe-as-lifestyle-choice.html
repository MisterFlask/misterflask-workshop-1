I Tell This Story On Dates

Also to my dance friends. Sometimes to strangers at parties. The point is: I’ve developed this practice, where when I notice something I could say that makes me feel that specific twinge of “ooooh this makes us look silly,” I make a point of saying it.

Because there’s this feeling you get when you’re about to say something that’s potentially status-lowering. Not exactly shame, it’s not exactly fear. The cringe-detector apparatus.

I’ve started treating this feeling as a diagnostic tool. Like, oh cool, my brain just detected a gap between what I’m “supposed” to say and what my genuine impulses are. That’s fun! What if we decided to go with the genuine impulses?

Turns out this twinge is an incredibly reliable sensor for impression management opportunities. It’s like having a built-in detector for where your authentic self diverges from your managed self. Once you notice it, you can’t un-notice it.

Things That Trigger The Twinge: A Partial List

  • Telling my dance studio friends I that I did a whole-ass dating course (“The Attractive Man”) to chill the fuck out around girls I’m interested in, which involved hitting on random girls in nightclubs. At one point, I did this in Mexico; I have a truly marvelous (and surprisingly wholesome!) story about some accidental interactions with a Mexican sex worker that these margins are too narrow to contain.

  • Mentioning my SlutCon talk to dates

  • Showing my dating profile to my dance partner who immediately roasts me for e.g. my video of taser-knife-fighting (“YOU NEED TO BE MORE NORMAL ON YOUR PROFILE” “False! I get many matches!” “BUT YOU COULD HAVE MORE IF YOU WERE MORE NORMAL”)

  • Writing blog posts about, uh, (gestures wildly)

  • The sextortion story (every. single. time.)

Notice the pattern? These are all a little bit status-risky but (here’s the key) non-depressing. I’m not trauma-dumping about my childhood or monopolizing the conversation with my existential dread. I’m sharing specific, entertaining stories that happen to violate norms around what you’re supposed to admit.

The distinction I’m attempting to draw with oversharing

Oversharing is problematic when you use other people as involuntary therapists. You dump your trauma, they feel obligated to comfort you, everyone’s trapped in an emotional labor transaction nobody signed up for.

What I’m doing is different. The sextortion story takes two minutes and ends with everyone laughing (or at least me laughing). It’s vulnerable information as entertainment, not vulnerable information as cry for help.

The key distinction:

  • Oversharing: Status-risky + depressing = emotional hostage situation

  • Normal conversation: Status-safe + entertaining = boring but fine

  • This practice: Status-risky + entertaining = actual connection

The Nature Of Embarrassment

Embarrassment isn’t a fact about situations, it is just the collection of internal experiences you’re having about those situations.

Take the sextortion thing: objectively, I sent dick pics to a scammer. That’s just what happened. But I get to choose whether that’s mortifying or hilarious at any given moment; it’s like one of those pictures where if you look at it one way it’s a duck and the other way it’s a rabbit. And fundamentally, hilarious is more fun.

The facts remain constant; the emotional valence is optional.

What Actually Happens When I Do This

People lean in. They’re curious. They’re engaged. Nobody has ever seemed uncomfortable.

What they DO, almost invariably, is (1) make fun of me and then (2) immediately offer opsec advice. Every single person wants to explain how to avoid getting sextortion scammed in the future. They will do this even if I lead with specifically telling them not to give me opsec advice because I’ve heard it all before. Every last one of them will, as if by magic, materialize a deep professional well of expertise around operational security and scam prevention while giving me such delightfully original takes as “never send a dick pic with your face in it”. At this point, I just accept it as an inevitable part of the conversation and deflect as soon as is feasible.

But in any case, I’m, like, eighty percent sure this practice brings me closer to people. And it’s more fun! Normal social interaction operates within these narrow bands of acceptable disclosure, and when you violate those bands (upward into entertaining territory, not downward into depression), you create novelty. The conversation immediately gets… well, not always better. But interestingly different.

The Therapeutic Angle Nobody Asked For

This is actually the true form of exposure and response prevention (ERP) for social anxiety. See, normal ERP for social anxiety is broken because:

  1. The feared outcome (”they think I’m weird”) is unfalsifiable

  2. You can’t effectively prevent safety behaviors that are mostly mental

But when you deliberately say something status-risky and just lean into it, you CAN’T simultaneously manage impressions upward. It’s structurally impossible. The blessed, glorious freedom of social self-immolation can be yours for the low, low price of one (1) mortifying truth.

The cringe impulse persists; it still fires every time I tell these stories. But that’s good! It means the sensor keeps working. I’m not becoming totally shameless (yet; growth mindset) or losing social awareness.
