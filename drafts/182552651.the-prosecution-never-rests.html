You know the type.

Maybe you are the type.

Someone whose social media is peppered with casual declarations of their own fundamental unlovability. “I think I make a great first impression! It’s just that that’s all I have, and after that, I’m basically useless.” “I can’t hide my ineffable aversiveness irl.”

The naive interpretation: this person just needs reassurance. The cycle goes: this person feels bad → seeks reassurance → receives reassurance → feels better.

But this isn’t what happens. Obviously.

What happens is: this person feels bad → seeks reassurance → receives reassurance → feels briefly better → feels bad again in a very slightly different way within 48 hours → repeat.

If this were simple uncertainty about a fact, external evidence would resolve it. But it clearly doesn’t, not for more than a trivial period.

This is an absurdly common pattern. Why? What exactly is happening, here?

You Are Not A Detective

The reassurance-seeking is a side effect, rather than the primary process. The angle-generation is the primary process.

The mind of someone caught in this loop is actively manufacturing frames through which to view themselves as deficient. “Ineffable aversiveness.” “Too boring to connect with.” “Only good at first impressions.” Each manufactured frame arrives feeling like discovered evidence rather than what it actually is: prosecution material manufactured by an internal lawyer with infinite budget and a weak opposing counsel.

The specific angles are highly dependent on what the person’s deep-set fear about themselves is. These are what I mostly see others compulsively generate:

  • Angles from which they might, possibly, have hurt someone else

  • Angles from which they might have alienated someone

  • Angles from which one might think they are weird or cringe or unloveable

The reassurance-seeking is totally secondary. It’s what you do after an angle has been generated in order to handle said angle. You think you’re being epistemically responsible! You’re not just believing your own negative self-assessment, you’re checking it against external reality!

How admirably scientific of you.

But here’s the problem: the angle-generation has no natural stopping point. Sure, you’ve proven yourself innocent on nine charges, but why ought that help you against charge #10?

The feeling of anxiety is the feeling of evaluating yourself against a stream of invented criminal charges levied by yourself, against yourself. The reason reassurance isn’t particularly useful is… well, that deserves its own section.

Why The Reassurance Doesn’t Help And Never Will

Watch this exchange:

Person A: “Aversiveness?? Girl you were one of the sweetest people I met, I have no idea what you’re talking about”

Person B: “I think I make a great first impression! It’s just that that’s all I have.”

See the move? The reassurance got incorporated into the prosecution’s case. “You found me sweet” becomes “I fooled you temporarily, which is how I fool everyone, before the real me becomes visible.”

This, writ large, is how the system works. Any piece of positive evidence can be reframed as evidence that the deception is working. The only evidence that would count is confirmation of the negative belief—which, notably, rarely arrives, because the belief is usually false. But the absence of confirming evidence doesn’t register as disconfirming evidence, because maybe people are just being nice, or they haven’t seen The Thing yet, or they’re in too deep to notice.

Even When The Reassurance Lands It STILL Doesn’t Matter

Even if every single court case lands in an “innocent” verdict, you’re still spending all your time in court. Being in court (in this sense) is the experience of anxiety; the innocent verdict doesn’t mean you didn’t experience the anxiety, it just means you get a temporary, brief reprieve before the internal prosecutor generates more ammunition. Like the brief moment of sublime pleasure generated by scratching a mosquito bite before the itch comes back, worse than before.

The game is rigged. The only winning move is to notice you’re playing and then— crucially— to stop.

Why The Felt Necessity

It feels like due diligence. Like good, prosocial self-awareness.

I used to do a version of this. Not publicly—I wasn’t posting about it or asking friends about it, mostly because it felt cringe—but internally I was running a continuous audit of How Am I Being Perceived, and it felt important. Like I’d be negligent if I stopped. Like there was a real question, “did I act weird in that social encounter?”, that deserves a real answer, and abandoning the investigation would be irresponsible. Letting myself off the hook.

Because “did I act weird in that social encounter” was fundamentally just a proxy question for the real thing I wanted to know: “am I fundamentally okay as a person?”

Thing is, “am I fundamentally okay as a person” isn’t actually a question. It’s not the kind of thing that has an answer in the way “is it raining out” has an answer. But it feels like a raining-type question. So it’s very tempting to keep checking, and to keep finding new proxy questions that I can slot in to help with finding an answer to the super-question: “am I fundamentally okay?”

That checking behavior is the anxiety. It’s not about the content of any particular sub-question, but rather the process of manufacturing new sub-questions and finding ways to check them. Each cycle of “generate angle from which I suck → feel bad → seek reassurance that I don’t suck → feel briefly better” is just training you to generate more angles. The temporary relief is reinforcing the overall habit that’s causing the suffering.

That’s OCD, baby!

A Plausibly Helpful Reframe

You are not a detective uncovering hard truths about yourself. You are a prosecutor with infinite budget and—crucially—no judge. There’s no verdict coming. There’s no court. There will, in all likelihood, never be a court conducting the trial of “Is Aaron Fundamentally Okay As A Human Being”.

There’s just two lawyers, arguing heatedly in front of an empty jury bench, forever. The court case will never be complete because completion isn’t what it’s for. It’s a process that exists to perpetuate itself.

It’s fundamentally not useful mental behavior and the first step to knocking it off is recognizing this fact.

Regrettably this is not also the last step but frankly a lot of people don’t even get here so I’ll take what I can get.

The Difference Between Status Questions And Action Questions

An action question is something like: “Should I apologize? Should I act differently toward my partner? Should I conduct myself differently at parties?”

These have answers you can arrive at. And crucially, once you get the answer and do the thing, you’re done. “Should I apologize?” terminates in either apologizing or concluding you don’t need to. Either way, the question exhausts itself. There’s no infinite regress.

A status question is something like: “Am I a good person? Am I lovable? Am I fundamentally okay?”

These sometimes also have answers, in some vague, abstract sense. You can tell what they’re trying to get at. But the answer doesn’t terminate in anything. Suppose you got a definitive verdict—guilty or innocent, lovable or unlovable, okay or not okay. What would you do differently tomorrow?

Not a god-damn thing. You’d still try to be decent, still try to repair harms you’ve caused, still try to connect with people. The verdict is totally ephemeral. It doesn’t touch the things you actually do.

Which is why the loop never ends. You’re trying to answer a question that doesn’t want an answer. It wants a verdict, and the verdicts don’t do anything. Verdicts are also much more stressful.

The Way Out

Stop asking status questions. Seriously. Not “try to answer them correctly,” not “get enough reassurance to feel okay about them.” Stop asking them.

When you notice yourself asking “am I okay as a person,” find the action question hiding underneath.

Usually there is one. “Am I a bad friend?” might cash out to “I haven’t texted Sarah back in three weeks and I feel guilty.” Great. Text Sarah or decide you’re not going to. Done. The status question was a parasite on top of the action question, making it feel bigger and more existential than it needed to be while also neutering it of all actionability.

Sometimes there’s no action question underneath! “Am I somehow ineffably aversive” is floating free, unattached to any specific thing you did or should do. In that case: notice that you’re asking a question that doesn’t want an answer, and see if you can just... drop it.

There’s nothing to do. Which means there’s nothing to figure out.

Maybe there was never anything to figure out.

The Boring Conclusion

A significant chunk of what we call “insecurity” or “anxiety” or “overthinking” is probably subclinical OCD that doesn’t get recognized because the content seems reasonable. “I’m constantly convinced I hit someone with my car” is both unusual and life-disruptive. “I’m constantly convinced I just fundamentally suck” is life-disruptive but also extremely normal, so we’re just like “people gonna low-self-esteem, am I right?”

This matters because we have evidence-based treatments for OCD—better treatments than we have for “being insecure.” And people aren’t accessing them because they don’t realize that’s what they have.
