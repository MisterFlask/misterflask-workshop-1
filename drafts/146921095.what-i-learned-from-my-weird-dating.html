The Problem

I avoid pretty girls.

It’s not a choice I made at any point, really, and I certainly don’t endorse this behavior on reflection. But when I’m in the presence of someone I’m attracted to I can feel my personality contract inwards; I shut down and start aggressively self-monitoring, which makes the things I say— even just, like, in regular conversation— feel horribly stilted and awkward.

I find this an embarrassing admission to make, frankly; I’m a grown-ass man at thirty-six years old and this is some high school-level bullshit. (It is probably not a surprising revelation that I also had this problem in high school.)

As to why I want to fix this: I’m single and frankly this seems like a pretty necessary step to becoming not-that.

A… Solution?

So a few months ago I was looking at this aspect of my life and decided to do the obvious thing: get coaching! Since this is clearly a very common problem for dudes which the broader American culture doesn’t really have the language to talk about or address, beyond the hackneyed “beeeee yourself” evangelized by Disney movies. So yeah. Coaching seems like obviously the way to go.

Which brought me immediately to a deeply awkward wrinkle in American culture, where “dating coach” tends to be rounded off to “pickup artist”, and the pickup artist community is a weird mixed bag containing:

  1. genuine insights into the nature of attraction and the practical application of same from the perspective of a heterosexual man, and

  2. misogyny, dishonesty, and an intensely extractive view of sexual relationships. Which I dislike.

Happily, in the Year Of Our Lord 2024 there are quite a few dating coaches out there that seem like they have very healthy views about women and dating and sexuality. And I’m now sampling them! Just did a bootcamp from The Attractive Man, actually, which was a delightful experience overall and also fascinating.

The approach this group advocates has all the grace and subtlety of a brick through a window: you go up to a girl. You tell her that she’s extremely pretty (or beautiful, or cute, or stunning or whatever). You gauge her response, and assuming she’s at least moderately into it, you have a brief conversation that probably ends in asking for her number, whereupon either she gives it to you or is like ‘nah’.

There’s more to it, of course: the conversation should generally include both a bit where you’re getting interesting information about her, and where she’s getting interesting information about you: a very slightly meaningful connection of some kind!

(This is distinct from pickup artistry in that PUAs historically have done a lot of seedy bullshit like putting her down in the hopes of manipulating her into romantic interest. That is not what this is; it is overwhelmingly more direct and upfront, with the point of the compliment being to both convey attraction and also to orient her about what genre of conversation you’ve initiated.)

Anyway, the bootcamp was basically just social scaffolding for practicing this style of approach in two sorts of environment: a busy shopping district and a nightclub.

The bootcamp

The bootcamp itself was three days— really, two and a half— and like half the time was spent approaching random women in public, stopping them, and (with gravitas!) delivering a sincere physical compliment. And I’m aware this probably doesn’t sound like much if you’ve never tried it, but it’s agonizing for me (and I understand for most guys) to do because it feels like I’m forcibly inserting myself into this girl’s day. She didn’t ask me to be there! She didn’t ask for any of this! AND YET HERE I AM. TOO BAD, RANDOM GIRL, YOU WILL NOW BE DELIVERED A SHALLOW BUT RESPECTFUL APPRECIATION OF YOUR PHYSICAL BEAUTY oh god i’m so sorry.

Truthfully, in my gut, I expected far worse responses than I got from this. The breakdown (across maybe about about 15 or 20 unsolicited compliments?) was:

  • 20%: not into it. Generally this is expressed by a wary expression and a quickening of footsteps in daytime; at clubs this is very rare.

  • 30% : Neutral; a sincere but perfunctory thank you with little emotional affect one way or another. (This is more like 50% at the clubs; I think the negative reaction is because of the gut level “this guy wants to extract money from me” that anyone is going to have being approached in a public place.)

  • 30% Sincere appreciation; she seems genuinely happy to have been stopped.

  • 20% I clearly have made this girl’s day; it’s like she’s been dying of thirst and I popped up out of nowhere to bring her a glass of water.

I have a friend of mine who has expressed concern to me that this kind of thing makes the world a worse place, on net. But from what I’ve observed so far, I don’t think that’s true. I think that the negative-responders forget it three minutes later; the 20% who were extreme-positive-responders, though, act like they will retain and treasure that memory at least for a couple of days. (I think most guys remember very clearly the occasions where a pretty girl said they were hot; from what I’ve heard this is a thing most girls only typically hear in the context of catcalling, which apparently is experienced very differently.) The cost-benefit calculation here is not difficult and it doesn’t feel, to me, like a near thing1. (I also take comfort in the fact that the coach was a woman, and is unlikely to ask me to do something that would be scary or alienating to the girls involved.)

There was also a nightclub portion. One of the more socially… adventurous… things Lynn (the coach) had me do was go up to a girl there, get her attention, and say— very slowly, looking her dead in the eyes— that she is sexy. As. Fuck.

This struck me as very bold. But that had also been true of every single other thing I’d done at the bootcamp, so I did it anyway.

Very positive responses! One girl seemed super pleased (though was physically pulled away by her girlfriend before she could respond further) and one, uh:

It’s remarkably difficult to avoid contorting yourself into unnatural shapes in order to please others. I can feel it when I start to say random stuff for the sake of filling the terrifying void of silence. I can feel it when I start to rattle off scripts that are basically just people-pleasing gas. Learning to not do this is a genuine and oftentimes frustrating struggle! But reconditioning myself to not do this seems to be the gist of a lot of what the bootcamp was attempting to help with, especially during the model work.

Which brings me to the second portion of the bootcamp: the model work! “Model work” is a term of industry in dating bootcamps: they hire some pretty girls to come in for a few hours. Then, you do a number of weird exercises with them, mostly revolving around authenticity (in the sense described above). For instance:

“Soulgazing”

This is where you look at a model in the eyes and attempt to reveal your genuine emotional state. You do not move. You do not speak. You simply stare at her left eye. For about fifteen minutes.

I was so bad at this. The model kept snapping her fingers in front of me. “Stop trying. I can see you’re trying. Stop it. God, I feel like a scientist experimenting with a rodent2.”

man idk

Simulated Approach

This is where you go up and hit on the model, and after a few minutes she tells you how you did. Similarly difficult! The running theme is authenticity: if you’re saying shit because you want them to like you, or to keep the conversation rolling without genuine interest in the subject matter, the models notice and they hate it. A lot of the challenge is forcing yourself out of the habitual people-pleasing mode and into the mode where you’re saying stuff because you want to say it.

The Judgment of Osiris

At the end, the models all collectively weigh your soul against the weight of a feather and then tell you if you are going to Heaven or Hell give you, personally, their honest feedback about the experience.

FAQs

What’d you actually learn?

In the main, girls respond to polite expressions of attraction mostly quite positively! These don’t always convert into numbers/hot makeouts (see above anecdote about the girl and her boyfriend) but it doesn’t have to for the experience to have been fun for all involved.

Isn’t this cringe?

Probably!3

Wait, isn’t it unrealistic to expect that some rando you hit on walking around the mall or whatever will actually be a good match?

Yes! But dating is a numbers game. If you date enough girls you will eventually click with one.

And if a year goes by and I am unsuccessful with this approach: well, the time will pass anyway, and it’s certainly not the only thing I will be doing with my year.

In any case, I have always been quite socially anxious, and this is basically just exposure therapy for the same.

Wait, don’t your ex-coworkers read this blog? This all seems rather unprofessional.

Maybe! I did send the URL to a bunch of them before I left Microsoft. Sup guys!

I think that people hide too much of themselves as a modern habit and I would like to not do that. People who I admire on the internet are the people who don’t contort themselves at all to please the nameless, faceless Other. I would like to be one of them. Possibly this will cost me job opportunities! Someone will be like “hey, wait, isn’t that resume from the dude who wrote a cringe blog post about attending a dating bootcamp” and another will be like “oh holy shit you’re right, NEXT” and then I’ll lose out on the position of a lifetime.

Seems unlikely though.

Wait, I thought you were socially anxious even in non-romantic contexts!

I am indeed! So this bootcamp was done with a DIY drug assist: 750 mg of phenibut morning and evening for two days (so, 1.5 g per day).

Phenibut is a very interesting anti-anxiety drug that in the US is legally considered an unregulated supplement. But it should definitely be treated with all the care and gravitas of an actual drug. It suppresses social inhibitions in a very similar way to how alcohol does without of a lot of the side-effects of alcohol, but it also creates tolerance extremely quickly so traditional advice is to give a couple weeks between doses: that way, you don’t become addicted and then have a hellish comedown when you go off of it. (Apparently, it’s very common for people breaking a phenibut addiction to develop strong convictions that they’re about to get fired and also have random panic attacks for several days during the comedown.)

That said, I could tell this was extremely effective for me being able to leave my comfort zone and do the kinds of things that historically I haven’t done before. Feels like a useful drug to have in my toolbelt for this project, even if I can’t use it all that often.

What’s the endgame here?

That I develop comfort around expressing attraction and initiating conversations with people I don’t know. I think this is a deeply important life skill.

“Cringe”, as a concept, is very very modern: it literally means “the thing you are doing would make some neutral onlooker cringe in sympathetic embarrassment at your behavior”. It comes from the web: on Twitter, you act as though everyone’s watching you because at any moment everyone might start watching you. This breeds paranoia.

But the concept of “cringe” at its core represents the opposite philosophy from authenticity. “Cringe” suggests that you ought to contort yourself into shapes you think will please nameless, faceless others; “cringe” says you ought to try and cut off the parts of yourself that other people might disapprove of, or at least hide them so nobody knows your shame.

I think it’s a toxic philosophy and to be honest I think less of people who use “cringe” as a term of abuse toward others.

But re the dating bootcamps specifically, they’re always going to be cringe because

  • Intrinsically they are interested in the differences between how men and women experience attraction, and we… don’t like to talk about that, culturally. At least not in liberal areas (I don’t really know about conservative ones).

  • Going to a dating bootcamp implies you aren’t already romantically successful, and that’s embarrassing! What are you, some kind of loser?

Notice that this is entirely consistent with the bootcamps being both good and useful. I suspect that is why there’s so little writing about them online.
