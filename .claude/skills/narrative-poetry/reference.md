# Narrative Poetry Reference

Detailed prosody reference for the narrative-poetry skill.

## Stress Notation

The CMU Pronouncing Dictionary uses:
- **0** = unstressed (schwa, reduced vowels)
- **1** = primary stress
- **2** = secondary stress

For most metrical purposes, treat 2 as flexible—it can function as stressed or unstressed depending on context.

## Forms in Detail

### Ballad Meter (Common Meter)

Alternating tetrameter and trimeter lines: 8/6/8/6 syllables.
Rhyme scheme typically ABAB or ABCB.

```
The king sits in Dunfermline town,    (8)
    Drinking the blood-red wine;       (6)
"O where will I get a skeely skipper   (8)
    To sail this ship of mine?"        (6)
```

**Long meter** variant: 8/8/8/8 (all tetrameter).
**Short meter** variant: 6/6/8/6.

### Blank Verse

Unrhymed iambic pentameter. The workhorse of English dramatic and narrative poetry.

```
Of Man's first disobedience, and the fruit
Of that forbidden tree whose mortal taste
Brought death into the World, and all our woe
```

Allows significant metrical variation:
- **Feminine endings** (extra unstressed syllable)
- **Initial trochaic substitution** (stressed first syllable)
- **Pyrrhic + spondee** combinations
- **Caesura** (mid-line pause)

### Heroic Couplet

Rhymed iambic pentameter in pairs. Dominant form of 18th-century poetry.

```
True wit is nature to advantage dressed,
What oft was thought, but ne'er so well expressed.
```

**Closed couplets**: Each couplet is a complete syntactic unit.
**Open/enjambed couplets**: Syntax flows across couplet boundaries.

### Ottava Rima

Eight-line stanzas in iambic pentameter, rhyming ABABABCC.
The final couplet often delivers a punchline or turn.

Byron's *Don Juan* is the canonical English example:

```
I want a hero: an uncommon want,
When every year and month sends forth a new one,
Till, after cloying the gazettes with cant,
The age discovers he is not the true one;
Of such as these I should not care to vaunt,
I'll therefore take our ancient friend Don Juan—
We all have seen him, in the pantomime,
Sent to the devil somewhat ere his time.
```

### Rhyme Royal

Seven-line stanzas in iambic pentameter, rhyming ABABBCC.
Used by Chaucer (*Troilus and Criseyde*) and later poets.

### Spenserian Stanza

Nine lines: eight iambic pentameter + one alexandrine (hexameter).
Rhyme scheme ABABBCBCC.

The interlocking rhymes create forward momentum; the closing alexandrine provides weight.

## Form Selection Guide

**Critical:** Form carries emotional and tonal information independent of content. A mismatch between form and content creates friction—the meter fights the meaning. Select form *before* drafting based on the poem's intended effect.

### Meter Vibes

#### **Iambic** (da-DUM)
The "default" English meter—close to natural speech but elevated.

**Iambic Pentameter** (5 feet, 10 syllables)
- **Vibe:** Serious, weighty, authoritative, literary, declamatory
- **Associations:** Shakespeare, Milton, formal verse, tragedy, philosophical meditation
- **Best for:** Serious subjects, dramatic monologues, elevated speech, characters who see themselves as important
- **Avoid for:** Light comedy, energetic narratives, absurdist humor, breathless speakers
- **Example:** "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"

**Iambic Tetrameter** (4 feet, 8 syllables)
- **Vibe:** Lighter than pentameter, songlike, brisk but still dignified
- **Associations:** Marvell, Frost, hymns
- **Best for:** Lyric poetry, moderate-paced narrative, wry observation
- **Example:** "Had we but world enough and time"

#### **Trochaic** (DUM-da)
Falling rhythm—feels incantatory, insistent, slightly unsettling.

**Trochaic Tetrameter** (4 feet, 7-8 syllables)
- **Vibe:** Chanting, magical, relentless, primitive, hypnotic, marching
- **Associations:** "Hiawatha," witches in Macbeth, folk charms, military cadence
- **Best for:** Spells, curses, mythic narratives, things ancient or fated
- **Avoid for:** Casual conversation, modern settings, relaxed tone
- **Example:** "Double, double, toil and trouble"

#### **Anapestic** (da-da-DUM)
Galloping, bouncy, propulsive—the rhythm runs ahead of itself.

**Anapestic Tetrameter** (4 feet, 12 syllables)
- **Vibe:** Energetic, breathless, comedic, headlong, enthusiastic, slightly manic
- **Associations:** Dr. Seuss, "'Twas the Night Before Christmas," Byron's "Destruction of Sennacherib," limericks, comic verse
- **Best for:** Comedy, children's verse, breathless narration, salesmen/hucksters, manic energy, adventure, accumulating absurdity
- **Avoid for:** Solemn reflection, grief, quiet moments, dignity
- **Example:** "'Twas the night before Christmas, when all through the house"

**Anapestic Trimeter** (3 feet, 9 syllables)
- **Vibe:** Limerick energy, punchier, bouncier
- **Best for:** Light verse, short comic pieces

#### **Dactylic** (DUM-da-da)
Rolling, grand, classical—rare in English.

**Dactylic Hexameter**
- **Vibe:** Epic, classical, Homeric, processional
- **Associations:** Classical epic (adapted), Longfellow's "Evangeline"
- **Best for:** Mock-epic, deliberately archaic/classical subjects
- **Avoid for:** Almost everything—very difficult to sustain naturally in English
- **Example:** "This is the forest primeval, the murmuring pines and the hemlocks"

#### **Mixed/Loose Meters**

**Ballad Meter** (alternating 8/6 syllables, iambic)
- **Vibe:** Folk, narrative, singable, accessible, traditional
- **Associations:** Folk ballads, Coleridge, sea shanties
- **Best for:** Storytelling, traditional tales, deaths and tragedies told plainly, supernatural encounters
- **Example:** "There lived a wife at Usher's Well / And a wealthy wife was she"

**Common Meter** (8/6/8/6, ABAB)
- **Vibe:** Hymn-like, devotional, can be ironic when mismatched with content
- **Associations:** Hymns, Emily Dickinson
- **Best for:** Spiritual subjects, ironic juxtaposition, deceptively simple statements
- **Note:** Almost all Dickinson poems can be sung to "Amazing Grace" or Gilligan's Island theme

**Fourteeners / Long Ballad Meter** (14+ syllables per line, AABB)
- **Vibe:** Rollicking, yarn-spinning, pub-song, campfire tale, expansive
- **Associations:** Banjo Paterson, bush ballads, Kipling's barrack-room ballads
- **Best for:** Tall tales, comic narratives, working-class speakers, extended anecdotes, building to punchlines
- **Note:** Long lines allow asides, qualifications, rambling buildup—perfect for comic timing
- **Example:** "There was movement at the station, for the word had passed around"

**Patter** (rapid trochaic or mixed, heavily accented)
- **Vibe:** Theatrical, virtuosic, comedic accumulation, showing off
- **Associations:** Gilbert & Sullivan, music hall, comic opera
- **Best for:** Lists of absurdities, self-important characters undercutting themselves, rapid-fire comedy
- **Example:** "I am the very model of a modern Major-General"

### Rhyme Scheme Vibes

#### **Couplets (AA BB CC)**
- **Vibe:** Conclusive, epigrammatic, punchy
- **Effect:** Every two lines deliver a complete thought or payoff
- **Best for:** Wit, satire, aphorisms, comic verse with regular punchlines
- **Risk:** Can feel monotonous if too end-stopped; needs enjambment or variation

#### **Alternate Rhyme (ABAB)**
- **Vibe:** Interlocking, songlike, narrative flow
- **Effect:** Pulls reader forward—the ear waits for resolution
- **Best for:** Ballads, songs, narrative poetry, anything meant to be sung

#### **Enclosed Rhyme (ABBA)**
- **Vibe:** Contemplative, circular, self-contained, elegant
- **Effect:** Stanza feels closed; return to opening sound creates resolution
- **Best for:** Reflective lyrics, contained emotional moments

#### **Monorhyme (AAAA)**
- **Vibe:** Obsessive, hammering, hypnotic, comedic when sustained
- **Effect:** Relentless same-sound becomes incantatory or absurd
- **Best for:** Comic excess, chants, drinking songs, deliberate monotony

#### **Limerick (AABBA)**
- **Vibe:** Bawdy, joke-structured, pub humor
- **Effect:** Setup (AA), pivot (BB), punchline (A)
- **Best for:** Short comic verse, wordplay, dirty jokes

#### **Terza Rima (ABA BCB CDC)**
- **Vibe:** Propulsive, linked, journey-like, serious
- **Associations:** Dante's Divine Comedy, Shelley
- **Best for:** Journeys, philosophical progressions, sustained serious narrative
- **Avoid for:** Comedy (too interlocked and weighty)

### Matching Form to Content

#### Comedy Types

| Comedy Type | Recommended Meter | Recommended Rhyme | Why |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|-----|
| Breathless/manic | Anapestic tetrameter | Couplets (AA BB) | Galloping rhythm + regular payoffs |
| Tall tale/yarn | Fourteeners | Couplets (AA BB) | Room for buildup, asides, pub energy |
| Satirical/witty | Iambic tetrameter | Couplets (AA BB) | Brisk enough to be light, formal enough to be pointed |
| Absurdist accumulation | Patter (trochaic) | Couplets or monorhyme | Virtuosic listing, snowballing ridiculousness |
| Dark/ironic | Ballad meter | ABAB or ABCB | Folk earnestness undercut by dark content |
| Bawdy/punchline | Anapestic trimeter | AABBA (limerick) | Classic joke structure |

#### Serious Types

| Serious Type | Recommended Meter | Recommended Rhyme | Why |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|-----|
| Epic/heroic | Iambic pentameter or blank verse | Couplets or none | Weight and dignity |
| Elegiac/grief | Iambic tetrameter or pentameter | ABBA or ABAB | Contemplative, circular |
| Mythic/ancient | Trochaic tetrameter | ABAB or none | Incantatory, fated |
| Lyric/emotional | Iambic tetrameter | ABAB or ABBA | Songlike, personal |
| Narrative/folk | Ballad meter | ABCB | Traditional, accessible |
| Spiritual/hymnal | Common meter (8/6/8/6) | ABAB | Devotional associations |

#### Character Voice

The speaker's personality should influence meter choice:

| Speaker Type | Suggested Meter | Why |
|--------------|-----------------|-----|
| Huckster/salesman | Anapestic | Breathless enthusiasm, won't let you interrupt |
| Sage/authority | Iambic pentameter | Weight, gravitas, expects to be heeded |
| Raconteur/yarn-spinner | Fourteeners | Takes their time, enjoys the telling |
| Witch/oracle | Trochaic | Chanting, outside normal speech |
| Common folk | Ballad meter | Accessible, traditional, unpretentious |
| Pompous official | Patter or iambic pentameter | Self-undermining virtuosity or self-important dignity |
| Child/innocent | Anapestic or simple iambic | Bouncy or sing-song |

### Form/Content Mismatches to Avoid

These combinations create friction where form fights meaning:

| Content | Problematic Form | Why |
|---------|------------------|-----|
| Slapstick/absurd comedy | Iambic pentameter | Too dignified; meter fights the humor |
| Grief/elegy | Anapestic | Too bouncy; feels disrespectful |
| Breathless huckster | Heroic couplets | Pomposity without ironic distance |
| Ancient myth | Limerick | Bathos (unless intentional) |
| Children's verse | Blank verse | No propulsive rhythm, too flat |
| Quick wit/epigrams | Fourteeners | Too sprawling; dilutes the punch |
| Manic energy | Iambic pentameter | Meter too stately; constrains momentum |
| Solemn pronouncement | Anapestic | Galloping rhythm undercuts gravity |

### Form Selection Checklist

Before committing to a form, verify:

1. **Tone match:** Does the meter's inherent vibe match the poem's intended emotional register?
2. **Speaker match:** Does the rhythm suit how this character would speak?
3. **Pacing match:** Does the meter support the narrative's energy level?
4. **Comedy/gravity match:** If funny, is the meter light enough? If serious, is it weighty enough?
5. **No red flags:** Is this combination in the "mismatches to avoid" table?

If any answer is "no," reconsider the form choice before proceeding to generation.

## Metrical Variation

Strict adherence to meter sounds mechanical. Good verse uses controlled variation:

### Acceptable Substitutions

1. **Initial trochee** in iambic line:
   `GATH-er ye | rose-BUDS | while YE | may`

2. **Pyrrhic-spondee pair** (redistributed stress):
   `of the | DARK WOODS`

3. **Feminine ending** (extra unstressed syllable):
   `To BE | or NOT | to BE | that IS | the QUES-tion`

4. **Elision** (synaloepha):
   "the evening" can scan as 2 or 3 syllables depending on speed

### Problematic Patterns

1. **Two consecutive stressed or unstressed syllables** in iambic verse (unless intentional spondee/pyrrhic)

2. **Consistent metrical inversion** mid-line (reads as different meter)

3. **Syllable count off by more than 1** from expected

## Rhyme Types

### Full Rhyme (Perfect Rhyme)
Identical sounds from the stressed vowel onward: light/night, desire/fire

### Slant Rhyme (Near Rhyme)
Close but not identical: love/move, eyes/voice

### Eye Rhyme
Looks like it should rhyme but doesn't: love/prove, wind/mind

### Rich Rhyme (Rime Riche)
Homophones: their/there, night/knight

### Feminine Rhyme
Two-syllable rhyme with stress on first: running/cunning, nation/station

### Dactylic Rhyme
Three-syllable rhyme: merrily/verily, tenderly/slenderly

## The 5000 Character Limit

For reference, approximate lengths:
- Sonnet (14 lines): ~600-800 characters
- Ballad stanza (4 lines): ~150-250 characters
- 20 stanzas of ballad: ~4000-5000 characters

A narrative poem of 16-24 quatrains is a reasonable target. Plan accordingly in Phase 1.

### Phase 3: Assembly
- [ ] All stanzas concatenated
