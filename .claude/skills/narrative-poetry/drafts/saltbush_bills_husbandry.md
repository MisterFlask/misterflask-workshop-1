# Saltbush Bill's Husbandry

'Twas Saltbush Bill, with his travelling sheep, had strayed for a week or more

Through the paddocks wide of Doolan's Run, where he'd never been before;

Or so he said when they found him there, just resting beneath a tree,

While his old scrub ram ran with the ewes as happy as rams can be.

Now "Professor" Doolan owned the Run, and was famous throughout the West

As a scientific breeder of sheep -- he'd studied among the best;

He'd imported rams from the studs of Spain, and pored over charts at night,

And he'd lecture any man who'd listen on Doolan's Methods Done Right.

'Tis strange how often the men out back will seize on some hopeless cause,

Some dream of science or system grand that follows imagined laws;

It keeps their minds from the mortgage due, and the price of wool gone flat,

And Doolan's cause was his bloodline charts -- he was scientific Pat.

He sneered at the mongrel travelling mobs that wandered the stock routes through:

"Unscientific and scrub-bred trash, with never a pedigree true!

My rams are measured and weighed and scored, each mating precisely planned --

No bushman's guesswork will ever match the work of a learned hand!"

Now Saltbush Bill, when he heard this speech, just scratched his unshaven jaw,

And gazed at Doolan's prize merinos like a man who's found a flaw.

"Your ewes look well," said Saltbush Bill, "they're healthy and sleek and sound --

I'd say whoever has served them last has done a job profound."

Old Doolan swelled with pride at this, not catching the drover's game;

"My imported ram, Don Doloroso, a sire of the highest fame!"

But Saltbush smiled and scratched his ear and said, "Well, here's my thought:

I'll wager I can improve your lambs with methods that can't be taught."

"What methods?" Doolan scoffed at him. "Some superstitious rot?

Moon phases? Singing? Old wives' tales? I'll take that bet on the spot!

A hundred pounds says science wins -- your folk-ways are a fraud --

We'll count the lambs come shearing time and let the count applaud."

The wager made, Bill tipped his hat and gathered his mob to go,

And nobody noticed his scrub-bred ram was moving a little slow,

Nor that the ewes in Doolan's yards were looking rather content --

But Saltbush Bill had a knowing smile as off through the gate he went.

The season turned, and the lambs were dropped, and Doolan watched his yield:

His scientific, pedigreed stock were dying across the field.

Too fine of bone, too thin of fleece, they couldn't survive the frost;

For every lamb that Doolan saved, it seemed like three were lost.

But then came word from Doolan's hands of lambs out in the back --

A mob of sturdy, thick-wooled beasts that nothing seemed to crack.

"What sire is this?" cried Doolan then. "These lambs aren't from my strain!"

They'd got their fire from Doolan's ewes, their grit from Bill's campaign.

When Saltbush Bill came back that spring to settle up the bet,

Old Doolan's face was thunderous, the darkest Bill had met.

"Your lambs outstripped my pedigrees! What method did you use?"

And Saltbush Bill leaned close and said, "I'll sell it, if you choose."

"A hundred pounds for the secret, then!" cried Doolan, mad to know;

And Bill took out a battered book and cleared his throat just so:

"You sing to the ewes at the quarter moon, and feed 'em on saltbush tea,

And whisper the name of their future lamb -- it's an old bush mystery."

Old Doolan paid and wrote it down with a scholar's careful hand,

And Saltbush Bill was a mile away before he could understand

That his ewes had met with a different ram while Bill was "lost" out back --

And the secret he'd bought was worth precisely the paper in Doolan's shack.

The passing stranger within his gates who stops at Doolan's Run

Must never speak of breeding sheep, or the damage that's been done;

For the record shows in the local Court that a drover copped a fine

For asking Doolan, in all good faith, if he'd tried the saltbush line.
