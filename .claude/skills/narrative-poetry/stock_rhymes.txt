# Stock Rhymes - Cliched rhyme pairs to avoid or use with caution
#
# Format: category | word1 | word2 | ... | wordN
# Categories:
#   HARD - Almost always groan-inducing, avoid in serious poetry
#   SOFT - Cliched in romantic/sentimental contexts, may be fine elsewhere
#   GENRE - Tired in specific genres but acceptable in satire/parody

# === HARD AVOID ===
# These rhymes are so overused they've become punchlines

HARD | moon | June | spoon | croon | swoon | tune | soon
HARD | love | dove | above | thereof
HARD | heart | apart | part | start | art
# Note: heart/apart specifically in romantic "you stole my heart / we're torn apart" context

# === SOFT WARNINGS ===
# Overused in romantic/sentimental poetry, fine in other contexts

SOFT | fire | desire | higher | liar
SOFT | night | light | bright | sight | flight | right | might | white
SOFT | eyes | skies | lies | cries | dies | rise | wise
SOFT | kiss | bliss | miss | this
SOFT | dreams | seems | streams | gleams | beams
SOFT | tears | fears | years | hears | appears
SOFT | day | way | say | stay | away | play
SOFT | hold | gold | told | old | cold | bold
SOFT | true | you | blue | through | new | knew
SOFT | forever | never | together | weather
SOFT | breath | death
SOFT | rain | pain | again | vain | remain

# === GENRE-SPECIFIC ===
# Tired in their home genre but can work ironically or in other contexts

GENRE | baby | maybe | crazy
# Pop/country music cliche

GENRE | tonight | alright
# Rock/pop cliche

GENRE | soul | control | whole | goal | roll
# R&B/gospel cliche

GENRE | world | girl | unfurled
# Ballad cliche

GENRE | free | me | be | see
# Anthemic rock cliche

GENRE | time | rhyme | climb | crime
# Meta-poetry cliche (especially "time/rhyme")

# === NOTES ===
#
# The presence of a word pair here doesn't mean you can NEVER use it.
# Context matters:
#
# 1. Satire/parody: Stock rhymes can be used deliberately for comic effect
# 2. Genre pastiche: If writing in the style of Tin Pan Alley, moon/June is period-appropriate
# 3. Subversion: Setting up a stock rhyme then breaking it can be effective
# 4. Semantic necessity: Sometimes the stock word IS the right word for the meaning
#
# The goal is to avoid UNTHINKING use of these pairs. If you reach for
# "love/dove" because it's the first rhyme that comes to mind, query the
# rhyming dictionary first. If you choose "love/dove" after seeing all
# options because it genuinely fits best, that's a defensible choice.
