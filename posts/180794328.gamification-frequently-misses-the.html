Slay the Spire is an arithmetic game.

It is not marketed this way. If it were, nobody would play it. But it absolutely is an arithmetic game.

You’re constantly running an inner monologue that sounds like this:

  • “If I play this card for 6 damage, then this one for 8, that’s 14, the enemy has 23 HP and 5 block, so I need to do 28 total...”

  • “This does 3x4 damage, that’s 12, plus the vulnerable modifier of 50%, so that’s 18…”

Every round of combat in Slay the Spire involves, as a run proceeds, escalating amounts of arithmetic as a basic requirement of avoiding death. It is a non-optional feature of gameplay. But nobody thinks of Slay the Spire as a “math” game.

Part of this is, of course, that the Venn diagram of “people who need to learn basic arithmetic“ and “people who can understand deckbuilder mechanics“ is basically just two non-overlapping circles; this would make it wildly inappropriate as a genuine educational product, so marketing it in this way would be clearly insane.

But it is nevertheless the case that grown-ass men will spend countless hours happily performing the same basic activity that children need active coercion to do even a little bit of.

What’s happening here?

The Strategic And Tactical Layers

The combat part of Slay the Spire is pretty simple. There are frequently nontrivial decisions you have to make (most frequently: do I accept chip damage to play this sweet power card), but most turns you’re doing things that are pretty obviously the correct line of play.

The strategic layer is… different. You are trying to make your deck into an engine that reliably protects you and kills whatever you get put in front of, and the question of “did I choose the right card to put in my deck“ is fully non-answerable. The game doesn’t ding you for choosing the “wrong” card; you simply choose, and the rest of the game simply allows the natural consequence of that choices to play themselves out in a brute, mechanical sort of way.

This is true of the tactical layer too. If you get the damage calculation wrong the game doesn’t yell at you “HEY DUMMY YOU FUCKED UP,” it says “you have now taken damage,“ and the natural consequences of this error play themselves out, and you as the player take note that you got the problem wrong. This is what learning feels like from the inside.

We tend not to notice that we’re being called upon to do enormous amounts of tedious arithmetic on each run because even though the arithmetic is functionally necessary it’s also not the point. The point is building a beautiful engine that gets us through the game.

Starting From The Worksheet (or, The Right Answer To The Wrong Question)

Worksheets are the worst way of learning that still kinda works.

Think about it: almost all learning IRL is a byproduct of doing whatever it is you need the information for. You’re constantly noticing that you have to retrieve the same basic nugget of info, and eventually your brain flags it as “relevant, should recall for the future.”

Worksheets are easy for educators to write and grade. This has been a comprehensive summary of the virtues of worksheets. Their flaw is that they are intrinsically boring and hence ineffective, and the REASON they are boring is because no fact is intrinsically interesting. Every fact is interesting solely because of the context the fact exists in, and the way it links to goals you have that you care about; worksheets are effectively attempts to getting someone to memorize information utterly divorced from the context in which that information is useful. Of course worksheets are boring.

Slay the Spire doesn’t make arithmetic interesting. Arithmetic is neither interesting or uninteresting. What’s interesting, always and everywhere, is the context in which it’s found, and Slay the Spire has a lovingly-developed context.

300-day streak and nothing to show for it

Edutainment games— Duolingo is probably the most popular example— work, in a sense. Duolingo is successful at getting people to both form an identity as a “language learner” and to apply this identity to language-themed memorization exercises. Note that “success” hear does not mean “users now know the language;“ the internet is replete with people mourning that their several hundred hours of duolingo doesn’t get them basic conversational proficiency.

Because this illustrates the second failure mode of worksheets: because the information is given a totally sterile and artificial environment to be absorbed in, usage won’t automatically generalize the contexts we actually give a shit about which are IRL conversations. The only way to learn how to have a conversation in a language is… by repeatedly trying and failing to have conversations in that language because you have no other way of achieving your goals. Full immersion, is what they call it.
