So, I’ve had pretty significant recurring social anxiety for about as far back as I’ve been socially aware. I remember feeling it intensely as far back as middle school, which frankly is about where my memories cap out so I think it’s probably just the Default Aaron Configuration.

It applies in really specific scenarios: namely, where I have initiated an in-person conversation with a stranger without social permission to do this. It’s remarkably general! And has been easy enough to go through my adult life without really dealing with this, since our society makes cloistering yourself in your home very very easy.

As to why this anxiety persists: I’ve though a lot about this! I suspect that there’s a feedback loop where your brain trains itself— in the predictive-processing sense— using simulations of things that might happen (see also: dreams), and if the thing your gut deep-down believes is “talking to strangers in public will result in massive social shaming and be a terrible experience for all involved” then your natural response to that will be… to not talk to strangers in public. Which means your sole experience to talking to strangers in public will be based around your brain’s distorted simulations of those experiences, which are universally negative.

I know that these simulations are distorted because (1) I know people that love talking to strangers in public for whom that has dramatically enriched their lives and (2) when I can bring myself to do it, a lot of times *I* have found this extremely rewarding! Wild. But my lizard-brain, deep down, believes these are statistical anomalies and should not be counted.

How to get out of that? Well, it sounds easier than it is: very frequently you must talk to strangers. In public.

The problem that I, and many other people I know, have is that this is terrifying. Like jumping off a bridge. Not for any good reason; my subconscious does not have any specific terrifying outcome it’s envisioning. The fear is more abstract. What if I’m cringe? What if I’m the subject of some unflattering story this person tells later? What if I’m what’s wrong with American men?

Pickup Artist culture

Let’s talk briefly about pickup artists.

So, pickup artist culture is kind of a mixed bag. On the one hand, the entire term of art— “pickup artist”— is kind of centered around the idea of sex and relationships as being transactional and combative: “the girl has the sex. I want the sex. I need to manipulate the girl into giving me the sex.” It’s kind of repellent to most people because most people actually like the gender they want to date! I like girls, and the girls I want to date like guys! This is meant to be mutually beneficial!

On the other hand, this subculture actually provided value because there’s an intense aversion in our culture to talking about the mechanics of attraction in any concrete or technical way. Guys and girls both have ways of conveying “I am attractive” that are pretty unique and distinct and also have no functional value, similar to the Birds Of Paradise mating displays. And these ways are… sort of mechanical and soulless-sounding. For women, it’s mostly based around looking hot (with a small social desirability element.) For men, I’ve heard they consist largely of (through a variety of both explicit and tacit cues) conveying the concept that “I am high-status and having sex with me is an accomplishment.”

So our culture tends to dance around the question of “okay, how do I as a guy become in some objective sense attractive” because the concrete answers are kind of cringe and unflattering to everyone involved.1

I think of it as being fairly analogous to an alternate universe where men’s attractiveness was based on “skill at chess”. In this alternate universe, Disney would have movies where the basic thesis is “if you remember to be yourself, and you meet the right girl, you will suddenly know how to castle.” And on Tumblr you’d see a post with ten thousand re-tumbles: “Men talk about the Queen’s Gambit opening like it’s hard, but maybe they would be better at it if they respected women more.”

Like we desperately want “skill at dating” to have been borne out of some generic goodness-as-a-person, but that’s not actually the world we live in. Charisma is distributed precisely as unfairly as intelligence or wealth. Deal with it.

So over the past couple of decades there’s been this shift where the groups practicing and training these techniques no longer call themselves “pickup artists”, and to be fair this isn’t just branding: they’ve mostly pushed out the misogynistic elements of the culture to the unpopular periphery while still maintaining the usefulness of the rest of it.

Anyway, now they call themselves “dating coaches” or similar.

But the techniques remain similar because the problem remains the same: in an atomized society where people mostly don’t know other people, how do you convey the concept “I am great and high-status, and having sex and/or being in a relationship with me is an accomplishment”?

Your job and net worth aren’t inscribed above your head, and also, wealth isn’t in itself impressive. What’s impressive is status-within-your-community and that’s not something that can be conveyed easily to anonymous randos. But what you can convey is the effects of being high-status:

  • confidence and ease with girls, since there are more where that came from.

  • Being all muscley (though my understanding is that most of male attractiveness to women is behavioral.)

  • Having some kind of prominent and apparent community position.
