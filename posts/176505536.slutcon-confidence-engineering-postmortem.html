Most of my blog is pretty theory-heavy. I had this sort of weird enlightenment-adjacent event a bit less than a year ago; I reverse-engineered it to fix my social/romantic anxiety; and I’ve been systematizing it into a protocol that could in-principle be taught to someone via a lecture or a blog post. I was super excited to be offered a speaking slot at Slutcon; the talk was basically a gussied-up version of my Confidence Engineering post, given in almost the only setting where guys would feel both deep motivation to execute on it (girls!) and where I could semi-effectively follow up to assess how well it went.

I gave a feedback survey, baited with 200 dollars (which I later upped to 600 dollars) and results (incorporating both vocal and survey-based feedback) are far more positive than I expected, by a substantial margin.

Truthfully, I didn’t know if these learnings would transfer to anyone other than myself. I had a lot of theory saying that they should transfer, but this is unknowable until you actually try. But the learnings did transfer. Not always, not for everyone, but for a small handful of guys my Confidence Engineering talk appears to have been a load-bearing element of their Slutcon experience.

This is excellent because it implies the core methodology is ~sound; the baseline probability of a placebo talk about confidence having any particular impact on someone’s behavior is quite low, which implies a nontrivial level of effectiveness for the payload of my specific talk (basically, me trying to incept into the guys a functional mechanism for jump-starting genuine social confidence).

Verbal (8ish guys?)

About 8-10 different guys over the course of Slutcon grabbed me as I was walking around to tell me about how much difference the Confidence Engineering talk made. One guy said it reframed the entire conference for him. One guy suggested to me, in all earnestness, that it “could change someone’s life” which is nuts. One just complimented the fact that I clearly put effort into artistry of presentation (“like five minutes in I realized, oh, he’s doing a bit.”)

I wish I had the presence of mind to get recordings or write down what was said explicitly. Even so!

At this point, Person 1 has gone through a full cycle of belief disconfirmation, which I think might give this a pretty good chance of sticking.

Core Takeaways

It worked. The core concept worked. Not for everyone, but as far as I’m concerned the baseline probability of an arbitrary confidence talk generating the kinds of notes I received above is pretty damn close to zero, so I have something here.

My main problems seem to be (1) conveying the ideas effectively (a couple people seemed confused about the protocols), (2) helping people remember them (possibly I just have too many protocols), and (3) bootstrapping guys up to the place where implementing the protocols is doable at all. I know a lot of guys at Slutcon would totally freeze up pre-interaction and figuring out ways for dealing with that is also part of The Work.

A friend of mine recently brought up the idea that touch-starved guys may be fundamentally uncomfortable with touching someone in general and having someone start off by touching them might be a tremendously useful stepping stone. I think I have created a reasonably useful set of protocols that gets someone from “low-tier functional” to “mid/high-tier functional” with girls, but bootstrapping from “can’t function” to “low-tier” is still something that I’m sure one could devise replicable protocols to do if they tried.

Threat Response Channeling

So the Fully Known guys— Nick and friends— did something I thought was fascinating: when a guy was up in front of the girls and was too stiff, Nick said to them:

“I want you to shove my chest. Really hard, like you’re trying to hurt me.”

And the guy would do it a few times, and then he’d turn back to the girls and continue flirting with them and this resulted in significantly improved performance each time.

I have strong suspicions about what’s happening here (which I’ll be tackling in another blog post shortly) but I think that specifically is another piece to the puzzle that I’ve been missing, where the “puzzle” is “how does one incept confidence into someone who doesn’t feel it already, in a way that sticks around ~forever.” There’s a lot of low-hanging fruit in this domain that doesn’t get covered by CBT+ERP and I want to eat all of it.

Pedagogical improvements

I’m considering pedagogical improvements, such as

Make the audience derive the protocols?

I was telling the guys the protocols, but I suspect retention could have been improved by attempting to make my audience derive the protocols themselves given the problem statement “If we’re looking for a guiding principle for conversation that’s not ‘make this person like me’, what might such a thing be?” or “we need to cull these specific responses. What concrete rules can be used to do so?”

Solicit suggestions from the audience. Engage with their ideas, discuss pros and cons before i show them my own stuff.

Make the audience do exercises?

(2) I suspect the audience might have found it easier to implement the protocols if there had been an exercises portion of the talk instead of having it be all-lecture. I’m not sure on this point— the reason I had such a long “theory” section was because that was basically constructing an argument about “why should you guys listen to me, some rando, about how you can fix your social-romantic anxiety.” But possibly next year I’ll have credibility such that I can just launch into the “practical” section of the talk with an “epistemic status: trust me bro” at the beginning. Not sure, gonna think about it.

Feedback Issues

I think feedback would have been easier to come by had I upped the bounty to 600 dollars to begin with and done it split-the-pot style instead of raffle-style and announced it as such in the lecture. My strong suspicion is that most of the guys attending slutcon did not read the followup email from the people running the convention, and therefore did not see that now they have a remarkably easy way of earning like 70 bucks.

I think making people do a phone-survey over the last five minutes also could have been effective, though this would only give me an idea of “persuasiveness level of the talk” and not “did the talk’s methods actually work.” Still, we can think of this as a marketing funnel and “the talk was engaging and persuasive” is a prerequisite for people actually being willing to change their con experience to accommodate it.

Really, at this point what I have is precisely one example of a guy who wrote extensively detailed notes about his experiences with the protocols and who appears to have gone through probably-multiple cycles of implicit belief disconfirmation around “my safety behaviors were never keeping me safe, this changes everything.” That’s a big deal, and I’m proud of this and of him! But I need to be careful to not overgeneralize.

Followup

I should not let this go to my head; flaky breathroughs (as Chris Lakin reminds us) pervade basically all forms of self-help and other-help. It felt like when people did the things it pretty much worked out as I’d hoped, at least over the course of the weekend.

Now, if all I did was help some guys have a good weekend at their hedonistic sex festival, I would consider that to be entirely worthwhile. But my goal isn’t to “give some guys a good weekend,” my (admittedly lofty) goal is to figure out an order in which I can put words such that hearing those words will one-shot social/romantic anxiety for a significant subset of the anxious-nerd population. (By one-shot I mean “provide adequate scaffolding for people to bootstrap themselves out of social/romantic anxiety given perhaps four months of integration time.”)

This is, at its core, a very complicated engineering problem. I don’t think it’s an impossible engineering problem.

Anyway, that means I need to do followup. In a month I intend to ping the two people who had solid successes+gave me their contact info to ask about whether they felt this stuff had real-life application vs whether that was a Slutcon-specific experience for them.

Conclusions

I think the talk was clearly successful and well-liked enough, based on what I’ve seen thus far, that I can make a reasonable pitch to Pandora that I should give an improved version of it next year. There were a lot of guys who mostly stood on the sidelines this year owing to presumably social-romantic anxiety, and I want to be able to reach them and give them as good a Slutcon as I got.
