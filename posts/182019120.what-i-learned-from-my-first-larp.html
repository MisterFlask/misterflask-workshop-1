This last weekend I attended Fielding’s Hollow— a daylong LARP— because Alice (its GM) did a presentation at Metagame where she advertised it as a bizarre, stressful, and paranoid several-hour experience patterned after a game world she couldn’t stop thinking about (Pathologic) and I thought it sounded like an amazing time. And it was!

It’s tricky doing a review of a LARP when the LARP is actually an ongoing twice-yearly thing that you don’t want to spoil for others. “I can talk about it, just not about the content.” Hmm.

But spoilers excluded, here’s some things I noticed during Fielding’s Hollow, the most spectacularly involved and complex LARP I’ve ever heard of or suspect I will participate in:

The Bay Area Is Filled With Spreadsheet People

Spreadsheet people, when given a problem, will compulsively attempt to optimize around that problem. It is our nature; similar to how the scorpion will compulsively sting a frog carrying it across a pond, we will compulsively attempt to in-our-heads solve problems that we are given before performing any action toward them whatsoever. We will, in fact, attempt to solve our way out of having fun in a LARP because we can’t stop ourselves.

We have the spreadsheet-nature. It is who we are.

Character Bleed Is Brutally Difficult To Avoid

Personalities will out.

There’s a guy I know in the rationalist community who is deeply, deeply focused and agentic. Very leader-shaped. He was, for reasons, given a character meant to embody wu-wei, effortless action via non-doing.

I could tell he felt quite bad about his performance afterward because he mostly ignored this specific aspect of his character, having (as is the way of such things) accidentally turned himself into the primary political force in the LARP in spite of playing kind of a weird hermit character with no in-game political powers.

The intent of the GM was to give this guy a bit of a stretch, offering him an opportunity to run a sort of character that he’s not used to running. I think the reason it failed is because most character stretches let you actively perform the difference: I got a character who allows me to play grim seriousness when ordinarily I’m a bit of a goofball. I could lean into the seriousness. A character asks me to dance? I look at her and state flatly “Dancing is pointless frivolity.”

But wu-wei can’t really be leaned into in this way, because it’s defined mostly as the absence of effort. I think our agentic friend had problems because, ultimately, he was being asked to do nothing, and doing nothing is hard because he has goals and he is used to taking effective action to accomplish those goals. He has the spreadsheet-nature.

I don’t think resisting that is impossible, but I do think it’s harder than other kinds of character stretch in the same way that “stopping smoking cold-turkey” is harder than “taking up vaping instead of smoking.” Humans do better when given substitute actions.

There Are Three Modes Of Action

When a player is acting in a LARP— or any RPG, really— I suspect they continuously swap between three different modes:

  1. Character simulation: “What would Hiram Cross do?”

  2. Player-default: “What would I, personally, do?”

  3. Goal optimization: “What achieves my character’s stated objectives?”

I think most often I was doing (3): goal optimization. The character sheet specified goals, in a very loose sort of way; I would work toward those goals in whatever way felt like it made the most sense through whatever in-character means were available to me.

Spreadsheet people are in the habit of running mode (3) as often as possible. (It is— I suspect— why so many rationalist types are bad at flirting, but that’s another essay.)

It Is Freeing To Play Someone Who Is Predictably Irrational

My character had biases. There were specific people I was inclined to trust and help out, as spelled out in my character sheet. Others, less so.

Were they actually trustworthy? Like, for reals? I found it tremendously relaxing to not have to answer that question: my character was biased and I would act according to those biases. And then whatever happens, happens.

It was, in many ways, a permission structure to— at least for a little while— not be a spreadsheet person. That was great. Loved it. It was a way of forcing myself to go from goal-optimization mode to imitate-this-character mode, which is faster and easier and more fun.

But Also The Goals Are Probably Essential

It’s tricky because operating in mode (3) is itself stressful and anxiety-generating, intrinsically, and the LARP wanted to inspire those emotions. Like, explicitly one of the goals of the GM was to inspire what she called “beautiful suffering” in participants. But stress and anxiety are emotions lived in by the player, so this somewhat cuts against the desire for the players to just be inhabiting characters.

Can stress and anxiety be felt as a character? I’m not sure; it could be a mode of operating foreign enough to me that I simply didn’t see how to do it, and that it’s really just (as the kids say) a Skill Issue.

Tough call.

Bay Area People Love Compromise And Hate Conflict

The GM mentioned a few times that, when she ran the same game on the east coast, the players would yell at each other, throw pens, jockey for position, interrupt people, commit murder at the drop of a hat. Their body count at the end: fully half the players dead.

But on the west coast? We would constantly be giving turns for others to speak. Talking very quietly. Body count at the end? [few]/16 players dead, almost nobody. One time a character even said, in the middle of a town meeting:

“I have knowledge about several different simultaneous plots which I cannot say here because that would result in very bad things happening.”

And everyone just accepted it.

I don’t exclude myself from this analysis, by the way. As Judge Hiram Cross I absolutely could have thrown my weight around more and not accept this kind of lip from a random townsfolk. Would this have resulted in a better outcome for me? Probably not, but it would probably have been truer to the character.

The strategic spreadsheet-brain part of me observed that throwing my weight around in a way that probably would have made sense in-character would definitely not have been good for my political standing. Being loud and abrasive and angry is not the way one gains social standing in most circles I run in, and so naturally the strategic part of me shies away from that, especially after I subconsciously notice those norms are currently in effect in the LARP.

Would the character I was playing have been more abrasive and decisive? Honestly, probably!

But for my first time doing a LARP, I think I did all right.

(The GM found this maddening, by the way. She had carefully constructed all of these beautiful in-character conflicts and we tried to make compromises and be agreeable. Appalling!)

Thus concludes the saga of how I went to a LARP to experience beautiful suffering inside an intricately-designed pressure cooker, and instead, me and fifteen other Bay Area people politely optimized our way to a [few]/16 body count in a compromise ending that was apparently totally unprecedented in its peaceful affability.

We can only ever be who we are.
