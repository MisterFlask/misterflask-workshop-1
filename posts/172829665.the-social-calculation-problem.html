The People's Committee for Making Sure You're Having a Good Time

You have appointed yourself Central Planner of the party’s comfort economy. Your mandate: to ensure all conversation partners are happy and comfortable at all times.

Wait. Oh, no.

You have just realized: another guy at the party thinks that actually he's the Central Planner of this conversation (ridiculous!) The two of you have, by sheer happenstance, ended up in conversation together. You're having a good time. But— you think— he might not be.

Perhaps it's time to intervene in the comfort market.

You: (sweating profusely) "Hey, feel free to leave whenever!"

Him: (profusely sweating) "Oh, I’m fine! But no worries if you're busy!"

You: (sweating intensifies) “I have nowhere to be but I notice the 1 PM talks are starting if that’s something you were going to!”

Him:

It’s a deadly game of cat-and-also-cat: you both are attempting to hide your true preferences, while attempting to get full transparency about the other person’s true preferences. You never asked him to manage your wellbeing. He never asked you to offer him exit opportunities.

“I would really prefer to be the Conversation Bureau’s Central Planner for this conversation” is what you both want to say, but you can’t. Because then, you'd have to admit you've appointed yourself to the Bureau, which would be admitting the Bureau exists, which would be admitting you think the other person needs managing, which would be rude, which is exactly the dire outcome that the Bureau was founded to prevent.

The Revolution Eats Its Extremely Polite Children

In a bourgeoise market economy, the price signals should be simple: "I'm enjoying this" costs nothing to say. "I need to go" costs nothing to hear. But in the grand tradition of totalitarian social planners, you've abolished this market. Instead, you've created a system where all true preferences must be hidden, replaced by an elaborate codebook of false signals:

"Feel free to go whenever!" (Official translation: I am definitely not holding you hostage) (Alternative translation, sometimes used: I feel I am being held hostage, please leave)

"Oh, I'm having a great time!" (Official translation: I am happy in the current conversation) (Alternative translation, sometimes used: I feel you need reassurance, which I will now provide regardless of actual conversation quality)

"The 1 PM talks look interesting!" (Official translation: Here's a state-approved exit visa if you need one) (Alternative translation, sometimes used: I would like to go now, I really hope you find something on the schedule so I don’t have to kill the conversation myself)

Like Soviet factories producing heavy, useless cars because they were rated on “tonnage of car produced”, for all your effort you have succeeded in producing a sort of fake conversation with no actual information content.

You already know how this started. It's basically just social anxiety in a funny, plausibly-prosocial hat— you don’t want to be pushy, and you don’t want to accidentally override peoples’ preferences with your own. You appointed yourself to the Politburo because at least if you're managing everyone else's comfort, you're in control. You're not the one being rude. You're not the one trapping anyone. You've pre-emptively absolved yourself of the social sin of existence.

This might be able to kind of work if you have exactly one central planner. Never with two, or god help you, more than that. "Where should we eat?" becomes a farce where everyone insists they have no preferences until you all end up at the one place nobody wanted but everyone could pretend to tolerate. The group chat planning a weekend trip devolves into "I'm flexible!" and "Whatever works for everyone!" until you've collectively agreed to do precisely nothing, which at least has the virtue of being equally disappointing to all.

The Social Calculation Problem

You've been trying to be the Universal Comfort Administrator, the Central Bureau of Not Being Rude, the Soviet Planning Committee but for all human interaction. And now, you are running into what the old Austrian economists called the "socialist calculation problem." No single person or group can possess enough information to centrally plan all social comfort. Not even you.

The market solution? Everyone bears responsibility for their own comfort (a quantity only they can truly know), and everyone is responsible for requesting things they personally need. Sometimes someone stays in a conversation two minutes longer than they prefer and (here's the surprising bit) they survive.

Adults are allowed to say "I need to go." It's not your job to say it for them through increasingly baroque social semaphore.
