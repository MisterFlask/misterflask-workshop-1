[Context: My college friends and I have a tradition called Secret Fiction Santa, whereby each participant writes a story request and gets paired up with a random other participant whose job it is to fulfill that story request. I wrote this (the prompt was “the stairwell into night”), and I acquired permission from the recipient to post it here also.]

“Wait, and they didn’t find the trapdoor during inspection?”

“Joe. Come on. This is San Francisco. If you want to buy a house you basically have to waive the inspection. Which is also how I wound up with a mold problem that’s looking like it’ll take 30 grand to remediate, but we can address that later.”

“So… what’s beyond the trapdoor? Torture dungeon? Sex dungeon? Some secret, third type of dungeon?”

“None of the above!” Alice said. “It’s just a stairwell.”

“A stairwell? Like, below the house?”

“Yeah. Weird, right?”

“Where does it go?”

“No idea. I went down five stories but I got creeped out at that point and decided to turn around. Just kept going down, as far as I can tell. It’s one of those old-timey concrete stairwells that wraps around an empty shaft. Big, too. The stairs measure ten feet across.”

“Any idea what’s at the bottom?”

“Nope. Can’t actually see to the bottom, there’s no lighting so it just looks like an empty void.”

“Oooh. Okay. So… are you going to go further? See what’s down there? Like an explorer or one of those people who get eaten in horror movies?”

Alice thought for a second. “Well… I want to know what’s down there, but honestly it’s really dark and cold and it kinda freaks me out. I’ll have to think about how to tackle this.”

“Interesting. Interesting. So, I’m thinking probably five hundred dollars,” the man said.

Alice frowned. “Excuse me, I listed this task as one hundred dollars.”

“Yeah, but that’s before you told me it’s in a spooky-ass haunted stairwell.”

“Did I say haunted? I did not. You said haunted.”

“Five hundred or I’m out.”

“I’ll give you a bad review on TaskRabbit.”

He snorted. “Go ahead, I don’t give a shit.”

“...ugh. Fine,” Alice said. “Five hundred dollars. But make sure you get a recording of all the floors with your phone.”

“And you give me five stars.”

“Fine, whatever.”

“Seventy-five flights?” Joe said.

“At least. He tapped out at seventy-five. It goes down further from there.”

“Jesus. What do you think is at the bottom?”

“Well, if my instincts from watching the entirety of the Netflix horror catalogue are any indicator,” Alice said, “at the bottom is probably a monster, or, like, a mirror that shows your future, or some kind of weird door.”

“So… okay, it sounds like you’re not planning an expedition to explore its bottommost reaches?”

“Actually, I was thinking this could be a priceless opportunity.”

“For a gruesome death?”

“Well, also yes, but that’s not what I was thinking of. Actually I was thinking of whether we could, you know, monetize this space in some way. Our mortgage is ungodly and honestly if I could figure out some way of running a home business that makes use of my apparently infinite stairwell space this might have potential to earn a tidy profit.”

Joe snapped his fingers. “Oh! Like a fitness influencer challenge? How many flights can YOU do?”

“Ehh, feels gimmicky. Plus the wi-fi won’t reach down more than a couple flights before it cuts out.”

“Paranormal tourism?”

“I mean, nothing paranormal actually happens. It’s just a long stairwell.”

“Sure, that we know of! Also, nothing paranormal happens at other paranormal tourism sites either, so.”

Alice tapped her foot. “Hmmm. Still not feeling it. What if we used it as a dumping site? We do have that central well that goes down maybe-forever. Infinite capacity dumping ground!”

Joe snorted. “Sure, if you wanted randos bringing their trash through your house at all hours of the day and night. Plus, landfills don’t actually make that much; I had this idea earlier and ran it by ChatGPT, and turns out the trapdoor actually ends up being our bottleneck. Under reasonable assumptions we’d make basically nothing, unless we tore down the house and built ourselves a chute so we could move real volume. But then the city of San Francisco would be up our asses about the fact that we’re zoned residential.”

“Fuck. Of course. Residential zoning, that’s it. We could repurpose the stairwell as residential apartments. Tiny ones, of course. Tension rods and curtains to divide the walking portions of the stairwell from the living portions. It’ll be like Kowloon Walled City!”

Joe gave her a flat look. “Kowloon Walled City was a humanitarian disaster, Alice.”

“It was also extremely profitable for the landlords. Generational wealth, Joe.”

“It’s also unbelievably illegal.”

“Pfft. So are like half the basement flats in San Francisco. They don’t get shut down because the tenants don’t want to report it or else they’ll get kicked out. It’ll be the same here!”

“Wait, but we don’t have any rooms. We just have, literally, a stairwell. A stairwell that never ends.”

“What is a room, Joe? Like, conceptually.”

“I don’t–”

“It’s a place with a bed and privacy. Each landing on that stairwell—I measured it, it’s 10x10 feet. If you allocate two feet of that for travel, this gets you an 8x8 space. That’s actually livable. Put a bed down there and a desk and use some curtains for room dividers and you have the kind of cozy living accommodation that in this hot rental market will go for 500 a month, easy. Maybe more. Wait, lemme get all this down.”

“I don’t think you’ll be able to make people pay 500 dollars a month for a landing in an unlit concrete stairwell, Alice. You’d need lighting, internet, some way to get food and supplies down there—”

“Ooh. Keep going.”

“Excuse me, I’m listing problems, not solutions.”

“Lighting, internet, food delivery. What else?”

“I mean, obviously sanitation. You can’t have people trekking up forty flights every time they need to pee. Past maybe five residents you’d need distributed facilities. Composting toilets or something, every five floors or so. But—”

“Composting toilets! Every five floors. I’m writing this down.”

“Alice. Stop writing.”

“What about the internet thing you said?”

“You’d have to run ethernet, you can’t rely on wifi, the signal wouldn’t penetrate that much concrete. But that’s not the point—”

“Ethernet. Got it. And you mentioned food delivery? Joe, this is all extremely helpful.”

“We’re really doing this, aren’t we.”

“You’re basically my co-founder at this point. And boyfriend.”

“For now!”

“So this is the entrance. Watch your step.”

“Uh-huh,” Maya said, peering down. “How far does it go?”

“We’re still figuring that out.”

“You’re still figuring that out.”

“Don’t worry about it. Anyway, you’d be on floor six, so it’s not really relevant to your situation. Come on.”

“Is that a microwave?”

“Oh, that’s Derek’s. He does QA for a gaming company. Works remote. Ask him if you want to use it.”

“He lives behind a curtain.”

“He picked the curtain himself. We encourage tenants to personalize their dwellings.”

Maya seemed to consider this. “What’s he paying?”

“Four fifty, but he’s on a legacy rate. Floors five and below are five hundred.”

Floor six was empty. A bare concrete square, ten by ten, with the stairwell continuing both above and below.

“So this would be yours,” Alice said. “I’d throw in a bulb, obviously. You provide your own furnishings. Bed, desk, whatever. Most people do a little rug, makes it homier.”

Maya walked to the edge of the landing and looked down the central shaft. The lights continued for another ten or twelve floors before the darkness swallowed them entirely. “What’s down there?”

“More floors.”

“Past the lights, I mean.”

“More floors.”

“Have you been down there?”

“I hired a guy. He made it to seventy-five.”

“Seventy-five floors?”

“Yeah. Then he got spooked.”

There was an uncomfortable silence.

“What’s the bathroom situation?”

“Composting toilet on every fifth floor. We’re still installing. For now you’d trek up to use mine. Just don’t make a mess.”

“Laundry?”

“There’s a laundromat on 24th Street.”

“Is there any natural light?”

“No.”

“Okay. What about noise? Is it echoey?”

“You’d think so, right? But actually it’s weirdly quiet. Sound really doesn’t travel down here. Nor does light, really.”

“Creepy.”

“Don’t worry about it.”

“Does the wifi reach down here?”

“We ran an ethernet cable. You get a solid 200 megabits.”

“First and last month?”

“Plus a security deposit. But I’m flexible on the deposit if you want to do a payment plan.”

“Can I move in Saturday?”

“I’ll email you the lease,” Alice said. “Welcome to floor six.”

“A paternoster,” Joe said. “You want to install a paternoster.”

“Down the well hole, yeah. Think about it. Continuous loop, no waiting, services every floor simultaneously. It’s perfect. I sketched out a diagram, check it out.”

“Paternosters are—Alice, they’re not even legal anymore. In most of Europe they’ve been banned since the seventies because people kept getting crushed.”

“Good thing we’re not in Europe.”

“They’re not legal here either.”

“Joe. We’re running an unlicensed rooming house in an infinite stairwell.”

Joe rubbed his temples. “Okay. Sure. Walk me through the engineering here.”

“Simple: we just need enough juice and materials to build the lift down to, let’s say, floor 100, then loop back. That’s our serviceable zone.”

“Your serviceable zone.”

“Right. Below floor 100 is—I don’t know, we’ll call it the Exclusion Zone. We can still rent down there, but it’ll probably be mostly storage space. Or… I don’t know, tenants are imaginative, I’m sure they’ll find some use for it. But we won’t support it directly.”

“Huh. So you’ve got a paternoster running a hundred floors down into a concrete shaft. How are you powering this thing?”

“We’ll put the motor above the trapdoor and power it from an outlet in the main house.”

“Wait. Can a wall outlet even supply that much power?”

“No, listen, I looked this up. A paternoster doesn’t actually use that much power. Each car isn’t driven individually—the whole thing is basically a giant bike chain connected to a single motor at the top. We put the motor in the living room and run the chain down the well hole and our food and water transportation problems are solved.”

“The living room.”

“I’ll put a rug over it! You won’t even notice it’s there.”

Joe stared at her for a long moment. “And the food service component?”

“Right! So three times a day—breakfast, lunch, dinner—we load up the cars with meals. Residents can subscribe to the meal plan, a hundred bucks a week, and their food just arrives. They hear the car coming, they step over to the well hole, they grab their stuff. It’ll be the best ready-to-eat food Costco has to offer. And by ‘best’ I of course mean ‘best cost-per-calorie ratio’, don’cha know. Also tapwater, free of charge.”

“How do they know which car is theirs?”

“We number the cars. You live on floor 47, your food is in car 47. Simple.”

“What if they miss it?”

“Then it comes around again. That’s the beauty of the paternoster. Continuous loop!”

“Comes around again from where? From floor 100? That’s… one sec… like an hour round trip, and that’s if the thing moves at any reasonable speed.”

“Ninety minutes, I calculated. So you really don’t want to miss your car.”

Joe looked at her skeptically. “What happens to the food in the meantime? It’s just riding up and down in a concrete shaft?”

“Costco dry foods actually keep quite well. Beef jerky. Those little fruit bars.”

“Wait, it just occurred to me to ask. How cold does it get in there?”

“Fifty-five degrees at every hour of the day. Natural refrigeration, basically. It’s a feature.”

“The tenants are living in fifty-five-degree stairwells?”

“Don’t look at me like that; they dress warmly and it’s fine. I told them no space heaters, the power draw is too high. Anyway, that’s not the point. The point is, with the paternoster in place, we unlock the lower floors as viable housing. Right now we’re capped at maybe floor 20; past that, the walk to the surface becomes prohibitive. People don’t want to climb twenty flights just to get a burrito. But if we bring the burrito to them? Suddenly floor 60 is attractive. Floor 80, even.”

“You’re going to have people living on floor 80.”

“At four hundred a month! Plus the meal plan. We’re providing so much affordable housing, Joe. We’re basically public benefactors at this point.”

“Sure! Like all those people in jail.”

“You worry too much.”

“How are you even going to set up this kind of lift? You’re not an engineer.”

“Eh, I’ll just use Taskrabbit.”

“So many spreadsheets, Joe.”

“Can’t run a slum without spreadsheets.”

“It’s not a slum! It’s affordable housing.”

“Right.”

“Okay, with that established, hit me with the finances. How are we doing?”

“Surprisingly all right! We had to hire an electrician to install some, uh, stuff to handle the power draw. So that was an expense.”

“Stuff?”

“Big-boy wiring– extension cords literally don’t work past a few dozen yards because something something electrical resistance something something. I don’t really know the details. Anyway, that cost a few grand, and we’ve been going through maybe a hundred bucks a day in Costco groceries which the residents compensate us for via the meal plan; we’ve hired a nice immigrant woman to be our well attendant for another hundred bucks a day plus free housing on floor 11.”

“What do you mean, ‘well attendant’?”

“Food doesn’t put itself into the paternoster. And garbage doesn’t remove itself. She’s a steal at twice the price, trust me.”

“I guess that makes sense. Okay, please continue.”

“Sure. The paternoster was just a few grand, but we had to cut a couple corners engineering-wise. Nothing too much to worry about. And aside from the Costco groceries and staffing costs, these are all basically one-time fixed expenses, so we’ve been in the black for probably three weeks now.”

“Cool, cool.”

“Okay, so, revenue,” Joe said, pulling up another spreadsheet. “Floors one through fifteen, we’re at 94% occupancy. Sixteen through fifty, more like 60%, which makes sense, that’s a hike. Fifty through—”

“Wait, we have people past fifty now?”

“Alice. You have a guy on floor eighty.”

“I do?”

“I CC’d you on the leases. Leases, plural.”

“You CC me on everything, you know I don’t read those.”

“Anyway, fifty through eighty is where it gets patchy. I’m showing maybe forty percent occupancy, but honestly I’m not sure the numbers are right because some of these people are paying through the Warden and I don’t know if he’s remitting to you or what; we just pay him two hundred a month out of rents, which–”

Alice set down her coffee. “Joe.”

“Yeah?”

“What the fuck is the Warden.”

“Alice. Thank you for coming down.”

“I didn’t catch your name,” Alice said. “Your actual name. You’re what my boyfriend calls the Warden, correct?”

“I am, yes.” His voice was scratchy and tired. “My name is Gerald.”

“Okay, Gerald. My colleague tells me some of my rent payments are going through you now.”

“Some of the lower residents prefer it. I keep records.” He held up a clipboard. “It all gets remitted, minus expenses. You can audit me if you like.”

“I will. What else is going on down here? Joe mentioned demands.”

“Concerns. I’ve consolidated them by floor cluster. Floors 31 through 40 would like the paternoster schedule shifted an hour earlier. The current timing interferes with the quiet hours.”

“What quiet hours?”

“Seven to nine PM. It’s been in place for about two months.”

“Who established quiet hours?”

“It emerged organically.”

“...I see. What else?”

“Floors 41 through 50 have requested a secondary ethernet line. Also, there’s a dispute about the composting toilet on 45. Floors 46 and 47 feel that 43 and 44 are not following the agreed-upon protocols.”

“What protocols?”

“It’s an internal matter, I wouldn’t worry about it. Just keeping you updated. Anyway, I’d say we’ve got things well in hand up to about floor 79.”

“Wait. What’s going on past floor 79?”

“Well, I’ll be honest with you: at this point, I don’t really know. There’s a barricade at floor 79. We built it about six weeks ago.”

“You built a barricade. In my stairwell.”

“Just for safety.”

“Safety from what?”

“The below-80 situation.”

“What is the below-80 situation?”

Gerald looked uncomfortable. “Listen, I don’t have full visibility into that. I’m only the Warden through level 79.”

“Gerald. I own this stairwell.”

“Do you?”

There was an uncomfortable silence.

“The rent is current,” Gerald said. “I want to be clear about that. Whatever’s happening below, the rent has always been current.”

“Ma’am, I’m here because we received a few complaints about this address. Electrical violations, sanitation, unlicensed construction, failure to provide adequate egress—”

“Who’s complaining?”

“I can’t disclose that.”

“Was it the Hendersons? It was the Hendersons, wasn’t it. Wait, are you a cop?”

“I’m a building inspector. Can I come in?”

“Can I say no?”

“Not really.”

The motor was, regrettably, hard to miss. It dominated the living room beneath a large Persian rug slung haphazardly over its chassis. Chains stretched taut from the motor to the trapdoor, its opening slightly ajar.

“So… what’s under the trapdoor?”

“Storage space.”

“What’s the motor for?”

“…the storage space?”

“Ma’am.”

“It’s for moving things. In the storage space. Up and down.”

“Was this change to the property permitted?”

Alice hesitated. “I’m actually not sure your authority extends below grade. Isn’t subterranean space a different department?”

“It is not.”

“Are you sure? I feel like I read something about that.”

“I’m sure. Ma’am, I’m going to need you to open the trapdoor.”

“I’d really prefer not to.”

“It wasn’t a request.”

“...Fine, but I want it noted that I have concerns about your jurisdiction.”

The inspector leaned over the opened trapdoor, phone flashlight barely illuminating the first landing. “Ma’am, how many floors is this?”

“Define ‘floors.’”

“I’m seeing what appears to be a continuous concrete stairwell descending beyond the range of my flashlight. I’m also seeing what appears to be a chain-driven vertical conveyor system. I’m also seeing—” He squinted. “Is that a person?”

A cheerful feminine voice drifted out of the darkness. “Buenas noches!”

“That’s Marta. She’s the well attendant.”

“The well attendant.”

“She handles, uh, logistics.”

“Ma’am, is she living down there?”

“The stairwell predates the building. I’m pretty sure it’s grandfathered.”

“That’s not—” He pinched the bridge of his nose. “That doesn’t make sense. Structurally or legally.”

“And yet.”

“Ma’am, how many people are living in this stairwell?”

“I’d have to check the spreadsheet.”

“Give me a ballpark.”

“...A number between fifty and two hundred?”

“You don’t know?”

“There’s some confusion past floor seventy-nine. Governance-wise.”

“This is—” He closed his eyes. “This is the most illegal thing I’ve ever seen.”

“In San Francisco? Really?”

“I’m giving you thirty days.”

“Marcus. Sweetie. Come look at this.”

He found Jen in the living room, standing over a trapdoor she’d pulled open.

“What the hell? Was this in the disclosure? How’d we miss this?”

“We waived the inspection, remember?”

“Right. God damn this housing market.”

Marcus pulled out his phone and pointed the flashlight down. Concrete stairs, wrapping around an empty shaft. The beam faded into nothing.

“How far do you think it goes?” Jen asked.

“No idea. I can’t see the bottom.”

“We should get someone to check this out. Like a contractor.”

“Yeah.” Marcus was quiet for a moment. “Although—”

“Although what?”

“I don’t know. Just thinking.” He looked at the landing below. “Wow. This is actually pretty big. Each landing is, what, ten by ten?”

“I guess? Why?”

“No reason. Just thinking.”
