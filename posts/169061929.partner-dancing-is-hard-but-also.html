Over the past seven months (or thereabouts) I decided that one of my big Unemployment Projects would be trying to get into partner dance.

It’s been one hell of a ride!

Why Do It At All?

I’m single; I aim to be less-single. Dance is pretty great for that, for a lot of reasons!

  1. A dance lasts for like 3 minutes. What if, instead of making boring small talk, you could get a full-on vibe check with an attractive stranger without saying any words at all, while doing something joyful and embodied?

  2. Dancing well is sexy. (He said, so, so obviously.)

  3. Dance studios often transform into full-on communities because dance is hard and sucks up a frankly obscene number of hours to gain any level of proficiency at, especially for leads. Hundreds, easily. Consequently, all the people who really devote themselves to getting good at it are spending a lot of time in the dance studio, and they all get to know one another quickly!

Beginner Hell Is Real

Starting out from zero in partner dancing blows. Not quite universally, but perhaps close enough to “universally” that you can see it on a clear day. This is especially true starting out as a lead doing group classes, because you’re learning a lot of things at once:

  1. how to keep on the beat

  2. the literal steps of the dance

  3. The memorized sequence of moves the instructors are teaching this week (called “patterns”)

  4. the arm motions that go along with these patterns, which tell the follow what the next move in the dance is.

This is a lot of stuff!

Now, the way introductory group classes tend to work is the instructors are trying to teach a pattern over the course of perhaps 30 minutes. Here’s the loop: they give you a new piece of the pattern, you get 2-3 tries at doing this pattern with a follow, and then they repeat with additional elements in the pattern, and you keep doing that until the lesson ends.

The emotional experience when I was just starting was wanting to apologize constantly because I could tell, both innately and from the facial expressions of the follows, that I was not providing a positive dance experience, even in this tightly controlled environment where I’m just doing a chain of predefined steps. And it worsened as the class would go on, because the instructors (by the nature of the group class) were continuing to add new elements even as I was failing to digest the elements they’d already given me. By the end it sometimes felt like I was drowning in a firehose of information, flailing uselessly to the music as follows looked on in horror.

Beginner hell is finite (and I’m well past it now), but it is real.

Anyway, after a few tries at group classes where I felt super stressed out I decided it would be less mortifying to just shell out money for some private lessons so that I can be taught precisely to the level I’m at. Which is expensive— private instruction starts at like 70 bucks an hour and goes up from there— but honestly I’ve got money and I lack dance skill and the beauty of capitalism is I can exchange one for the other!

Which leads me to my next bit:

Group Classes Aren’t For Learning (For Leads)

Really, group classes succeed in three things:

  1. It tells you about new dance elements that you can perform. Every piece of the pattern the instructors teach you should be independently leadable in social settings, and the skill of taking apart the patterns and cannibalizing individual moves in them is deeply useful for increasing your dance vocabulary.

  2. Group classes give you more face time with the follows; a weird facet of partner dance group classes is you make friends with the follows much faster than the leads because they’re who you spend all your time dancing with. Making friends with leads is also valuable but has to be done much more intentionally.

  3. If you know how to do a move, but fail often at it, this gives you a good place to gain reps.

Here’s what group classes don’t teach you: how to lead a dance. The core issue is structural: the follows know what’s coming at every step of the dance because everyone’s doing the same choreography, which means even if you’re totally failing to communicate a particular move of the dance a lot of times the follow will do it anyway because they “know” they’re supposed to. (This is called backleading, and it’s a pretty common bad habit in new and sometimes even quite experienced follows. Dance is hard!)

Private 1:1 instruction is where most learning to lead occurs

By “private instruction” i basically mean either a professional you’re paying to teach you or a follow that’s down to practice with you.

But 1:1 practice allows you to drill down into what leading a move right feels like in your body. The loop in 1:1 practices, frequently, is that you attempt to lead a dance, and then you’ll do a dance move that doesn’t land, and then the instructor will kill the music and have you try that move alone, in isolation, and then you get correction— “you need to shift your body weight more“, or “you need to provide resistance when i’m pushing against you in this hand on count 2“ or “you’re trying to move me with the palm of your hand but it needs to be with the side“ or “you need to move your shoulder down on this step“ or “you just touched my boob” (accidentally!). It gets remarkably granular.

One time my dance instructor had me lead a Madrid step in bachata with foam blocks under my armpits and a PVC pipe held in both my hands to lock my body into the correct configuration and it worked.

The Social-Economics of Getting Practice Partners in Dance

There are a few asymmetries in learning to dance as a lead vs as a follow:

First, following isn’t strictly easier than leading— at a high level it might even be harder since you have to be able to read and respond to a lot of signals at once— but the learning curve is universally agreed to be much steeper when you’re starting out as a lead. You can have fun as a good lead dancing with a very inexperienced follow; with a very inexperienced lead nobody is getting a good dance. Beginner Hell for leads lasts longer than for follows.

Second, there’s also a bit where… well, it’s a bit crude to talk about, but if someone is going to be down to dance with you regularly then you need to be providing them value of some kind. That value can be in the form of “dancing well enough that they can work on their stuff instead of just walking you through your stuff.” The value might be in the form of just “being a really fun and engaging person to be around”; oftentimes friends or romantic partners serve this role since the value they're getting is “fun hangout that maybe gets my friend deeper into my hobby.”

The value can also be in the form of “being a pretty girl”; I spoke to a girl at my studio, actually, who reminisced that she mostly advanced in skill by just going to socials as an attractive but unskilled 20-something and getting danced by just a whole bunch of leads. Totally works!

But this leads to a bit of a rich-get-richer dynamic for, specifically, male leads, since before a hundred or so hours of dance experience you’re not particularly rewarding to practice with, so hunting down practice follows can be genuinely quite tricky.

Paid instruction is an effective but expensive way to fill this gap while you’re initially learning. There’s also an emotional component— I felt way less guilty about not-providing-pleasant-dances while training with someone who’s getting paid 80 dollars an hour to witness me suck. Mostly You’re Learning About Communicating Via Dance Physics

There is a thing I like to call “dance physics.” Like: all partner dances I’ve tried have a “basic step” which is kinda the default behavior for both lead and follow in the absence of the lead doing anything. All the other steps of the dance branch off the basic step and flow back into it; the basic step is like the canvas on which the more-complicated elements of the dance— turns, spins, dips— are painted.

The follow’s part of the dance is, in some ways, a deterministic flowchart (technically a state machine diagram, if you know what that is) where the lead’s role is primarily about communicating to the follow what part of the flowchart they’re doing next. Learning dance for leads is chiefly about internalizing this flowchart at a deep enough level where you can improvise on the fly based on the music you’re hearing at a given moment.

Take salsa: Salsa has the lead and follow dancing forward and back in the basic step. There is also, conceptually, an “empty side lane” to the left and right of the dancing pair, and if the lead steps into one of these side lanes then the follow, at certain beats, is expected to step into the center lane past the lead; at which point the lead might step back into the lane, and the end result is the partners have swapped places. This follow behavior is a fact about salsa’s dance physics; it is fundamental, and the lead uses this fact to scaffold moves that can be done in the expectation that the follow obeys salsa-physics.

Everything in partner dance is kind of like this. Follows have a lot of freedom to do stuff independent of this structure (traditionally referred to as “styling”) and so do leads; but while they’re starting out everyone has their hands full just in trying to follow the flowchart.

Dance is a whole-ass language spoken by the body; basic communication comes well before witty flourishes.

I am!

It's a tale as old as time, really: partner dance is a demon which promises romantic success to its conjuror. Inevitably in the fullness of time, the demon will slip its bindings, murder the summoner, and wear his body around as a skinsuit. I’ve spoken to a couple highly skilled leads at the studio and apparently that’s often the way things go.

So wait, how long does it take to get out of beginner hell?

For leads, at a guess: Probably about 20 hours of group classes, or 5 hours of private instruction, or some mix of each; that’s how you get to “slightly comfortable mediocrity where you don’t feel the urge to apologize constantly.” For follows: man idk probably less

Out of curiosity, how much money have you spent on this project of yours?

I’m trying to learn both salsa and bachata and dance is hard, so this has been a project of about seven months. Probably the cost breakdown has been:

  • 4x private lessons a week (2 in salsa, 2 in bachata) for ~24 weeks: roughly $8000 dollars?

  • Other dance related stuff (studio membership, etc): roughly $1500 dollars.

The other cost has been time: I’ve spent probably on average 15-20 hours a week doing dance stuff over these past months. It’s basically a part-time job that you're paying to attend. Now, this job is fun and comes with a community and you get friends and exercise and maybe hotness out of it, so I think it’s totally worth it, but let’s be real: to get the sweet ROI out of dance as a lead you’re gonna have to invest hard in some mix of hours and dollars. Not as much as I did, necessarily, but this is a hell of a hobby and certainly the most rewarding of the unemployment projects I have engaged in, so I have no regrets.
