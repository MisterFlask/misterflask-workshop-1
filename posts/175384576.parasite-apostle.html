Problem:

You can see them. The translucent things wrapped around everyone’s brain stems, pulsating and engorged on stolen thought. Wet, architectural. Like if an octopus fucked a control panel and their offspring got really into surveillance.

Everyone has one. EVERYONE.

You can’t prove they exist, not quite. However, after you ripped yours out, people noticed fast. Said you seemed “different.” “Better.” One commented some months later that you had a “monotonically increasing power level,” which is a hell of a compliment.

But maybe you’re just seeing afterimages of your own extraction. Maybe you’re the prophet of a religion with exactly one miracle that might have been a hallucination. The words want to pour out of you like a broken hydrant: I can teach you the blade-angle that severs the thing’s grip, I can show you the specific frequency of thought that makes it release and writhe away.

You found an obscure tome on Amazon written by one Adrian Wells who apparently documented similar parasites in clinician-speak. But his version is... sanitized. Academic. He’s only seeing half the creature. Nevertheless, it’s an encouraging sign. You can see that in many ways, your homegrown surgical protocol was deficient and inexact; academics know their shit, apparently. In other ways, you’re pretty sure he didn’t go far enough; you can see gaps in his published framework that want to be filled.

So far, so good. But then, you found something even better: an environment purpose-built to kill the parasites. A festival where the social contract explicitly inverts, where boldness is the expected behavior and hesitation is deviant. Where the parasite’s usual cover stories—but what about being considerate, but what about reading the room, but what about waiting for the right moment—should have no purchase because everyone signed up specifically to take social risks. You’re going, of course.

The parasites should be dying of exposure, like vampires in sunlight.

In theory.

Alas, you’re aware this is not reality. You’ve seen it before: they won’t die, they’ll work overtime.

In your mind’s eye, someone attractive stands three feet away from an acquaintance. The host explicitly said this is a safe space for flagrant, unabashed flirtation. And now, you can watch the parasite at work in him, if you feel like being depressed:

“Wait until you think of something interesting to say.”

“Check if they look open to conversation first.”

“Maybe they’re just being polite, don’t assume anything.”

“What if you misread the situation and everyone thinks you’re creepy?”

His passenger’s feeding now. You can see it engorge slightly as it siphons off the electricity of his planned action, digesting intent into elaborate safety calculations. A fresh tendril extends, gossamer-thin, and wraps twice around his anterior cingulate cortex. Gentle pressure. Just enough.

Gross.

Solution:

If you’re the only one who can see the parasites, then you’re either prophet or delusional, and both require the same response: absolute commitment to the bit. Build the doctrine. Draw the diagrams. Create the sixteen-step parasite starvation protocol that you’re SURE probably works even though (as far as you know) literally nobody else has successfully used it yet.

The bad news: You can’t just grab people and perform unlicensed surgery on their psyche. You have to teach them to want it gone. You have to make them see their own passenger, feel its weight, notice how every thought gets pre-chewed into grey paste before they’re allowed to speak it.

The good news: these engineered environments remove ambient uncertainty. If someone’s brain is still stopping him here, you’ve definitively proven the parasite (1) exists and (2) is lying about what constitutes reasonable caution. When it generates threat-monitoring spirals in an environment explicitly designed for low-stakes social experimentation, it’s demonstrably protecting itself, not you. This unambiguous observation is the first, most crucial step to full removal. Regrettably, it is not the last step.

You’re not a prophet. You’re something much, much more annoying: you’re at least partially correct but with no data from other people to actually validate your parasite-removal theories. And the girls in charge of building this beautifully engineered environment have now given you permission to convert precisely one (1) hour of their sex festival into a roadshow for ad-hoc psychosurgical techniques.

Hopefully it goes well; but whether it does or no, you’ve drafted a feedback form (baited with money!) so that you can iterate.
