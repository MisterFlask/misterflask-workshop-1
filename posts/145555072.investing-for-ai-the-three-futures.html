Introduction

AI is a pretty big deal [citation needed]. It does, of course, have investing implications.

For it is written:

I’m not actually that sure the world ends from AI, of course. Let’s say… forty percent. And frankly, the idea of “having to be sure of a prediction to act on it” is flawed in itself. The world is made of probabilities; “certainty” and “proof” are fairytales made for fools and children.

For it is also written:

So, let’s say that “AI ends the world” is among the hypotheses I am entertaining about how the future shakes out. Really, I see three different futures ahead of us. Let’s take them in order!

Future 1: The Scaling Laws Hold Until We Get The Singularity In Like Five To Ten Years, Good Luck

After the singularity in like 99% of futures either everyone’s dead or there is unthinkable abundance. Either way, your portfolio doesn’t matter. I keep hearing midwit takes on Twitter about “Sam Altman or whoever wants to build God so he can make a bunch of money off of it” and I always want to ask these people: what do you think “having built God” means if not being utterly beyond scarcity in any sense including monetary? You aren’t going to run your hedge fund using God! You’re going to use God to build a paradise where instead of running a stupid hedge fund you and everyone else spend your days creating art and having awesome sex with a variety of extremely compatible partners, for all of eternity!

Similar sentiments include “after having achieved omnipotence and omniscience I’m going to corner the market on safflower oil.” I feel like there should be a pithy name for this kind of thinking. “Thinking too small” doesn’t really cover it.

That being the case, what I’m interested in is investment implications up to the point of the singularity and not after. Because either it’s an unthinkable paradise or everyone’s dead.

Good Investments:

  • NVDA (since they make the only AI hardware anyone respects)

  • TSMC (since they supply NVDA and, also, are a majority of worldwide market share for computer chips of any kind)

  • ASML, maybe? (Since they make the machines TSMC needs to make the chips.)

  • The hyperscalers and tech megacorps that own the big, sexy customer touchpoints: Microsoft, Google, maybe Meta, maybe Apple. Since fundamentally the AI models themselves seem unlikely to be huge differentiators over any reasonable span of time, which means they will chiefly be differentiated by what data they have access to. e.g. Microsoft Copilot can access your Outlook and Teams data, and so on for the other hyperscalers.

Bad Investments:

  • Long-term government bonds.

    • skyrocketing productivity should cause interest rates to increase

    • Additionally, if interest rates increase that would tank both your investments in bonds and your investments in the above hyperscalers and big tech companies. It’s very much correlated with your risk-on assets.

  • Commodities (because the price of stuff is going way down because of robotics)

  • Companies that, broadly speaking, offer undifferentiated commodity intellectual labor, which is among the first things that you can replace with AI. This includes:

    • Shutterstock/Getty Images

    • Chegg

    • Call centers; first-level support— the kind you don’t give any actual power to— can probably be swapped out with AI fairly easily once GPT-4o is enabled for this use case.

    • Fiverr/Upwork (I’m not sure about this one; you could make an argument that “hire a person to do some kind of ai-assisted work“ actually represents a value add for these companies because instead of “hire a fiverr person to draw some shitty art“ you get “hire a fiverr person to repeatedly hammer at Midjourney until he gets something kinda good.“)

Future 2: Future #1, Except Disaster Struck

You know! Like war, or pestilence.

There are a lot of ways this one shakes out, but the common theme here is “The AI people were right about the scaling laws and their implications, but there was a war or a really bad Carrington Event and that sets back scaling for a decade.”

Broadly speaking, disasters are terrible for equities and good for “safe assets”: gold, long-term US treasuries. It seems also plausible that if the disaster hits TSMC then Intel and friends will benefit from being the only games in town (since TSMC accounts for a majority of world semiconductor manufacturing and without them other companies would love to pick up the slack.)

Honestly the most obvious thing that might occur here is “China decides to invade Taiwan as they have been threatening to do since forever and then the USA cruise missiles TSMC to stop the CCP from getting their hands on it, which is also an intention the US has very clearly telegraphed.” I probably think too much about this particular calamity; the space of calamities is very large; any number of things could happen and this is just one.

Other massively disruptive events include other possible wars, especially involving the US, or the Climate Change Disaster arriving on-schedule.

Still, I do have a couple grand worth of far-out-of-the-money TSMC puts. Just in case.

Good Investments

  • Gold

  • TSMC puts (if this was brought on by China invading Taiwan and accidentally destroying TSMC.)

  • EWT puts (same; EWT is the taiwanese stock market ETF)

  • Long-Term Government Bonds

  • Commodities

  • Intel/GlobalFoundries/Tower/Samsung, who get to fill the market void left by TSMC

  • Defense industries, I guess?

Bad Investments

  • NVDA (dependency on TSMC)

  • ASML (TSMC was a big customer)

  • Basically all other equities since TSMC undergirds most of the world economy. Nobody is having a great day other than perhaps gold bugs and survivalist dudes hiding with their guns out in the Minnesota hinterlands.

Future 3: Actually Gary Marcus Was Right

In this future, LLMs are hitting a wall. That wall probably is something along the lines of:
LLMs are basically just associative memory; they need long-range planning to do anything useful and current techniques need extensive rework in order to enable this long-range planning.

or

Because we teach these models through imitating the training corpus, you can’t really get much smarter than a somewhat above-average human.

or even

The Pause AI people got their way, and the wall Deep Learning is hitting is regulatory.

Now, let’s much one thing clear: In the Gary Marcus universe, LLMs are still an enormous deal and the economy will still twist itself around them as an infinitely cheap source of poor-quality intellectual labor.

why? Consider the following, which I don’t believe require any real capabilities advance beyond what we’ve at least already seen in demos:

(1) Microsoft Copilot, but it can look through all your documents to find “the notes from one meeting where we made the decision about Project Forestgreen, you know, Steve was there“. This requires cost optimization, but I don’t think even Gary Marcus would disagree with the idea that LLMs are clearly getting cheaper over time and are likely to continue to do so.1

(2) First-line tech support staff entirely replaced by Chatgpt-4o.

(3) AI waifus/husbandos. OpenAI has already demonstrated this with ChatGPT-4o Not-Quite-Scarlett-Johannsen. All the corporate blandness in the world cannot long stem the tide against loneliness, both male and female.

(4) Devin but actually good, which you can get from hooking it up to the compilation output and introducing a thing that can see the screen and do everything the user can do (though depending on how ChatGPT4-o’s vision is when it’s fully rolled out, this might end up requiring a capabilities advance.)

(5) a dating app where an LLM gives you an interview and vibe-checks potential matches so you get a curated list of people that actually seem like good personality fits. I think Meta should do this, actually. They have (1) a shitload of compute with LLM expertise and (2) a shitload of users and (3) a dating website that already has a ton of user data. The ingredients are all there! It just needs someone with vision, and I think that’s something Zuckerberg can actually bring to the table.

(6) RPGs where the characters run off of LLMs and act in contextually appropriate ways. As elsewhere, this wants LLMs to be cheaper than they are (or, alternatively, to run locally, which we’re approaching rapidly with Microsoft’s copilot PCs.)

(7) Automated LLM-based access policy checks, and similar stuff in the “security (observability)” space. Does something have overly-broad permissions? Does someone not in our org have the ability to do stuff on our systems? These are things I classify as “not requiring fundamental advances beyond LLMs just getting cheaper” and that’s *absolutely* going to happen. We can’t trust LLMs yet to act on this knowledge without our say-so, but most of the difficulty in this space is figuring out what the problems are in the first place.

As pointed out by https://situational-awareness.ai/, LLMs are still getting cheaper over time. If LLMs are 100x as cheap as they are now, you unlock lots of latent capabilities like “error checking on every single thing anyone does on a computer.”

Good Investments

  • The AI hyperscalers here— MSFT, GOOG, META— are still great, but in this future we’ll find specific models becoming a commodity, which means what really matters is having sticky customer touchpoints. Because if you own a touchpoint then you can make people pay to have more-convenient LLM-mediated access to the data stored in that customer touchpoint. The Microsoft Copilot model, in other words. Plus, training your own models is hugely expensive and a pain in the ass, and the cloud providers are very well-positioned to vend these out as a service.

  • NVDA is a lot less great here (though i think still pretty good in the short term since companies desperately want inference compute and NVDA is damn near the only game in town at the moment.)

  • AAPL is also quite good since they own one of the biggest customer touchpoints of all. If you’re using AI on your iPhone, it’s going through Apple or someone that has paid Apple to be the LLM-provider.

So How Do We Construct A Portfolio To Account For All Three Futures

I suppose I’m a fan of barbell strategies. That is, you have a tranche of assets dedicated to something “safe”, that is, advantageous in Disaster World. Gold. Short-term bonds (since long-term bonds kinda suck in most non-disaster worlds.) Let’s dedicate half the portfolio to this.

The other half is dedicated to levering-up hyperscalers, who do pretty well if everything doesn’t go to shit. which is to say, if we want to go full degen, it’s gonna be a mix of long-dated call options and LETFs on the hyperscalers and semis supply chain.

It’s almost certainly inefficient in some ways to do this. The reason I organize my portfolio in such a way is this: it ensures that if everything suddenly goes to shit i’m not bankrupt, and the tranche of safe assets gives me the ability to make stupid degenerate bets on the other side (since in my median timelines those stupid degenerate bets actually pay off.)

Sample portfolio, which is kinda close to what i’m doing though for my portfolio has somewhat drifted from this since I am restricted from rebalancing by short-term cap gains. But i think it’s what i endorse for the version of myself unencumbered by US tax rules:

30% short-term treasuries

20% gold

5% TBT (inverse 2x leveraged long-term government bonds)

15% TQQQ (3x QQQ)

5% SOXL (3x SOXX)

15% 2x LETFs: MSFU, GGLL, FBL, AAPL

5% NVDL (2x nvda)

5% long-dated (2.5 year out) call options on a mix of MSFT/GOOG/META/NVDA/TSMC

Net exposure to equities in terms of nominal portfolio value: about 105% (15% * 3 + 30% * 2 = 105%) (not including TSMC call options since effective leverage depends on strike price but it’s gonna be similar to between 3x and 10x)

FAQs

“Wait, why are you doing both short-term treasuries and LETFs? You’re paying more for the leverage than you’re getting from the treasuries.“

It might be a little bit inefficient. LETFs have high expense ratios— mostly hovering around 1%-1.25%— and also charge LIBOR, so in a sense it’s strictly more expensive for me to be doing it this way.

The main advantage is that in extreme bear case scenarios— say, the market goes down 10% per day for 10 days— LETFs perform better than putting a similar amount of money in the underlying ETF. (It’s basically the flip side of the math around volatility decay— there’s a sense in which if you’d had LETFs around during the big dot-com bubble burst in the early 2000s, doing a barbell strategy as discussed above would actually have saved you a significant sum because instead of losing 80% of your portfolio you’ve now lost 40% or so.)

Because my bear cases for this strategy in large part revolve around these extreme scenarios, especially war, I’m very interested in this property. There’s probably a more-principled way of thinking about this.

It’s possible the true advantage is that this gives me the courage to pull this ridiculous bullshit at all, since I’m very aware of black swan-type portfolio risks and the left hand of the barbell being ironclad makes me feel much much better about taking big swings on the other side.

“Why no commodities?”

They introduce a lot of portfolio drag in a way that gold mostly doesn’t, which I noticed messing around with portfolio visualizer and some longer-dated backtests with a bogleheads excel spreadsheet.

“What do you think about call options vs LETFs?”

There’s a sense in which call options are just another form of leverage, but where the cost you’re paying is in theta decay. This is universally going to be a much higher cost of leverage than you’re paying via leveraged ETFs or for IBKR portfolio margin.

In return, you get a form of leverage where the downside is strictly capped. Which is why degenerate retail investors love call options!

It’s trivially true that in any case other than the one where the underlying asset tanks, you would have been much happier as an investor using LETFs or portfolio margin for your leverage instead of call options. If you leveraged NVDA up 1.5x using portfolio margin, and your friend leveraged up 1.5x using call options, then your friend will at the end of this have earned less money than you because he will have paid a lot more money for access to that same amount of leverage.

Of course, on the flip side, if NVDA goes down to 0 overnight because the whole enterprise was a fraud (it’s this whole meme on Twitter), you are now fending off angry creditors whereas your friend is just down to 0 dollars.

What the hell do you mean, ‘leverage 1.5x using call options?‘ That’s not a thing.

I think it’s a reasonable approximation of the truth. Call options grant you a non-recourse form of leverage, that is, exposing yourself to some multiple of price fluctuations in the underlying asset, based on the ratio of option price to underlying asset price. I think the math around this isn’t actually too bad, but i wanna save that for another blog post.

If LLMs were going to be a huge deal in terms of business, shouldn’t that have happened by now?

This stuff takes a *long* time to build. Microsoft Copilot still kinda sucks, in my opinion; what we really need is to have it be able to treat everything you’ve done in teams/email/etc/etc as a big blob of text it’s allowed to search through, and it can’t do that. Yet.

It also needs a better-grained permissions model; when I ask copilot to tell me stuff I want it to have access to *all* the information I have stored within the microsoft cloud, including stuff written by other people but which I have access to.

But once it can, i predict the value add will be *insane*. Microsoft will make insane money off that. Can you imagine the returns to “what team is working on X in Y org?“

Why leveraged inverse government bonds?

It’s a hedge for rising interest rates— my portfolio is heavily exposed to big tech (OBVIOUSLY) and big tech tends to do very well when interest rates are lowered and very poorly when they are raised. I have no specific views on whether interest rates are likely to go up or down; but since their going up craters my portfolio I desire a hedge. I consider TBT to join gold and short-term bonds as portfolio ballast— it’s not *nearly* as volatile as the rest of my portfolio.

Why gold?

It’s a hedge against inflation, but mostly it joins short-term government bonds as portfolio ballast.

Why long-dated and not short-dated call options?

I suspect (please god someone who knows what they’re doing help me out here) that short-dated call options are going to get you generally-worse deals because short-dated call option prices should reflect non-public knowledge more than long-dated call options, because just in general it’s much much easier to reason about short-term price fluctuations in response to secret information than long-term price fluctuations.

Because my theses are all very long-term it seems natural to use long-dated options to express these viewpoints.
