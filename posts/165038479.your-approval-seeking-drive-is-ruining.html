A Very Fake Framework Of The Mind

This is 100% dumb and fake but it’s dumb and fake in a useful way. So:

Your mind is segmented into two chunks. Most people prefer to lean to one half over the other in dealing with life.

Strategy Brain

Strategy Brain, which is very verbal and goal-oriented, is what you (ideally) are putting in charge of your finances and various material life goals. If you’re playing a card game, Strategy Brain is what you engage to win it; Strategy Brain puts together contingency plans and logical arguments.

Lizard Brain

Then you have Lizard Brain. Lizard brain is nonverbal and vibes-based, and Lizard Brain is what you use to do low-latency stuff like “making conversation”. It also, crucially, is responsible for most of human social interaction. Humans are masters of reading microexpressions to divine what other people are thinking. When you see someone uncomfortable who clearly wants to leave a conversation, or you see someone pissed off about to punch someone, Lizard Brain is feeding you this information through some blended aggregate of body posture, facial expression, and tone of voice.

The Approval-Seeking Drive

I believe the root of ~all social anxiety is the doomed attempt to micromanage other peoples’ emotional state in-the-moment as you are speaking to them. Mostly, this is in service of making people like you (or at least not hate you).

First problem: It is impossible to guarantee that someone will like you. It is also impossible to guarantee someone is going to be comfortable and having fun for all parts of a conversation.

Obviously.

But not only that! If you have set as your consciously-held Strategy-Brain goal “I want this person to like me”, then not only is that goal impossible, but it is equally impossible to tell if you have succeeded in this goal. People hide their emotions sometimes! People often show emotions that have nothing to do with you!

That means if your barometer for success for an interaction has anything to do with the other person’s emotional state, and if you are a temperament where you’re prone to thinking about how to achieve this reliably (common in people with a Strategy Brain that’s dominant over Lizard Brain, aka nerds), and if you consider it a total disaster for someone to hate the interaction, then you will peg your internal CPU at 100% almost immediately and you will suffer for it.

Put this way it’s all pretty obvious, right? The experience of social anxiety reported by almost everyone is a constant stressful internal monologue of shit like:

  • Maybe I’m sitting weird. I should take up less space. Or possibly more space.

  • Oh god people will think it’s cringe that I’m sitting here not talking to anyone. I should talk to those people!

  • Wait shit those people are in a group conversation. I want to join but what if it’s a private conversation? What if they want me to go? Or worse, what if they secretly hate me and I’m just standing there being cringe?

And the times when it’s worst are ones where either you don’t know the people at all (maximum uncertainty) or, famously, on dates with people you really like (maximum stakes).

This is the Approval-Seeking Drive. It is utterly destructive to both your natural charm and your ability to enjoy unstructured socializing.

Supporting Evidence

  • Famously, nerds hate parties (unstructured places where this process makes their lives a living hell) and prefer much more structured socializing: D&D games, or board games. Which makes sense: if the root of social anxiety is strategy brain spinning forever trying to compute the nonexistent path to 100% approval, the most straightforward way of killing that process is by giving yourself something to do that’s totally unrelated. If you’re completely occupied by trying to figure out how to win your game of Magic: The Gathering, then you’re not freaking the fuck out about how to make this person sitting across from you like you.

  • Social Anxiety Inducing: giving a presentation to a massive audience on a cruise ship. Not Social Anxiety Inducing: getting up on stage in front of a massive audience in a cruise ship, because you need tell them that the ship is heading for an iceberg and they need to get the fuck to the lifeboats right now. Consider: In precisely one of those situations are you thinking about what the audience thinks of you personally.

  • Giving job interviews to candidates is not anxiety-inducing. Being interviewed is anxiety-inducing. In precisely one of those situations do you care about what the other person thinks of you.

  • Engineers almost never report design reviews as being anxiety-inducing. That’s because the engineer’s conscious intention going into the design review is not “make these people like me” but instead “I need to make this design function as intended.”

What To Do About It

I have taken this theory and mostly apply it as two different interventions on myself, applied whenever I feel nervous about social stuff:

“Abandon All Hope And Embrace Social Death” Intervention

I keep very close track of when I am feeling anxious in social events. Any time I do, I go through the following explicit chain of thought:

  1. Notice who I am afraid of disliking me.

  2. Imagine the worst-case scenario. Imagine the dismissive glances or awkward silence. In my heart, embrace this outcome as acceptable. In a tiny way, embrace social death; let go of the desire to be liked by this person.

  3. Imagine what I would do if this worst-case scenario didn’t matter.

  4. Know, in my heart, that I am not trying to avoid this worst-case scenario.

  5. Do whatever thing, safe in the knowledge that if I am hated this is fine.

“Intention-Setting” Intervention

I find it also helps to build in explicit goals for my social interactions that do not involve the other person liking me. A north star that guides my behavior, entirely independent of what the other person might or might not like.

I use the following north stars to dictate what i will say or do next:

(A) Vulnerable Honesty. Say exactly the thing that I’m thinking about in exactly the terms I’m thinking about it; do not make any attempt at shading the truth or obfuscating things I suspect will be embarrassing. If I think this will make the person think less of me, that is fine because the goal is not to have my conversation partner think anything in particular about me. I use Kindness as a specific concrete filter here; if I could say something true but I know it would really stress the other person out for no good reason, I will omit it because I’m not a monster.

(B) Selfish Impulse. Ask myself, silently, what deep down I would like to say or do next, if anything. Silence is an acceptable option. Then I ask “is there any really good reason to not do or say this thing.” Important Note: “They will think less of me” or “they might feel awkward about it” is not considered a legitimate reason to not do the thing.

I’ve noticed that the higher-stakes an interaction is the more I have to rely on Vulnerable Honesty in preference to Selfish Impulse, because the higher the stakes are the harder it is to notice what my selfish impulses even are. At this point, the way i ask girls on dates is specifically by cutting out literally any attempt at subtlety or derisking and just saying something along the lines of “I think you are extremely cool and I would love to take you out to karaoke”, then wait for a response. Because that shit’s nerve-wracking! It is deeply soothing to simply say true facts in any particular order and allow the other person to interact with those true facts in any way they choose.

Wait, isn’t not caring if the other person feels awkward kind of sociopathic?

Short answer: Kinda!

Long answer: social awkwardness isn’t that bad and, separately, cannot be avoided. Awkwardness is a state created in the other person’s mind for a long string of reasons, many of which cannot be predicted by you in advance. If attempting to stop people from feeling awkward imposes large costs on yourself— which I would argue that it does, if you are anxious in this way— you don’t have to do it. So don’t!

Even longer answer: people enjoy feeling valued. Indicating you like someone and want to spend time around them is a brutal double-edged sword because if they’re into it they will feel pumped about you saying this, whereas if they’re not they will feel awkward. Expected value is high but you cannot de-risk this dynamic and will hurt yourself trying; injecting a tiny bit of sociopathy into your psyche is how one does this.

But I want people to like me! I don’t want to be cringe!

Same!

There’s a shitty zenlike quality to all this where people will like you a lot better, but only after you shove your approval-seeking drive into a box and cut it off from ever influencing any of your behaviors. Only then will you be maximally charismatic and, by extension, generally well-liked.

It is what it is!

Why I Came To This Conclusion

For nearly all of my adult life I’ve felt a deep, visceral fear of talking to attractive women. After I quit my job at Microsoft, I dec ided I wanted to do something concrete about that instead of just hoping that someday I would feel differently.

I’ve been doing, for the last year, a bootcamp thing called The Attractive Man. It’s basically… well, I would call it a pickup artist workshop but it does away with the shitty and dishonest parts of PUA culture and attempts to distill it down into “how to be attractive as a man in ways that meet the bars of kindness and honesty.”

Some of what we did was just hitting on random girls in nightclubs and such, which was mildly useful— this basically just amounts to exposure therapy on romantic rejection, though on a couple delightful occasions I did end up making out with hot nightclub girls, which was fun.

But the really transformative thing that happened to me there was this workshop called Connecting With Women. It was a long series of escalatingly-intense intimacy exercises, the very first of which was literally Lynn (the coach) asking me to get up in front of a bunch of extremely pretty girls and select one that’s hot. Which I did. And then Lynn asked me, placidly, in front of all the assembled guys and girls: “Aaron, can you tell me what’s hot about her?”

I froze.

The possible responses that came to mind— her tits are amazing! Legs! The curve of her body!— were sexual and I could feel myself rejecting them immediately after thinking of them. I sputtered for a bit and eventually arrived, victoriously, at a conclusion that seemed vaguely-truthful-ish and also completely desexualized: her teeth. She had really nice teeth.

Lynn was… displeased. “Aaron. That was bullshit. You clearly were not thinking about her teeth. Sit back down and think about what you just said.”

So (after a bit of pointless argument about whether this was actually bullshit) I sat back down. And actually, I felt extremely put upon. “What the fuck did Lynn want me to say? That she was hot because had great tits and a tight dress?” And immediately after the thought hit me I realized that for god’s sake, yes. That was exactly what Lynn wanted me to say because it was the maximally honest answer. Lynn isn’t the fucking Theban Sphinx and she was not attempting to pose me an impossible riddle. She was asking a straightforward question with an easy, top of mind answer: “she is hot because she has great tits and she’s in a tight dress.”

So (I thought) why didn’t I just say that? Why did I give this other, bizarre answer that was obviously not the true one?

And the answer I came to, in the middle of the workshop, was that I had discarded the correct answer because on some fundamental level I believed that it would cause the girls to dislike me. I have a model in my head of socially appropriate behavior and every single truthful answer I could come up with hit smack against the wall of “that would be weird for the girls and cause them to hate me.”

And immediately after that thought formed I realized that the girls liking me isn’t the point of the workshop. Lynn would never ask us to say anything to make a girl like us; her whole shpiel is about ditching people-pleasing. And obviously my censoring the true answer wasn’t about the models’ comfort. The models have done a ton of these workshops! For God’s sake, they were being paid to be there! They know what they are getting into and my saying the word “tits” in response to a direct question is not going to be a traumatic experience for them!

Then I realized that this was literally the first exercise of the first day of the workshop. And that if I continued setting all of my behavior against the bar of “saying things to make the girls like me”, and letting that direct my behavior, then the entire workshop was just going to be an embarrassing shitshow from which i would gain nothing.

So I decided to try, as best I could, to simply… let the girls hate me. To just abandon hope that they would think I was hot or whatever and instead try and, as much as possible, simply say mortifying but true statements. To actually just accept social death, to embrace cringe as inevitable. To understand, in my heart of hearts, that I was going to say a bunch of deeply unsafe and unappealing things and the girls would probably hate me for it. And that was hard. Because my every social instinct was, at the time, geared toward being blandly likeable.

So I accepted social death and started just saying true things completely without regard for how I thought it would be taken. And I kept doing that. For the whole three day workshop, I tried as hard as I could to simply suppress any thoughts of how I was being perceived. To come up with incredibly simple, incredibly mechanical rules for what I could say and why so that I wouldn’t just lapse back into my default behavior of “say people-pleasing bullshit.” These rules included:

(1) If I had a thought that was true and vulnerable, especially about attraction, regarding one of the girls i had to say it at earliest opportunity. A longstanding crush? Say it! Thoughts re: cleavage? SPEAK MY TRUTH.

(2) If I was like “wait aaron that thought is super cringe” then i double have to say it

(3) No derisking allowed. If the girls asked a question that had an embarrassing answer I would simply give the true answer without embellishment and embrace their hatred.

And while i could adhere to these rules I was in a state of zenlike calm, saying and doing insanely bold and frankly socially unacceptable shit (at one point during an exercise, unsolicited, I picked up a girl and joyfully twirled her around) with utter tranquility. The thought was like a talisman: “the girls are allowed to hate me and are allowed to hate what I say. I am doing the workshop. My role is to say true facts and act on impulse, unapologetically, without hedging. If I have done this I have succeeded and if I am chewed out later this is fine. If the girls declare me The Worst that is fine. What matters is the experience of saying true and vulnerable things, because I will never see any of them again.”

But I… wasn’t chewed out. Bafflingly, impossibly, the girls fucking loved it. Like, before that I could tell I had bored them, that they found my presence in some undefinable way slightly annoying, but now that I wasn’t trying to make them like me, whenever I spoke or even approached them their eyes lit up and I had (bafflingly, impossibly) their obvious adoration. What the actual fuck.

The models, at the end of the workshop, had the unanimous opinion that before this particular private mindset shift, I was very unsexy and timid and unimpressive, and afterward I was hot as hell.

And it was obviously not a coincidence that this change coincided directly with the utter tranquility and joyful ease that came of no longer trying to alter my behavior to suit the girls’ imagined preferences.

Anyway, after the three-day workshop I went home and… immediately regressed to worrying excessively about everyone’s opinions of me any time I was in a room with strangers.

What I’ve been trying to do— actually with a lot of success!— since that workshop was to reverse-engineer what exactly happened there into a thing I could apply to every single other part of my social life. Which was hard, conceptually— the nice thing about the workshop was that there are no consequences for anything you do, and because the girls know what’s up you don’t have to worry about really stressing anyone out. I don’t actually comment on girls’ tits in real life.

So I’ve been trying to crack how exactly I can go about stopping myself from contorting myself to suit peoples’ imagined preferences about me. And mostly this involves being mindful of when I am tempted to try and improve someone’s opinion of me (and then to not do that). This falls just short enough of fully actionable that I’m not really satisfied with it, but it does work just well enough that a very nice lesbian at LessOnline commented, unsolicited after a ten minute interaction, that I seemed way sexier and more at-ease than last year when I had met her last.

I would not say I’ve completely lost all social fear, but I no longer identify as “socially anxious”, particularly, and I no longer studiously avoid interactions with pretty girls. This is a huge life improvement and I highly recommend it.
