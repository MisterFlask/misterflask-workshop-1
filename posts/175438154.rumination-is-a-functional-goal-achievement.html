Rumination and worry— which is to say, pondering at length something that happened in the past (or that may happen in the future) while you figure out how whether your behaviors should have been different— is, in many ways, like a neural network running multiple epochs over its training data: your brain wants to wring as much insight as possible out of past events because events are quite scarce. Likewise, your brain attempts to generate synthetic data on future events for the same reason by running simulations of possible scenarios and their likely outcomes, then backpropagating on the results of those simulations.

These worry/rumination processes can and do kick in automatically any time you have set yourself a goal. Your brain is a goal-achievement machine and conceptualizing rumination as a well-oiled part of that machine is instructive.

What Is Happening During Unpleasant Rumination Activities

Your rumination subprocess has booted up and suspects: “You did something cringe at that party. Your goal was to be liked universally at all times. You are FAILING AT YOUR GOAL.” Now it is running deeply unpleasant epochs over your sad training data in order to generate learning. This is not an accident; this is (a part of) how learning works.

Unfortunately, this is basically self-torture. You’re running epochs over a deeply unpleasant memory, and the way people want to intuitively defuse this is by challenging their memory directly:

“I’m sure it wasn’t as cringe as all that.”

But your brain’s helpful ruminative subprocess interprets your objections as

The central insight of metacognitive therapy is that engagement of any kind with ruminative thoughts just fuels the rumination; challenging thoughts directly with responses like “I’m sure it wasn’t that bad because xyz” (as is traditional in cognitive-behavioral therapy) can be actively counterproductive for that reason.

Clippy is your cheerful, industrious, never-gives-up partner in achieving whatever goals are most important to you. Which of course brings us to a secondary problem:

“Clippy. Clippy, no. I don’t actually care what these people think of me. Clippy please”

You can’t gaslight Clippy. Clippy knows. Clippy has behavioral log access.

Why Social Anxiety Rumination Seems More Prevalent Than Other Types

Your ruminative subprocess, if you have the goal “be universally beloved” or any of its variants, needs to know if you accomplished your goal. And you do not read minds; “be universally beloved” is a goal relating to other peoples’ unknowable internal states.

So Clippy will spin forever figuring out if you actually said something weird at the party because “weird” isn’t a judgment about reality, it is an unfalsifiable hypothesis about someone else’s memories of the party.

Clippy wants to help you so badly, but you have given him a doomed task and he could spend forever on it and not make progress. And oh, he will.

Maybe Goal-Reorientation Kills Rumination About Mortifying Social Events?

This is how it’s worked for me, anyway.

When I implement goal-reorientation behaviorally—when Clippy’s logs show I’ve implemented behavioral protocols where “avoid embarrassment” is not an objective— the rumination stops. Clippy looks at my behavioral data and updates: “Oh, we’re not optimizing for ‘universal approval’ anymore? I guess there’s not really any point to going over these incident reports, then.”

The rumination about that mortifying party incident becomes mechanically pointless. There’s no failure to analyze because I wasn’t pursuing the “avoid embarrassment” goal. The training data becomes irrelevant.

This is, at least, what I have observed in myself; “it wasn’t that bad” has never worked for me, and nor has “nobody cares about my random social faux pas.” What has worked for me is “I am not obligated to analyze whether [incident] was embarrassing from any particular perspective,” and this started working for me after I adopted “honesty about internal state > > impression management” as a general social philosophy. It’s possible that it would have worked before then, but I doubt it; I suspect strongly that you cannot lie to Clippy, and if I had tried to do this pre-framework I would have failed for that reason.

Mull it over.
