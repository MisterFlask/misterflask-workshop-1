A couple weeks back I was riding in a moving van with a friendly acquaintance that was switching apartments. We chatted for a while about our lives, and then lapsed into silence, and then I realized I was sleepy.

I suddenly became very aware that I should be more entertaining than “guy who falls asleep in a moving van,” and I started thinking: “What conversation do I make? What do I ask about? Is there a specific train of thought we haven’t gone down yet? Am I discharging my conversational duties acceptably if I say X rather than Y?”

I noticed I was getting a bit stressed out and thought: “Wait. What would I do right now if literally nothing mattered, and I could do as I pleased?”

And the answer came: “I want to doze in the passenger’s seat until we arrive at our destination.”

And I asked: “Is there any REAL reason I can’t do that?”

And the answer came: “He might get bored. Can we accept this?”

And I responded: “Yes.”

And then I dozed in the passenger seat for twenty minutes. It was great.

Anxiety Is A Control Loop, Fear Is A Feeling

Fear is a STATE. Anxiety is an ACTION. The fear was just the input signal to kick off the loop: “He might think I’m boring” or “I’m failing at being a good passenger.” But the anxiety was the entire active process that kicked in to handle that signal:

  • Monitor reactions and the silence

  • Generate possible conversation topics

  • Evaluate each option (”is this interesting enough?”)

  • Execute whatever behavior

  • Monitor the results

  • Repeat

Anxiety is the felt experience of running that loop. It’s not the fear itself— “I was insufficiently entertaining while I was literally helping somebody move their furniture” won’t lose me any sleep at night. Rather, it’s the ruminative process of trying to figure out how to fix the thing you’re worried about.

How To Notice

Once you understand anxiety as a control loop, if you notice you’re anxious it becomes possible to observe what your loop is actually optimizing for. Every single time you’re socially anxious, you should be able to trace it back to three distinct components:

(1) THE TARGET: The specific person (or people) you are trying to make feel a particular way about you. (This is also allowed to be a diffuse crowd of people, as is common at parties.)

(2) THE FEAR: The specific concerns you have about how you might or might not be perceived, or what might or might not happen.

(3) THE RESPONSE: The specific methods you’re thinking about employing to make this person (or people) feel a particular way about you.

In the van: TARGET was my friend, FEAR was “he’ll think I’m boring/bad company,” RESPONSE was “generate entertaining conversation.” Once I made these explicit, I could ask: “Do I actually endorse running this optimization?” And the answer was no.

Maybe This Extends To Anxiety In General?

Maybe all anxiety is ruminative mental control loops where either the monitoring or actuator part is broken— where either you cannot verify success or can’t ensure a successful outcome in the first place. “I Don’t Know What To Do (But I’m Trying To Figure It Out)” is basically the slogan of anxiety as a fully-general category.

I Suspect Real Catastrophic Thinking Is Actually Pretty Uncommon

A thing I don’t have to do is explicitly persuade myself that any particular social failure is catastrophic to my goals and life. I suspect this is the norm— that most socially anxious people aren’t walking around thinking “if I’m boring, everyone will abandon me and I’ll die alone.” They’re just… trying not to be boring. It’s a background task, like keeping your balance while walking.

Like, the actual van thought process was basically:

  • [Silence in van]

  • [Automatic subroutine: GENERATE CONVERSATION]

  • [Shit, what do I talk about?]

  • [ANXIETY SPIKE]

Social anxiety for me is less about explicit catastrophic fears and more just a neverending series of thankless tasks that I feel obligated to perform for no obvious reason.

Other Kinds Of Pointless Tasks I Have To Consciously Ban Myself From Taking

On Dates

“I am obligated to be witty and charming at all times.”

  • TEMPTATION: To be witty and charming (alas, this cannot be summoned at-will)

  • BUT ACTUALLY: I ain’t gotta say shit.

“As the man it is my job to have a plan, and to ensure that everything goes according to that plan.”

  • TEMPTATION: To build enormous chains of contingency plans (“if the restaurant is full we’ll…”)

  • BUT ACTUALLY: if something goes wrong in date logistics we’ll figure out some alternative, together, like adults.

At Parties

“If someone finds me alone and not talking with anyone they’ll think I’m weird. Therefore if I want to be alone I should go use the restroom where that’s normal.” (Hanging out with any animals around is also an acceptable solution.)

  • TEMPTATION: Find an animal, look at books on the shelf, get on the phone.

  • BUT ACTUALLY: Just sit on a couch in the common area. Make aggressive eye contact. If necessary, wink.

In 1:1 Conversation

“It is my job to ensure that conversation partners feel engaged and happy at all times”

  • TEMPTATION: Read microexpressions continuously to try and divine whether they want to leave and if I should be saying something else.

  • BUT ACTUALLY: It’s my responsibility to be generally considerate and to not trespass stated boundaries. Others are allowed to feel bored.

(see also: Mutually Assured Consideration)

I classify myself as being not meaningfully socially anxious anymore, but I actually go through incidents like the van one pretty frequently. The way “no longer being anxious” actually cashes out IRL is this: any time I feel anxiety I can trace it back to a thankless job that I have accidentally given myself, and after I figure out what the job is, 95% of the time I just kill it because it’s not genuinely useful.
