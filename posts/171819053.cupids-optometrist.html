Problem:

You've discovered something terrible about the architecture of romantic desire. There's a lens you sometimes wear — let’s call it The Hunter's Monocle— that makes everything instrumental. The lens has a visual overlay in the upper-right corner of your vision, flickering with questions: "Are we winning? Are we closer? Did she like the joke?"

Regrettably, the lens stinks. Literally. It emits a pheromone of neediness and desperation that humans can detect at parts-per-billion. Wearing it makes you a salesman, and people hate being sold to.

The lens is also sticky. Grafted to your face. You can't just rip it off; that'd like trying not to think of a pink elephant while actively pursuing a pink elephant. The harder you try to remove the lens, the more it adheres.

Meanwhile, there's another lens— The Fool's Prism— where you're just some guy eating ice cream and talking about prediction markets and speculating about whether crows hold grudges (you’re pretty sure they do). The Fool’s Prism has no goals and attaches itself to no outcomes. This lens makes you funny and charming and magnetic. But the moment you notice romantic potential, you reflexively reach for the Hunter's Monocle, and suddenly you're back to being a used car salesman and you, personally, are the used car.

You find this whole dynamic deeply annoying.

Solution:

Wait. Shit. Wait.

There's a third lens you've discovered, hidden in your jacket pocket this whole time: The Documentarian's Glass.

This lens doesn't pursue. It doesn't hide. It simply reports: "I'm attracted to you." Said the same way you'd say "I noticed three crows on the powerline" or "This coffee is slightly burnt." It's not trying to achieve any specific outcome. It's just committed to accurate self-reporting, like a war correspondent from the country of your own experience.

This lens doesn't smell like desperation. It smells like nothing. It's clean. Through it, you might say: "I’ve been scheming about how to make out with you for the last half hour" or "I'm deciding whether to hit on you"— not as chess moves, but as field notes from your own consciousness.

This only works because the Documentarian’s Glass isn't a replacement lens. It works like those funny-looking clip-on sunglasses made for people that wear regular glasses. It fits right over the Monocle. You don't stop wanting. You don't stop the strategic thoughts. You just... observe them, and speak them aloud with as little embellishment as possible, and you watch to see what reality does with this utterance. You will notice the Hunter’s Monocle visual overlay start sending you messages like “this is a terrible idea” and “this will ruin everything;” these messages might even be correct. This is fine and expected.

The magic of the Documentarian’s Glass is that it collapses the paradox entirely. You're neither pursuing nor not-pursuing. You're just accurately existing in front of another person. Whatever happens with that information is almost... not your business? You're the reporter of reality, not its editor.

The Hunter wants. The Fool does not want. The Documentarian just watches the wanting and takes notes, then shares the data with scientific dispassion.

Utterances while wearing the Documentarian’s Glass, such as “I’m probably going to hit on you later” or “your cleavage is absolutely destroying me right now,” elicit responses that are suspiciously more positive than you think they should be. Somehow, people seem to confuse your newfound eyewear with deep confidence and utter self-assuredness.

You’re not sure: is there a difference?

Does it matter if there is?
