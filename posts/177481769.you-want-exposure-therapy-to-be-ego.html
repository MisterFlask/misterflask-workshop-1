There exists a monastery where the monks eat leaves; a botanist friend tells you they are of a rather potent variety of poison ivy. The monks eat them every single day. They have been doing this for years. They plan to do this until they die.

When you ask them why, they give you a strange look. “Because we’re the Poison-Eaters,” they say, as if this explains everything.

“So… like, you do it for long enough that you develop an immunity?” you ask.

“Not really,” they admit. “Well, maybe a little. But that’s not the point.”

“Then what is the point?”

The head monk considers this.

“We used to visit the poison. Once a week, when the abbot made us. We’d hold our breath, eat it fast. I’d always run away gagging, personally. It was horrible. We hated every second. But we kept having to come back.”

“So what changed?”

“The monastery had a schism and a group of us moved in here permanently. Built houses here. Started a garden. After a while, we stopped being people-who-occasionally-eat-poison-because-the-abbot-says-so. We became Poison-Eaters. It’s our whole thing now. We’re kind of famous for it in the region.”

“So… now you enjoy it?”

“No,” the monk states serenely. “No, we still hate it. But now we’re doing it as ourselves. We’re doing it because we’re the kind of people who do this. Does that make sense?”

No. No, it doesn’t make sense.

Not even a little bit.

Okay, so I’ve talked a lot about the nature of social anxiety and the ways different therapeutic modalities perceive the core ontology of the problem, and my own slightly-divergent view. Namely, this thing:

Exposure and Response Prevention Therapy as traditionally performed has the following set of fairly core problems when applied to social anxiety:

(1) The core compulsion to be prevented is reactive impression management (trying to make someone specific not-disapprove of you in-the-moment), writ large.

Unfortunately,

(2) It’s really hard to operationalize this in an effective way, which results in patients white-knuckling through exposure therapy. The white-knuckling is a 100% indicator that they’re still attempting to impression-manage in all the ways researchers didn’t think to ban; which is to say, they’re doing exposure therapy and not preventing the response which does not work for any OCD-spectrum disorder.

(3) Social anxiety exposure therapy is not ego-syntonic— that is to say, it’s not the kind of thing patients are going to feel good about doing in-the-moment, or proud about having done. One does not cultivate an identity as “the kind of person who’s doing exposure therapy;” it’s a regrettable necessity, discarded as soon as the problem is resolved. This same dynamic applies to therapy more-generally: people who take pride in being “the kind of person who goes to therapy” are, as one might expect, way more likely to keep doing the therapy.

Social mishap exposure therapy— where patients self-sabotage their social standing in tiny ways, like spilling a drink or pausing mid-presentation— work okay, but these are both very weak exposures (most people don’t obsessively worry about drink-spilling) and also are deeply ego-dystonic: nobody wants to cultivate an identity as “the guy who spills drinks” or “the guy who can’t do presentations.”

Ego-Syntonic Habits Stick Because They Become Part Of Your Identity

I’m still in contact with a guy from the dating workshop thing (“The Attractive Man”) I was doing last year. The dating workshop did a lot of community engineering-type stuff— attempting to build up a mutually-supportive web of practitioners who all reinforce practicing romantic boldness with one another.

Not insane, on the face of it! However, The Attractive Man was specifically very, very interested in “cold approaches performed per week” as the core metric measuring (not in so many words) moral worth and general deservingness. As a consequence, lots of guys in the program built self-images on top of “I’m the kind of guy that cold-approaches women in public”; this got wrapped up in self-identities like “I am a man who goes for what he wants in the world” and “I am a man who’s not afraid of rejection.”

The guys who ended up with this as part of their self-image did a lot of cold approaches. Not because the cold approaches became pleasant or even particularly effective— relatively few men in the program ever started genuinely enjoying this process, as far as I’m aware, including the men who did an absolute shitload of them— but because they felt good afterwards about being the kind of person who did this intrinsically unrewarding grind-y task. This reminds me of a thing I heard once about concert musicians: ones who do a shitload of practicing don’t necessarily enjoy it much, but they do have a self-image as a Serious Musician Who Does A Lot Of Practice.

(I, personally, hated cold-approaching and while it was probably useful for me as conclusive evidence that “I can ask girls out and the world won’t explode if I’m rejected” it was still kinda unpleasant for all involved; I’ve probably done a grand total of maybe like 25 and I’m good now, thanks. I also don’t think cold approaches are a great way of meeting compatible people. But that’s a different article.)

This is an existence proof: one can build even highly unpleasant activities into your identity and correspondingly into your lifestyle. The trick is finding activities to integrate that result in your life being better instead of worse.

So if one is to integrate exposure and response therapy into your lifestyle, I think you need two things to be true: First, that the practice of exposure not be miserable more than, say, 30% of the time; and second, that it be a thing you can roll into your identity rather than being a random annoying chore you’re doing for an outcome.

If you can get both of these you have a habit that can be stuck with permanently; if you can get one of them then you have something that maybe you can do for a while, and if you get none of them then I suspect you will find it remarkably draining to do the exposure more than a handful of times before burning out.

I think this kind of exposure can exist for social anxiety, you just have to be clever about it.

Weaponized Straight-Shooterism As Ego-Syntonic Therapeutic Protocol

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy is very, very interested in patients picking a virtue or a value they intend to orient their life around. Patients then (as I understand it) start performing small, concrete actions to instantiate that value in their life. ACT is actually quite value-agnostic— whether you pick a value of community or friendship or honesty or growth or service, the idea is to help the patient orient their life around that. Which is great!

I’m doing something a bit more specific, though. It is not a coincidence that my jerry-rigged anti-social-anxiety protocols have self-disclosure of moderately embarrassing truths as a central feature. Because if my theory of social anxiety is correct, then the value chosen is going to be at its most useful if it can act as a counterbalance to the impulse to reactively manage impressions. And the virtue that is most deeply hostile to impression management is radical honesty.

Think about it— friendships, benevolence and community can all lend themselves to people-pleasing but radical honesty explicitly rejects people-pleasing any time it runs counter to telling the full, unvarnished truth about your internal state. Which will be often!

If the goal is to batter down the impression-management compulsion, therefore, radical-honesty-as-motivating-drive probably can’t be beat as an explicitly-held value to integrate exposure and response prevention as a permanent feature of daily life. Consider the two requirements we had above for sustainable ERP:

(1) It can’t be miserable more than 30% of the time. I think “bounded radical honesty” (tempered by kindness and not-being-a-dick) is actually quite rewarding. I’ve told a lot of random normies about my dating workshop adventures and Slutcon and weird theories and every so often I’ll get the side-eye, but most of the time people are just curious. When I tell people in my dance studio about why I got into dance— namely, to attract a girl I like by way of getting really fucking good at salsa— there’s often this little BSOD that lasts for maybe a second where they’re like “oh, are we just saying this stuff now? Is that sayable?”

(And then, if it’s a guy and we’re one on one, he’ll be like oh yeah same here. In groups, people are just entertained, I think.)

Lobbing little prosocial truth-grenades gets to be intrinsically rewarding quickly, the moreso if you can lob them with a cheeky grin and total shamelessness. And as a side bonus you feel a little bit closer to people every time you tell them true, unpolished facts about your internal state instead of trying to people-please everywhere.

(2) It should be something you can roll into a positive identity that you’re proud of having.

You know who was really into honesty? Who explicitly spat on palatability and peoples’ opinions of you?

Punk rockers. Hippies. Specific kinds of self-styled rugged cowboy.

We already have existence proofs, multiple, that not just individual people but entire subcultures will build themselves around unpalatable honesty as a core value. “Guy who is a straight shooter that says what he thinks” is an identity that if you take it on you can feel genuinely proud of, and it’s also an identity that other people will also (often but not always) approve of you having. And this identity runs directly through “saying things that are unpalatable because they are true”, and each time you do this you are effectively running exposure and response therapy: exposure to “possible judgement of others”, where the response being prevented is “softening this true thing in the hopes they might like you more.”

Exposure Therapy As Lifestyle

When I was delivering my confidence engineering talk, one of the guys in the audience asked how long I needed to use the '“vulnerable honesty/kindness/selfishness” fallback personality until I stopped needing it, and my answer was “actually that’s kinda just who I am now.” On reflection, I think that was about half-true— I notice I’m anxious and then have to let go of the compulsion to manage the interaction (swapping to the fallback personality) briefly up to several times per conversation. Works great!

This probably works best in social circles (rationalism being the one I know best) that aren’t super exclusion-happy; probably a reasonable way to operationalize this for other circles is “be uncomfortably honest in ways that won’t invite definite social exclusion.” Hypocrisy? Absolutely. Remember, kids, the only non-hypocrites are the dead.

FAQs

Do you have any convincing reason why “say the true thing with a cheeky grin and shamelessness” isn’t just impression management with extra steps?

This is a definitional question, and like all definitional questions it is best answered by reframing away from the term being argued over.

First, let’s go back to why impression-management is crazy-making in the first place: the impossibility and unverifiability of “I want to make this other person have specific reactions to me.” If you’ve quashed “make this person like me” by replacing it with the goal of “perform the role of a guy who’s authentic”, intrinsically this is highly bounded as a computation because it doesn’t rely on other people responding in a specific way; you’re playing a role, not trying to get a specific reaction. (Relatedly, I have a delightful story about hitting on girls while roleplaying as a heavily-Russian-accented D&D character which these margins are far too narrow to contain.)

Which is to say, the crucial thing is for the existence of social feedback to be functionally separated from the in-the-moment decisionmaking protocol.

Second: consider the structure of exposure and response prevention therapy. The exposure here is exposure to negative judgment, and the response prevented is shading the truth in an attempt to avoid negative judgment. If you’ve taken on the identity of “straight-shooter that tells it like it is and is impossible to embarrass”, that doesn’t make the exposure less of an exposure. It just means that after doing it you’re like “excellent I have now lived according to values” instead of “OH MY GOD THAT WAS HARROWING CAN WE STOP DOING EXPOSURES NOW.”

So wait, how do you organize exposures if it’s a part of your identity?

It’s actually more of a set of trigger-response pairs.

COMPULSION TRIGGER: Notice I have considered saying something or revealing information, and that I’m considering not saying it because I suspect it’s embarrassing or might make someone think less of me.

RESPONSE PREVENTION: Think hard: is there a really good, highly specific reason I can’t say the thing? Like: it would be genuinely dickish, or it would get me fired? If not: say it.

And:

COMPULSION TRIGGER: Notice I’m in a social situation and I feel vaguely anxious that I ought to be doing… something.

RESPONSE PREVENTION: Observe that the reason I’m anxious is because I’m trying to manage the interaction so this person likes me at the end of it. Then stop doing that, and fall back to honesty/kindness/selfish impulse as the backup personality stack until I can get back into flow state.

And:

COMPULSION TRIGGER: Notice I’m worried that if I join a conversation they’ll resent the intrusion.

RESPONSE PREVENTION: Ask “can I join your conversation?” and accept what answer comes.

Such situations arise naturally over the ordinary course of conversation (and also while writing blog posts).

Impression-management seems important for a lot of contexts, though.

It can be! We typically put on the heaviest facades for stuff like work and certain kinds of family gatherings where “being our truest selves” is a lower-order priority than “putting food on the table” or “not making Aunt Margaret throw another of her famous Thanksgiving Day fits.”

However, social anxiety actually causes the most life problems in lower-stakes situations: first dates, parties, making friends. A protocol that gets you through low-stakes scenarios is really doing a lot of work.

Look, I’m not saying any of this is elegant; this whole thing is basically just two or three Frankensteined-together therapeutic modalities with warranty-voiding aftermarket modifications bolted onto the side. I’m just saying that if you’re going to spend your life eating social judgment (and this is unavoidable), you might as well have fun doing it.
