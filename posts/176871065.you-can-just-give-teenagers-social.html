So, I’ve got a pretty obvious beat on this blog. I think that social anxiety is fundamentally an active habit— you are attempting to control someone’s opinion of you, this attempt at mind-control is doomed because that’s impossible, therefore you make yourself anxious by burning cycles on trying to accomplish this fundamentally doomed task. This has implications! Testable, real-world implications! If social anxiety is an active habit, then you should be able to cause people to become socially anxious by asking them to take on this doomed task.

Apparently some social scientists studied just that idea!

The Intervention

Eleanor Leigh and her colleagues recruited 57 British teenagers (ages 11-14) and divided them into two groups based on their existing anxiety levels: kids who scored really high on social anxiety measures, and kids who scored really low (they exist, apparently).

They had each kid do two different five-minute conversations with college students they’d never met before. Same kid, same day, same setting—just different instructions for each conversation.

In one conversation, participants were told:

“Focus on yourself and watch yourself during this conversation. Think about how you are coming across to the other person. And because you are really thinking about how you are coming across, we also want you to be careful about what you say, thinking about whether it is the right thing to say. And check on that as you go along, deciding whether it is good enough to say before you say it.”

(For later discussion I will call this the “Mind Control” task.)

In their other conversation, the same kids were told:

“Focus on the other person. Don’t think about yourself or how you’re coming across. Just be your natural self and respond to whatever the other person says without thinking about that. Say whatever comes to mind. Absorb yourself in the conversation, focusing on the other person.”

(For later discussion I’m calling this the “Don’t Mind Control” task.)

That’s it. That was the whole intervention. Same teenager, just different cognitive instructions between the two conversations.

Why Am I Even Talking About This

This study is the closest thing I’ve found thus far to an experimental test of my core thesis around social anxiety: that it’s not, fundamentally, about preexisting social fears that need to be overcome through hyping yourself up or just powering through, but instead is primarily an active habit which one can either perform or not.

Think about what those instructions were actually asking kids to do:

  • “Think about how you are coming across to the other person” → attempt mind-reading.

  • “Think[…] about whether it is the right thing to say” → this one’s interesting because the researchers don’t define what “the right thing to say” means; they have implied but not specifically stated a goal. The right thing… for what? Well, a reasonably intuitive response is The Right Thing is something that will make the other person think well of me and not poorly OH SHIT IT’S THE MIND CONTROL TASK AGAIN

  • “Check on that as you go along” → Let’s keep that threat-monitoring alive, shall we?

The instructions were, effectively: do the specific cognitive activities that represent trying to control how you’re perceived. And that, it turns out, is entirely sufficient.

The fact that you can turn it up or down in the same teenager, in the same afternoon, just by changing what cognitive strategy they’re using in their conversation, is pretty strong evidence that social anxiety is an active process, not a static trait or a “condition you have.”

Jesus Christ How Horrifying

I love studies that confirm my preexisting biases.

When the kids were told to monitor themselves and carefully control what they said:

  • They felt way more anxious

  • They thought they appeared way more anxious

  • They actually DID appear more anxious (according to both their conversation partners and independent observers watching the videos)

  • Their conversation partners found them less likeable

  • Their conversation partners enjoyed the conversation less

  • Their actual conversational performance got worse

  • 49% of them spontaneously experienced negative self-images (“The weird kid sitting in the corner” or “Sweating, blushing, uptight, spoilt, posh”), versus 7% in the “no mind-control” condition.

When told to focus externally and just say whatever came to mind, all of this reversed.

And this happened to everyone. Both the socially anxious kids and the not-socially-anxious kids. The normally-chill kids, when instructed to monitor themselves and carefully filter what they said, became anxious messes who came across badly to observers (though not particularly worse than the normally-anxious kids under the no-monitoring condition)

Now, Some Nuances

Table of Results (1-100 Social Anxiety Self-Reports)

So: giving the anxiogenic instructions made everyone more anxious, but the anxious kids started at a higher anxiety baseline.

This is illustrative, but also… kinda weird? This implies the anxious kids just had a different social anxiety baseline which would be cranked up or cranked down based on whether they were instructed to perform mind control, but if the anxious kids were truly just doing these self-focused mind-control behaviors at baseline then it seems like we shouldn’t have seen much of a difference between “anxious kids with mind-control instructions” and “anxious kids without mind-control instructions.”

I’m not sure how to interpret this, frankly. Possibly the “true” way to interpret this is that kids are always going to imperfectly follow instructions around this kind of thing, so the actual behavior being performed will be some blend of “the way this kid habitually treats social interaction” and “the specific instructions this kid received.”

(This is, of course, an excuse/explanation that’s very in line with my preexisting thesis, which is always suspicious.)

Anxious Kids Can’t Stop Trying To Mind-Control People Even When Instructed

THAT SAID they did take measurements (again self-reported) of how well participants felt like they could follow the provided instructions, where 1 = “I couldn’t follow these instructions at all”, 50 = “I followed instructions maybe half the time or moderately”, and 100 = “I did the thing at all times.”

Compliance Self-Ratings (1-100)

So we see that instruction performance is iffy. The anxious kids, even when instructed to drop self-focused attention and just say whatever comes to mind, couldn’t.

I think we can reframe this (this next thing is not in the paper itself; I’m attempting to draw a reasonable inference):

*Calculated as (100 - compliance with “Don’t Mind Control” instructions)

Oooh, baby, now we’re cooking.

According to this table, we can see that when provided the Mind Control task both anxious and non-anxious kids were able to do it at similarly high levels of compliance. The real difference is that anxious kids couldn’t stop doing it even when explicitly instructed. Which makes sense, intuitively; remember that time someone told you to “be yourself” and you never had social anxiety ever again?

About that compliance with the Mind Control task.

I have a hunch. These were all self-reports so what we’re really measuring isn’t the actual compliance with the provided task, but rather something adjacent: how much participants were aware of fucking up their received task.

I think this implies that the mind-control task is pretty straightforward to initiate in yourself, to some degree. Mind control itself is impossible, but kick-starting the attempt at mind control can be done by pretty much anybody, as can be attested to by anyone who’s been in a high-stakes interview or had a hot date that they really wanted to go well.

“Mind Control” Task (Both Groups ~60-65% Claimed Compliance)

Both groups reporting: “I tried to do this effortful thing and moderately succeeded”

  • Both can upregulate mind control to some degree

  • Both experience it as somewhat difficult/unnatural

  • Both report moderate success

This makes sense: both groups CAN increase self-monitoring when explicitly instructed to.

“Don’t Mind Control” Task

Chill kids at 83% self-reported compliance:

“Cool, just be natural? That’s what I normally do anyway. Easy. Done.”

Anxious kids at 60% self-reported compliance:

“How can I be natural when I am SWEATING LIKE A SODA CAN LEFT IN THE HOT SUN”

Conclusions

This is basically one study with a small N that relies heavily on self-reported data. Or, to put it another way, it’s high-quality social psych academic literature (OOOH BURRRRN)

Even so, it seems broadly consistent with my theories in this area. What fun!
