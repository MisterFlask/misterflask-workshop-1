## Abstract

Cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) has a wide-ranging empirical base, supporting its place as the evidence-based treatment of choice for the majority of psychological disorders. However, many clinicians feel that it is not appropriate for their patients, and that it is not effective in real life-settings (despite evidence to the contrary). This paper addresses the contribution that we as clinicians make to CBT going wrong. It considers the evidence that we are poor at implementing the full range of tasks that are necessary for CBT to be effective - particularly behavioural change. Therapist drift is a common phenomenon, and usually involves a shift from 'doing therapies' to 'talking therapies'. It is argued that the reason for this drift away from key tasks centres on our cognitive distortions, emotional reactions, and use of safety behaviours. A series of cases is outlined in order to identify common errors in clinical practice that impede CBT (and that can make the patient worse, rather than better). The principles behind each case are considered, along with potential solutions that can get us re-focused on the key tasks of CBT.

## Key Arguments

### 1. CBT Works in Clinical Settings (Not Just Research)

Despite claims that CBT only works in research trials, substantial evidence shows comparable response rates in non-research settings:
- Franklin et al. (2000): OCD exposure therapy effective in clinical settings
- Ghaderi (2006): Bulimia CBT effective outside research
- Lincoln et al. (2003): Social phobia treatment effective in the field
- Persons et al. (1999, 2006): Depression CBT generalizes to private practice

### 2. The Core Problem: Therapist Drift

**Definition**: Therapist drift occurs when clinicians move away from evidence-based approaches, typically shifting from "doing therapies" to "talking therapies."

**Evidence of drift**:
- Thompson-Brenner & Westen (2005): CBT therapists engage in more psychodynamic methods with complex cases, despite no evidence this is appropriate
- Brosan et al. (2007): Even accredited CBT therapists often perform below acceptable competence levels
- Brosan et al. (2008): CBT practitioners over-rate their ability to deliver CBT competently
- Stobie et al. (2007): Patients told they received CBT were not exposed to key elements of the approach
- Becker et al. (2004): Exposure-based methods for PTSD are not universally known and used by only a minority of clinicians

**Key finding**: Therapist adherence to protocols may not matter for well-motivated patients, but poor adherence is predictive of poor outcome among panic disorder patients with low motivation (Huppert et al., 2006).

### 3. The Psychology of Therapist Drift

#### Clinicians' Cognitions
- **Fundamental attributional bias**: External attributions for treatment failures (patient- or therapy-centered) while ignoring clinician's role
- **Schema-driven preconceptions**: Seeking evidence that confirms beliefs while ignoring disconfirming data
- **Black and white thinking**: Seeing therapy success in limited terms while discounting retained symptoms
- **Permissive cognitions**: Assuming failure to stay on track "doesn't matter this time"
- **Magical thinking**: Overvaluing minimal changes in symptom categories

**Critical finding**: Geller (2002) demonstrated that neither clinicians nor patients effectively appraise motivation to change. Only neutral observers (without commitment to the idea of recovery) could assess motivation in ways that predict clinical change.

#### Clinicians' Emotions
- Anxiety about performance
- Fear of negative evaluation
- Shame at performing poorly
- Guilt
- Excitement about minor changes

These emotions lead to unhelpful reactions: avoiding case discussion in supervision, failure to consider all options, being excited about relatively minor or random changes.

#### Clinicians' Safety Behaviours

**This is the central thesis**: Clinicians fail to push for behavioural change (exposure, behavioural activation, behavioural experiments) despite evidence these are the most powerful elements of treatment.

**Mechanism**: We view our role as improving patient health, which conflicts with the experience of enhancing patient anxiety during behavioural methods. Result: clinicians reduce/avoid immediate patient distress by being "nice" - but this fails to press for critical therapy tasks, leading to long-term therapeutic immobility.

## Case Examples of Therapist Drift

### Case 1: Focusing on Immediate Crises (Robert - Anger Management)
- **Problem**: Patient arrived each session with a crisis, consuming time that should have been used for homework review and behavioural change
- **Clinician exacerbation**: Failed to address need for structure; Robert's avoidance was marginally reduced while clinician became frustrated
- **Solution**: Address avoidance pattern explicitly; educate on rationale for each agenda element; maintain session structure despite patient resistance

### Case 2: Protecting the Patient (Simon - Chronic Fatigue)
- **Problem**: When behavioural activation task failed, clinician concluded patient was "more fragile" and reduced demands progressively
- **Clinician exacerbation**: Took responsibility for both patient's immediate happiness AND change - contradictory goals. Clinician spent supervision time explaining why this patient was "different."
- **Key insight**: Clinician's own safety behaviour (not pushing for change to avoid own anxiety about being seen as inadequate) permits patient's safety behaviour (avoidance).
- **Solution**: Revisit formulation; acknowledge that "stuckness" is product of two interlinked safety behaviours (clinician's and patient's)

### Case 3: Collusion with Therapy-Interfering Behaviours (Ginny - Bulimia)
- **Problem**: Patient refused to be weighed, stopped completing food diaries
- **Clinician exacerbation**: Failed to explain rationale for paired tasks; allowed discussion of weighing to consume session time; created escape/avoidance conditioning that will carry to subsequent therapy
- **Solution**: Return to explaining rationale; use "5-minute session" technique if necessary (terminate session quickly without confrontation if key tasks not done, making clear task is essential)

### Case 4: Ignoring Physiological Elements (Jane - Social Anxiety with Panic)
- **Problem**: Clinician failed to incorporate physiological element; patient withdrew from behavioural experiments while still highly aroused
- **Clinician exacerbation**: Treatment based on cognitive challenges and behavioural experiments without addressing autonomic arousal feedback loops
- **Solution**: Incorporate physiological element into formulation; use exposure with response prevention and relaxation training before behavioural experiments

### Case 5: Persisting When Therapy Is Not Working (Sally - Alcohol/Stimulant Use)
- **Problem**: Patient's stimulant use worsened anxiety; patient refused to address this
- **Clinician exacerbation**: Over-invested in patient's recovery; avoided discussing case in supervision; failed to recognize when to terminate ineffective treatment
- **Solution**: Monitor outcomes responsively with clear data; supervision and team reviews essential; know when to stop treatment

### Case 6: Rushing into 'Third Wave' Therapies (David - Chronic Depression)
- **Problem**: Patient given schema therapy (45 sessions) without ever having CBT with behavioural change
- **Clinician exacerbation**: Bypassed evidence-based approach (behavioural activation for depression) in favor of developmental analysis
- **Patient outcome**: Internalized failure ("my own fault... it's part of who I am") and acculturated to expect "talking therapy" without behaviour change
- **Solution**: Try evidence-based approach (CBT with behavioural activation) before schema therapy; present patient with treatment options (informed consent)

## Key Quotes

On therapist errors being harmful:
> "Applying CBT inappropriately or inadequately is not cost-free. Such drift has obvious costs, including wasted time and effort on the part of the clinician and the patient. However, there are even more important costs - many such behaviours exacerbate patients' problems, reduce their belief in the possibility of recovery, and make it even harder for the patient to get well."

On therapist safety behaviours:
> "Our biggest single problem in implementing CBT is that many clinicians fail to push for behavioural change (e.g., exposure, behavioural activation, skills implementation, behavioural experiments), despite the evidence that these elements of treatment are among the most powerful."

On the irony of therapist avoidance:
> "We view it as our role to improve the patient's health, and this can be hard to reconcile with the experience of enhancing the patient's anxiety during the use of behavioural methods. Thus, many clinicians make the effort to reduce or to avoid immediate patient distress (and hence their own anxiety about whether they are doing the right thing) by being 'nice' to the patient."

## Implications for Research Question

This foundational paper establishes:

1. **The concept of "therapist drift"**: Systematic movement away from behavioural interventions toward talking/cognitive approaches

2. **The mechanism**: Therapist safety behaviours mirror patient safety behaviours - both avoid short-term discomfort at the cost of long-term outcomes

3. **The consequence**: Patients who receive "drift" CBT are actually made worse - they internalize failure and become harder to treat subsequently

4. **Why training alone won't solve it**: The problem is not primarily knowledge but emotional/behavioural - therapists know what to do but avoid doing it

5. **For your blog thesis**: This directly supports the argument that "CBT" in practice often lacks the behavioural core that makes it effective. The therapist drift framework suggests that MCT's explicit focus on stopping specific cognitive processes (rather than using exposure) might be harder to "drift away from" since it doesn't require the therapist to push through patient distress in the same way.
