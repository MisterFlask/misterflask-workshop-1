## Abstract

The current study examines factors related to use of exposure therapy by clinicians who treat children with anxiety disorders. A sample of 331 therapists from a variety of backgrounds (i.e., social workers, doctoral psychologists, masters level counselors, and marriage and family therapists) completed a survey regarding use of exposure and other treatment techniques for childhood anxiety disorders, as well as beliefs about exposure and child resiliency. Although the majority of therapists endorsed a CBT orientation (81%) and use of CBT techniques, exposure therapy was rarely endorsed. Holding a PhD in psychology as well as more positive beliefs about exposure and child resiliency were associated with greater use of exposure. The results suggest that exposure-based therapy is rarely offered in community settings and that dissemination should focus on individual evidence-based principles and correcting therapist misconceptions.

## Key Arguments

### 1. The Gap Between CBT Endorsement and Exposure Use

**Background**:
- Exposure-based CBT is the preferred evidence-based treatment for childhood anxiety disorders
- Traditional CBT manuals include 6-9 sessions of anxiety management strategies (AMS) followed by 6-8 sessions of exposure
- Expert consensus: Exposure is the "active ingredient" in treating anxiety disorders
- The incremental value of AMS is unclear - exposure alone may be more efficient

**The problem**: Adults with anxiety disorders rarely receive CBT, much less exposure. This study asks: Does this apply to children too?

### 2. Study Method

**Sample**: 331 clinicians treating anxious children in Minnesota
- Social workers: 42.9%
- PhD psychologists: 18.7%
- PsyD psychologists: 11.6%
- Masters-level counselors: 16.0%
- Marriage and family therapists: 10.8%
- Mean years of experience: 15.6 years (range 1-43)
- 46.6% endorsed specializing in childhood anxiety

**Theoretical orientation**:
- 81.2% endorsed CBT (alone or with other orientations)
- PureCBT (CBT only): Most common among PhDs (46%)
- CBTplus (CBT + other): 62-74% of other professions
- Other-orientation (no CBT): Minority

**Measures**:
- Treatment techniques (33 items, 0-4 scale)
- Therapist Beliefs about Exposure Scale (TBES) - 21 items
- Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale: Therapist version (CD-RISC:T) - therapist perceptions of child resilience

### 3. Treatment Technique Categories

**Exposure (Exp)**: The presumed active ingredient
- Therapist-assisted in vivo exposure
- Client-directed in vivo exposure
- Imaginal exposure
- Interoceptive exposure
- Elimination of safety behaviors

**Anxiety Management Strategies (AMS)**: Traditional CBT non-exposure components
- Problem solving
- Identifying emotions
- Breathing retraining
- Muscle/breathing relaxation
- Cognitive restructuring

**CBT-related**: Topographically related but not in traditional protocols
- Mindfulness, meditation, biofeedback
- Motivational interviewing
- Replacing negative thoughts with positive thoughts
- Thought stopping
- Distraction, positive imagery

**Non-CBT**: Not part of CBT
- Client-centered, nondirective
- Family systems, play therapy
- Art therapy, sand tray, music therapy
- Attachment, psychodynamic
- Hypnosis

### Proportional Technique Use

When comparing categories as proportions of total technique endorsement:

| Category | Mean Proportion |
|----------|-----------------|
| AMS | 38% |
| CBT-related | 27% |
| **Exposure** | **19%** |
| Non-CBT | 16% |

**Exposure was significantly less than AMS** (d = 2.33) and CBT-related (d = 1.02)

**How many therapists emphasize exposure?**
- 79.6%: CBT components (AMS + Exposure) > 50% of techniques
- **5.3%**: Exposure was highest proportional endorsement
- **1.9%**: Exposure highest AND worked with parents 50%+ of time
- **1.6%**: Exposure > 50% of total endorsement

### Predictors of Exposure Use

#### By Profession

| Profession | % Using Exposure | Proportional Exposure Use |
|------------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| PhD Psychology | **86.0%** | **.27** |
| PsyD Psychology | 67.7% | .20 |
| Social Workers | 49.6% | .16 |
| MA Counselors | 48.8% | .18 |
| Marriage/Family Therapists | 44.8% | .14 |

PhDs used significantly more exposure than all other professions (d = 0.62-1.17)

#### By Theoretical Orientation

| Orientation | % Using Exposure | Proportional Exposure Use |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| PureCBT | **76.1%** | **.25** |
| CBTplus | 58.8% | .19 |
| Other | 36.0% | .13 |

Pure CBT orientation associated with more exposure than CBTplus (d = 0.57) or Other (d = 0.98)

#### By Therapist Beliefs

**TBES (negative beliefs about exposure)**:
- Therapists who use exposure: M = 30.15
- Therapists who don't use exposure: M = 43.93
- **Effect size: d = 1.37** (the strongest predictor)

**CD-RISC:T (belief in child resilience)**:
- Therapists who use exposure: M = 30.67
- Therapists who don't use exposure: M = 27.92
- Effect size: d = 0.42

### Combined Regression Analysis

**Predicting proportional exposure use** (R² = .41):

| Predictor | Beta | p |
|-----------|------|---|
| Profession (PhD) | -.19 | .000 |
| Orientation (pureCBT) | -.10 | .073 |
| **TBES (negative beliefs)** | **-.52** | **.000** |
| Years of practice | .01 | .826 |

**Negative beliefs about exposure was the dominant predictor** (β = -.52)

## Key Quotes

On the irony of CBT endorsement without exposure:
> "Although the majority of therapists endorsed a CBT orientation (81%) and use of CBT techniques, exposure therapy was rarely endorsed."

On the availability of exposure-based treatment:
> "With only 5% of therapists endorsing more use of exposure than other techniques, it is likely exceedingly difficult for families to find therapists who provide exposure-based CBT in a manner consistent with clinical practice guidelines."

On technique competition:
> "Because therapy is time limited, a therapist's decision to use one technique comes at the cost of not implementing another."

On the problem with multi-component manuals:
> "Treatment manuals that include multiple components (e.g., problem solving, identifying emotions, relaxation, exposure) may allow clinicians to pick and choose techniques they feel comfortable with, while still considering themselves to be using EBT."

## Implications for Research Question

### 1. "CBT" ≠ Exposure
81% of therapists endorse CBT, but only 5% emphasize exposure. The label "CBT" is essentially meaningless for predicting whether a child will receive the active ingredient.

### 2. The d = 1.37 Finding
Negative beliefs about exposure had by far the largest effect size of any predictor. This is not a training problem - it's a belief problem. Therapists with negative beliefs about exposure (that it's harmful, intolerable, unethical) simply don't use it.

### 3. Training Level Matters But Doesn't Solve It
PhD psychologists are most likely to use exposure (86%), but even among PhDs, only about a quarter emphasize it proportionally. Advanced training increases but doesn't guarantee appropriate exposure use.

### 4. Pseudo-CBT Techniques Fill the Gap
When exposure is avoided, therapists don't do "nothing" - they fill sessions with:
- Thought stopping (ranked 10th)
- Distraction (ranked 11th)
- Replacing negative thoughts with positive (ranked 5th)

These are **misapplications of cognitive strategies that constitute avoidance** and are incompatible with exposure principles.

### 5. For the Blog Thesis

This paper provides the strongest evidence yet that:
- "CBT" as commonly practiced bears little resemblance to the research protocols
- The gap is primarily driven by therapist anxiety/beliefs, not training
- Non-exposure techniques "crowd out" exposure
- Only ~5% of therapists deliver what the research actually supports

The finding that belief in child resilience predicts exposure use is particularly interesting - it suggests therapists who view clients as fragile avoid the treatment that would help them.

## Limitations

- Self-report (therapists may overestimate CBT use)
- Single state (Minnesota)
- Cross-sectional design
- Novel CD-RISC:T measure
