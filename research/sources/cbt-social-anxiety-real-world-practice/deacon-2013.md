## Abstract

Interoceptive exposure (IE) is widely regarded as an essential procedure in the cognitive-behavioral treatment of panic disorder (PD). However, treatment manuals differ substantially in their prescribed delivery of IE, and little research exists to inform the optimal manner of its implementation. The present study examined therapists' perceptions and delivery of IE for PD. Results revealed substantial variability in how clinicians provide IE. In contrast to the prolonged and intense manner in which exposure techniques are traditionally applied, many therapists reported delivering a low dose of IE accompanied by controlled breathing strategies. Concerns about the potential adverse effects of IE were common despite the fact that participants reported the actual occurrence of negative outcomes of IE in their own practice to be extremely infrequent. It is possible that some therapists deliver IE in a cautious manner in an attempt to minimize the perceived risks associated with this treatment.

## Key Arguments

### 1. The Problem: Inconsistent IE Delivery

Interoceptive exposure (IE) - direct exposure to feared arousal-related body sensations - is considered essential for effective CBT for panic disorder. However, treatment manuals differ substantially in how IE should be delivered:

**Barlow & Craske's Panic Control Treatment**:
- Includes cognitive reappraisal and diaphragmatic breathing alongside IE
- IE introduced late in treatment
- Pre-specified number of trials (e.g., 3 one-minute trials)
- Rest periods between trials to allow anxiety to subside
- Concurrent use of controlled breathing strategies

**Prolonged/Intense Approach (e.g., Arntz, 2002; Otto et al., 2009)**:
- Minimizes or omits cognitive and breathing techniques
- Prolonged, uninterrupted trials
- Continues until habituation
- Similar to exposure protocols for OCD and PTSD

**Key finding**: Little empirical guidance exists to determine which approach is optimal. Deacon et al. (2012) found controlled breathing did NOT enhance IE efficacy.

### 2. Therapist Negative Beliefs About Exposure

Exposure therapy is associated with negative beliefs among therapists:
- Perception that it is unethical
- Belief that it is harmful
- Concern it is intolerable for clients
- Risk management concerns

**Critical hypothesis**: These negative beliefs cause therapists to deliver exposure in a cautious, less-than-intense manner.

**IE is particularly susceptible**: IE is the least-used exposure technique (Freiheit et al., 2004) and rated as least ethical, acceptable, and helpful form of exposure (Richard & Gloster, 2007).

### 3. Study Method

**Sample**: 66 therapists from the Anxiety Disorders Association of America who use IE
- Mean age: 47.3 years
- Mean experience: 16.1 years
- Majority doctoral-level psychologists (n=52)
- Average of ~99 PD clients treated with IE

**Measure**: Interoceptive Exposure Survey assessing:
- Use of rest periods between trials
- Number of IE trials
- Use of cognitive reappraisal
- Use of controlled breathing
- Perceived likelihood of negative outcomes
- Actual occurrence of negative outcomes

## Key Findings

### Style of IE Delivery

**Rest periods**: Substantial variability
- Mean rest period: 181 seconds (3 minutes)
- 13.6% allowed NO rest between trials
- 21.2% used rest periods of 5+ minutes

**Number of trials**: Varied markedly
- Mean leaned toward pre-specified number rather than habituation-based
- Average IE session duration: 17.6 minutes
- 16.7% averaged 5 or fewer minutes per session
- 22.7% averaged 30+ minutes per session

### Use of Concurrent Strategies

**Cognitive reappraisal**: 93.9% used it before, during, or after IE
- Primary reason: "facilitates cognitive change" (91.9%)
- Also: increases self-efficacy (71.0%), makes IE more acceptable (56.5%)

**Controlled breathing**: 40.9% used it with IE
- Therapists who used it rated it as moderately beneficial (M=51.5)
- Therapists who didn't use it rated it as unhelpful (M=5.9)
- **This difference was massive**: d=1.91

### Perceived vs. Actual Negative Outcomes

**Perceived likelihood** (for prolonged/intense hyperventilation vignette):

| Negative Outcome | Mean Likelihood |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Client would prematurely stop exposure | 54.9% |
| Client would drop out of therapy | 37.1% |
| Client would decompensate | 22.4% |
| Symptoms would worsen | 17.9% |
| Client would pass out | 17.3% |

**Actual occurrence** (across ~6,545 clients treated):

| Negative Outcome | Base Rate |
|-----------------|-----------|
| Client dropped out due to IE aversiveness | 1.4% |
| Client vomited | 0.1% |
| Client passed out | 0.03% |
| Client had seizure | 0.03% |
| Heart attack, stroke, psychosis, lawsuit, death | 0% |

**The disconnect is striking**: Therapists reported negative outcomes as having 15-55% likelihood, while actual base rates were near zero.

### Predictors of Concerns About Negative Outcomes

Hierarchical regression (R² = 30.3%):
- Therapist demographics: NOT significant
- **Use of controlled breathing**: Significant predictor (β = -.30)
- **Belief that controlled breathing enhances IE**: Significant predictor (β = .34)

**Key finding**: Therapists who used controlled breathing and believed it helpful were MORE likely to perceive IE as risky.

## Central Thesis: Therapist Safety Behaviors

The authors propose that **cautious IE delivery constitutes a therapist safety behavior**:

1. Therapists concerned about IE risks deliver it cautiously (short trials, long rest periods, controlled breathing)
2. Their clients don't experience catastrophic outcomes (pass out, decompensate, etc.)
3. Therapists attribute the non-occurrence to their cautious approach
4. This **maintains or reinforces** their inaccurate beliefs about IE dangers
5. Result: The safety behavior persists despite repeated disconfirmatory evidence

**The mechanism mirrors patient safety behaviors**: Just as anxious patients use safety behaviors that prevent belief disconfirmation, anxious therapists do the same.

## Key Quotes

On the disconnect between perception and reality:
> "The present study found a striking disconnect between therapists' relatively high endorsement of concerns about negative outcomes in the case vignette and the exceptionally low base rate of actual adverse effects from their own clinical practice."

On therapist safety behaviors:
> "From this perspective, the delivery of IE in a cautious manner in order to minimize the probability of negative outcomes constitutes a therapist safety behavior."

On belief maintenance:
> "Although the base rate of adverse effects in the present sample suggests that these consequences are extremely unlikely, therapists concerned with the dangers of IE may erroneously conclude that their judicious delivery style was responsible for the non-occurrence of these negative outcomes."

## Implications for Research Question

### 1. Exposure Delivery Quality Varies Dramatically
Even among ADAA members who use IE (a select group), delivery style ranges from brief, controlled breathing-enhanced sessions to prolonged habituation-based protocols. This variability likely affects outcomes.

### 2. ~40% Use Controlled Breathing Despite No Evidence
Controlled breathing doesn't enhance IE efficacy (Deacon et al., 2012), yet 40% of therapists use it. This suggests treatment decisions are driven by therapist comfort rather than evidence.

### 3. Therapist Beliefs Predict Delivery Style
The causal chain: Negative beliefs → Cautious delivery → Belief maintenance. Training alone won't fix this - the beliefs must be targeted directly.

### 4. The Therapist Safety Behavior Framework
This paper provides a mechanistic explanation for why therapist drift persists: cautious delivery functions as a safety behavior that prevents disconfirmation of catastrophic beliefs.

### 5. For the Blog Thesis
This strongly supports the argument that "CBT" varies dramatically in practice. Even the specific technique of interoceptive exposure - with clear protocols - is delivered in wildly different ways based on therapist anxiety. If therapists are this variable with panic disorder (which has well-established protocols), social anxiety treatment is likely even more variable.

## Limitations

- Low response rate (9.9% of valid contacts)
- Self-selection possible (therapists with adverse effects may not have participated)
- Self-report of behaviors (no direct observation)
- Cross-sectional design (can't establish causation)
- Focused only on IE for panic disorder
