# Digest: Therapist perceptions and delivery of interoceptive exposure for panic disorder

Source: [deacon-2013](../../sources/cbt-social-anxiety-real-world-practice/deacon-2013.md)
Access: abstract-only
Last Updated: 2025-01-16

## Summary

National survey of therapists examining how they actually deliver interoceptive exposure (IE) for panic disorder. Found substantial variability, with many therapists using "low-dose" exposure accompanied by controlled breathing strategies. Therapists expressed concerns about adverse effects despite rarely observing them in practice.

## Key Findings

- **~40% of therapists prescribed controlled breathing strategies during IE**
- **70% of those who used controlled breathing** did so "to make exposure exercises less aversive and more acceptable"
- Therapists delivered IE in "low-dose" manner rather than prolonged/intense
- Therapists reported concerns about adverse effects (decompensation, symptom worsening, dropout)
- **Actual negative outcomes were "extremely infrequent"** in therapists' own reported experience
- Gap between perceived risk and actual risk suggests therapist anxiety driving cautious delivery

## Operationalization of Key Concepts

| Concept | How Measured/Operationalized | Notes |
|---------|------------------------------|-------|
| Safety behavior during exposure | "Did you prescribe controlled breathing strategies during IE?" | Binary question; followed by asking *why* |
| Rationale for safety behavior | Asked why controlled breathing was used | 70% said "to make it less aversive" - confirms safety behavior function |
| Low-dose delivery | Compared to prolonged/intense traditional delivery | Operationalization unclear from abstract - need full text |
| Therapist concerns about IE | Presumably questionnaire items about perceived risks | Specific items unknown |
| Actual negative outcomes | Therapist-reported frequency of bad outcomes | Self-report of own clinical experience |

**Operationalization concerns**: The controlled breathing question is well-operationalized - it's concrete and behavioral, and the follow-up "why" question confirms the safety behavior function (reducing aversiveness). However, we don't know from the abstract: (1) sample size, (2) how "low-dose" was measured, (3) full list of concerns assessed, (4) how "negative outcomes" were defined.

## Methodology

- **Study Type**: Cross-sectional national survey
- **Sample**: Unknown from abstract (need full text)
- **Measures**: Survey on IE delivery practices, beliefs about IE
- **Duration**: Single timepoint

## Methodological Critique

### Strengths
- Concrete behavioral question (controlled breathing: yes/no)
- Follow-up on *why* - captures safety behavior function
- Compares perceived vs actual risk (therapists worried but rarely saw problems)

### Limitations
- Abstract-only - missing sample size and full methodology
- Self-report of delivery practices
- Panic-specific (may not generalize to SAD)
- "Low-dose" poorly defined from available info

## Relevance to Research Question

**This is the key operationalization paper for "therapists allowing safety behaviors during exposure."** The finding that 40% use controlled breathing, and 70% of those do it to reduce aversiveness, is a concrete demonstration of therapist drift. It's not just that they avoid exposure - even when they DO exposure, they often undermine it.

**Implication**: "Exposure" is not binary. A therapist can say they "use exposure" while simultaneously teaching the patient a safety behavior that prevents the mechanism of change from operating.

## Connections to Other Sources

- Provides mechanism for Waller's "therapist drift" - therapists' own anxiety about patient distress leads to cautious/undermined delivery
- Explains why van Minnen 2015's high "exposure use" rates might be misleading - Dutch therapists might ALSO be allowing safety behaviors
- Whiteside 2016's TBES measures *beliefs* about exposure; this paper shows how those beliefs translate to *behavior* (controlled breathing prescription)
