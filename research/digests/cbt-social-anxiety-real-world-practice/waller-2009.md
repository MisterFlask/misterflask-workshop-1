# Digest: Evidence-based treatment and therapist drift

Source: [waller-2009](../../sources/cbt-social-anxiety-real-world-practice/waller-2009.md)
Access: abstract-only
Last Updated: 2025-01-16

## Summary

Foundational paper introducing the concept of "therapist drift" - the tendency for clinicians to move away from evidence-based behavioral interventions toward less effective "talking therapies." Argues that this drift is caused by therapists' own cognitive distortions, emotional reactions, and safety behaviors, mirroring the very processes they're supposed to be treating in patients.

## Key Findings

- **Core concept**: "Therapist drift" = shift from "doing therapies" to "talking therapies"
- CBT has strong empirical support, but many clinicians believe it doesn't work for their patients
- Drift is caused by therapists' own:
  - Cognitive distortions
  - Emotional reactions (especially anxiety)
  - Safety behaviors (avoiding patient distress to manage own discomfort)
- Treatment failure often reflects **therapist factors**, not CBT's inherent limitations
- Paper provides case examples of clinical errors that impede CBT

## Relevance to Research Question

**This is the theoretical linchpin**: Waller's framework explains WHY the gap exists between research protocols and clinical practice. It's not just training deficits or time constraints - it's that therapists themselves have anxiety about implementing anxiety treatments.

**Stunning implication for your thesis**: Therapists are exhibiting the exact same pattern as their patients:
- Patient: Avoids social situations to prevent distress → maintains anxiety
- Therapist: Avoids behavioral interventions to prevent patient distress (and own discomfort) → patient doesn't improve

The therapist's "safety behavior" is doing cognitive restructuring instead of exposure. It feels helpful in the moment but prevents the mechanism of change.

## Connections to Other Sources

- Whiteside 2016 provides empirical support: therapist beliefs/anxiety predict non-use (d=1.37)
- van Minnen 2015 shows even "exposure users" outsource it to homework (avoiding in-session distress)
- Follow-up paper (Waller 2016) reportedly expands on mechanisms and solutions
