# Digest: On the use of exposure therapy in the treatment of anxiety disorders: a survey among cognitive behavioural therapists in the Netherlands

Source: [vanminnen-2015](../../sources/cbt-social-anxiety-real-world-practice/vanminnen-2015.md)
Access: full-text
Last Updated: 2025-01-16

## Summary

Dutch survey of 490 CBT therapists examining exposure therapy use across anxiety disorders. Found much higher reported exposure use than US studies (97.8%), but revealed that exposure mostly happens as homework assignments rather than therapist-directed in-session work. Also found significant gaps in disorder-specific best practices.

## Key Findings

**Overall exposure use:**
- 97.8% of Dutch CBT therapists report using exposure
- Compare to US: only ~60% report regular exposure use (Freiheit 2004)

**BUT - how exposure is delivered matters:**
- Exposure homework assignments: 89-91% use this
- Self-directed in vivo exposure: 78-83%
- **Therapist-directed in vivo exposure: only 35-52%**
- "Exposure was thus mostly practiced outside the formal therapy sessions"

**Gaps in disorder-specific practice:**
- 22.1% of panic patients get NO interoceptive exposure
- 15.3% of OCD patients get NO exposure and response prevention
- 9.2% of social anxiety patients get NO imaginal exposure

**What predicts exposure use:**
- Greater therapist willingness to use exposure
- Greater credibility beliefs about exposure
- Better workplace resources (protocols, materials)

**Barriers reported:**
- 55.3% lacked proper protocols at workplace
- 22.2% reported insufficient materials (equipment, stimulus materials)
- 24-56% of therapists (across experience levels) wanted more training

**Concerning finding:**
- ~25% of patients reportedly refused exposure treatment
- May indicate therapists framing exposure negatively or offering alternatives too readily

## Operationalization of Key Concepts

| Concept | How Measured/Operationalized | Notes |
|---------|------------------------------|-------|
| Exposure use | Self-report questionnaire on intervention use | Broken down by delivery method (homework vs in-session, therapist-directed vs self-directed) - more granular than Whiteside |
| Therapist-directed in vivo exposure | Specific item asking about this delivery format | Distinguishes from homework/self-directed |
| Interoceptive exposure (panic) | Asked if used for panic disorder patients | Disorder-specific; 22% reported NOT using |
| ERP (OCD) | Asked if exposure + response prevention used for OCD | 15% reported NOT using |
| Willingness/credibility | Attitudes toward exposure as treatment | Predicted usage rates |
| Workplace resources | Questions about protocol availability, materials | 55% lacked protocols, 22% lacked materials |

**Operationalization concerns**: Still self-report (no session verification). The key advance over US studies is distinguishing *how* exposure is delivered - but "therapist-directed in vivo exposure" doesn't tell us if safety behaviors are addressed or if exposure is done to protocol.

## Methodology

- **Study Type**: Cross-sectional survey
- **Sample**: n=490 members of Dutch Association for Behavioural and Cognitive Therapy (VGCt)
- **Measures**: Self-report questionnaire on intervention use across 4 anxiety disorders (social anxiety, specific phobia, OCD, panic)
- **Duration**: Single timepoint

## Methodological Critique

### Strengths
- Large sample size (n=490)
- Disorder-specific breakdown (not just "anxiety" as monolith)
- Examines HOW exposure is delivered, not just whether it's used
- Comparison to US data provides cross-cultural context

### Limitations
- **Selection bias**: VGCt members are committed CBT practitioners; community therapists likely worse
- **Self-report**: No verification of actual session content
- **Dutch context**: May not generalize to US/UK healthcare systems
- **No data on quality**: Using exposure ≠ using exposure well (allowing safety behaviors?)

## Relevance to Research Question

**Key insight**: Even among dedicated CBT practitioners, exposure is often outsourced to homework rather than done in-session with therapist guidance. This matters because:
1. Homework compliance is often poor
2. Patients may use safety behaviors during self-directed exposure
3. Therapist isn't present to prevent avoidance or address beliefs in the moment

**For your thesis**: The gap between "we use exposure" (97.8%) and "we do in-session therapist-directed exposure" (35-52%) is exactly the kind of implementation failure that could explain why "CBT" doesn't always work. If exposure mostly means "go do scary things on your own," the metacognitive/attentional components are completely unaddressed.

## Connections to Other Sources

- Much more optimistic than Whiteside 2016 (US data), but sample is more select
- Supports Waller's concern about therapist avoidance - even when therapists "use" exposure, they may be avoiding the hard part (being present during patient distress)
- Raises question: what happens in the sessions if exposure is homework? Probably cognitive restructuring / "talking therapy"
