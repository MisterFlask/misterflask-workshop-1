# Digest: Factors Associated with Practitioners' Use of Exposure Therapy for Childhood Anxiety Disorders

Source: [whiteside-2016](../../sources/cbt-social-anxiety-real-world-practice/whiteside-2016.md)
Access: full-text
Last Updated: 2025-01-16

## Summary

US survey examining why exposure therapy is underused for childhood anxiety despite being the "active ingredient" in CBT. Found that therapists' own negative beliefs about exposure are the strongest predictor of non-use, with a large effect size. Training level and theoretical orientation also matter, but beliefs dominate.

## Key Findings

- Only 61.4% of surveyed therapists reported providing exposure therapy
- Exposure comprised only 19% of total techniques endorsed
- Only 5.3% made exposure their highest proportional treatment focus
- **Negative beliefs about exposure** = strongest predictor of non-use
  - Non-users scored M=43.93 on Therapist Beliefs about Exposure Scale
  - Users scored M=30.15
  - Effect size: **d=1.37** (very large)
  - Correlation with proportional exposure use: r=-.59

**Training effects:**
- PhD psychologists: 86% use exposure
- PsyD: 68%
- Social workers: 50%
- Master's counselors: 49%
- MFTs: 45%

**Orientation effects:**
- Pure CBT: 76% use exposure
- CBT + other: 59%
- Non-CBT: 36%

## Operationalization of Key Concepts

| Concept | How Measured/Operationalized | Notes |
|---------|------------------------------|-------|
| Exposure use | Self-report endorsement of using exposure therapy | Binary (yes/no) - doesn't distinguish quality, frequency, or in-session vs homework |
| Negative beliefs about exposure | Therapist Beliefs about Exposure Scale (TBES) | Validated scale developed by Deacon et al. (2013); measures fears about harm, exacerbation, alliance damage |
| Theoretical orientation | Self-report of primary orientation | Categories: CBT, CBT+other, non-CBT |
| "Proportional exposure use" | % of total techniques endorsed that were exposure-related | Captures relative emphasis, not absolute frequency |

**Operationalization concerns**: "Exposure use" is self-report and binary - a therapist who assigns one homework exposure per treatment counts the same as one who does intensive in-session work. The study can't distinguish *quality* of exposure delivery or whether safety behaviors are addressed.

## Methodology

- **Study Type**: Cross-sectional survey
- **Sample**: n=279 practitioners treating childhood anxiety
- **Measures**: Therapist Beliefs about Exposure Scale (TBES), self-report of techniques used
- **Duration**: Single timepoint

## Methodological Critique

### Strengths
- Large effect sizes make findings robust
- Controlled for multiple variables in regression
- Measured specific beliefs, not just general attitudes

### Limitations
- Self-report (no verification of actual practice)
- Childhood anxiety focus (may not generalize to adults)
- Cross-sectional (can't determine if beliefs cause non-use or rationalize it)
- No breakdown by specific anxiety disorder (SAD vs. OCD vs. phobia)

## Relevance to Research Question

**Directly addresses core question**: Shows that even therapists who identify as CBT practitioners often don't use exposure. The mechanism is primarily therapist beliefs/anxiety, not training deficits per se.

**Key implication for your thesis**: Therapists themselves may be engaging in avoidance behavior! They fear exposure will "exacerbate anxiety, cause harm, damage therapeutic alliance" - but these are exactly the kinds of safety behavior-driven beliefs that MCT would target in patients.

## Connections to Other Sources

- Supports Waller's "therapist drift" framework (therapists' own anxiety drives avoidance of behavioral interventions)
- Dutch data (van Minnen 2015) shows much higher exposure use, but Dutch sample was CBT association members only
- Raises question: if therapists avoid exposure, what ARE they doing? (Probably cognitive restructuring without behavioral components)
