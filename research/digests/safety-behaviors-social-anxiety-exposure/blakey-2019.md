# Digest: Blakey et al. 2019 — RCT of Judicious Use vs Elimination of Safety Behaviors

Source: [blakey-2019](../../sources/safety-behaviors-social-anxiety-exposure/blakey-2019.md)
Access: full-text
Last Updated: 2026-03-16

## Summary

The first multi-session RCT directly testing "judicious use" (E/JU = fading SBs over sessions) versus immediate elimination (E/ESB) for specific phobia (spider fear). N=60, 4 twice-weekly sessions. No significant differences on any outcome measure at posttreatment or 1-month follow-up. Both conditions produced large treatment effects. Provides the best available operationalization of "judicious use" as a treatment protocol with systematic fading.

## Key Findings

- **Primary outcomes**: No significant group differences (FSQ: d=2.53 across conditions; BAT: d=1.94). Large effects in both conditions.
- **No acceptability/tolerability difference**: TAAS scores equal (both conditions endorsed positive ratings after receiving rationale)
- **No distress tolerance difference**: Both conditions improved equally on BAT distress tolerance
- **Safety misattributions** (exploratory): E/JU participants made significantly more SB-related exposure outcome attributions ("the spider didn't bite me because I was wearing gloves") — consistent with Salkovskis (1991) misattribution hypothesis
- **Negative expectancies** (exploratory): E/ESB participants had significantly higher pre-exposure negative expectancies (d=0.62) — consistent with ILT prediction that SBs reduce expectancy violation opportunity
- **Behavioral approach** (exploratory): E/JU participants reached exposure goal earlier across sessions; 3 E/JU participants could only complete goal *when* using safety behaviors
- **Clinical implication**: "therapists may not need to be concerned if their patient is unwilling to immediately eliminate their safety behavior(s) as long as the patient explicitly tests their fear-based negative expectancies"

## Operationalization of Key Concepts

| Concept | How Operationalized | Notes |
|---------|---------------------|-------|
| E/ESB condition | Therapist explains SBs interfere by: (a) preventing belief disconfirmation, (b) absorbing attentional resources | Instructions without SBs throughout all exposures |
| E/JU condition | Therapist explains SBs enhance by: (a) making situation less distressing, (b) enhancing ability to remain in phobic situation | Systematic fading: 2 SBs session 1 → 1 SB session 2 → 0 SBs session 3 |
| Safety behaviors used | Semi-idiographic: participants chose 2 from 8 items: eye goggles, dental visor face shield, long chemistry apron, long sleeve rain jacket, short work gloves, long chemistry gloves, boot/shoe covers, 12" clear plastic shield | Physical protective items appropriate for spider fear; not SAD-relevant |
| SB fading protocol | Most helpful item used in sessions 1 & 2; second-most helpful item used in session 1 only; no SBs in session 3 | Only 3 sessions involved direct exposure (sessions 2–4) |
| Negative expectancy measurement | "How strongly do you believe [primary feared prediction] will occur?" 0–100% scale, before each exposure | Averaged across 3 exposure trials |
| Attentional focus | "How much attention were you paying toward testing your negative prediction for harm, versus doing/thinking about something else?" 0–100% scale, at exposure midpoint | Averaged across 3 exposure trials |
| Safety attribution | Verbatim post-exposure attribution coded for SB-related content | E.g., "spider didn't bite me because I was wearing gloves" |
| Compliance | Treatment recordings coded (15% of sessions) for treatment fidelity using 12 process items + up to 17 session-specific items; interrater agreement 98.38% | **This is the only study in this literature with formal session-level compliance coding** |

**Operationalization notes**:
- SBs are all physical protective gear — entirely different from SAD's interpersonal/self-presentational SBs
- The "judicious use" with systematic fading is the most ecologically valid test of Rachman et al. (2008) to date
- Study explicitly excluded mental safety behaviors (distraction) in both conditions — artificially homogenizes cognitive engagement
- Both conditions received a clear treatment rationale; prior acceptability studies used brief vignettes without rationale, which may explain different acceptability findings

## Methodology

- **Study Type**: Two-arm parallel-group RCT (E/ESB vs E/JU)
- **Sample**: N=60, DSM-5 spider phobia, mean age 31.5 years, 85% female, community sample; excluded: prior exposure therapy, spider/bee allergy, ≥10 BAT steps at pre
- **Sessions**: 4 twice-weekly individual 1-hour sessions
- **Measures**: FSQ (spider fear self-report), BAT (behavioral approach task, 13 steps), TAAS (treatment acceptability), peak BAT distress, in-vivo distress tolerance
- **Follow-up**: 1-month
- **Fidelity**: 15% of sessions coded, 98.38% interrater agreement
- **Intent-to-treat**: Yes, last observation carried forward; 95% retention (3/60 dropped)

## Methodological Critique

### Strengths
- First multi-session RCT with proper judicious use fading protocol
- Multi-method assessment (self-report + behavioral + process variables)
- 1-month follow-up
- Formal treatment fidelity coding (unique in this literature)
- Power analysis used for sample size determination
- Expert consultation for both conditions (Abramowitz for E/ESB, Radomsky for E/JU)

### Limitations
- Spider phobia only — does not generalize to SAD (different SBs, different feared outcomes)
- Physical protective gear SBs do not resemble SAD's interpersonal SBs
- SBs limited to overt; both conditions instructed to avoid mental SBs
- Single site, homogeneous sample (85% female, 68% White)
- Possible ceiling effect (exposure is highly effective for specific phobias)
- 4 sessions may be insufficient to show fading effects in complex presentations

## Relevance to Research Question

**Critical finding**: The only RCT of "judicious use with fading" vs. immediate elimination shows NO differences in outcomes. This directly challenges the simple claim that SBs must be eliminated immediately. However, the spider phobia context makes generalization to SAD uncertain — the SBs are fundamentally different (physical protective gear vs. interpersonal impression management).

Also: this is the **only study in the safety behaviors literature with formal session-level compliance coding** (15% of sessions, 98.38% agreement). The compliance failure problem identified in prior studies is addressed here, at least for treatment fidelity (though not for moment-to-moment SB use during exposure tasks themselves).
