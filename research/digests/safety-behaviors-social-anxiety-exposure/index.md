# Synthesis: How Safety Behaviors Are Operationalized in Social Anxiety Exposure Therapy Studies

Last Updated: 2026-03-16

---

## Executive Summary

Safety behaviors (SBs) in social anxiety research are operationalized through four primary methods: (1) **patient-specific idiosyncratic identification** before exposure sessions (Wells 1995; Fang 2014); (2) **standardized self-report questionnaires** measuring frequency of use (SBQ, SAFE, ASBQ); (3) **scripted behavioral instruction sets** used in experimental manipulations (Goodall 2019; Selles 2021); and (4) **therapist-directed elimination protocols** combining psychoeducation, behavioral goals, and verification (F-SET; social mishap exposures). Definitions are broadly consistent — SBs are actions intended to prevent feared social outcomes but which maintain anxiety by preventing disconfirmation — but operationalizations diverge substantially across paradigms. A critical, underappreciated issue: most experimental manipulations confound SB use with self-focused attention; they cannot isolate safety behavior effects alone.

**SAD-specific evidence is unambiguous**: every clinical treatment study in the SAD domain (Wells 1995; McManus 2008; Morgan & Raffle 1999; Kim 2005; Plasencia et al. 2011; Taylor & Alden 2010/2011) found SB elimination beneficial or SBs harmful. The one multi-session RCT of "judicious use with fading" vs immediate elimination (Blakey et al. 2019) found no difference — but used spider phobia (physical protective gear SBs), not SAD. Compliance failure is a confirmed methodological problem across the literature; Blakey et al. (2019) is the only study with formal session-level fidelity coding.

## Key Themes

### 1. Definitional Consistency, Operational Divergence

All major sources share a functional definition: safety behaviors are actions performed to prevent or minimize feared catastrophes (Clark & Wells 1995; Blakey & Abramowitz 2016). However, what *counts* as a safety behavior — and how its presence or absence is verified — differs markedly:

- **Clinical studies** (Wells 1995; Morgan & Raffle 1999): SBs identified idiosyncratically per patient before each session. No standardized list.
- **Experimental analogue studies** (Goodall 2019; Selles 2021): A fixed set of 2–6 items selected from a validated scale. Participants instructed to "perform" or "not perform" those items.
- **Treatment outcome studies** (F-SET; social mishap): SBs identified collaboratively, then targeted through fading or antiphobic exercises.
- **Survey/correlational studies** (Cuming 2009; Goodall Study 1): Self-report frequency scales — SBQ, SAFE — measuring how often participants use a predefined set of behaviors.

### 2. The Standard Experimental Paradigm: Instructions-Based Manipulation

The dominant experimental design for social anxiety involves a two-condition conversation task:

**"With" condition**: participants instructed to use SBs. Based on published materials (Selles 2021, confirmed by McManus 2007 template):
> *"Focus on yourself and watch yourself during this conversation. Think about how you are coming across to the other person... be careful about what you say, deciding whether it is good enough to say before you say it."*

**"Without" condition**: participants instructed to drop SBs:
> *"Focus on the other person. Don't think about yourself or how you're coming across. Just be your natural self... Say whatever comes to mind. Absorb yourself in the conversation."*

**Critical problem**: These instructions simultaneously manipulate (a) self-focused attention, (b) cognitive safety behaviors (rehearsing/filtering), and (c) impression management monitoring. **The manipulation is confounded — effects cannot be attributed to safety behaviors alone.**

The Goodall et al. (2019) subtypes paper offers a cleaner operationalization for specific subtypes:

- **Avoidance manipulation**: "avoid talking about yourself"; "keep still"; "talk less"
- **Impression management manipulation**: "picture how you appear to others"; "check how you're coming across"; "try to come across well"

### 3. The Two Main Measurement Instruments

**Social Behaviour Questionnaire (SBQ; Clark 2005)**:
- 28 items, 0–3 frequency scale (never → always)
- Two subscales: avoidance (8 items, e.g., "talk less," "avoid eye contact") and impression management (5 items, e.g., "check you're coming across well")
- Used by McManus (2007), Hirsch et al., Goodall (2019), context-specific study (2024)
- Modified version (SBQ modified): 9-item IM (α=.87), 6-item avoidance (α=.85), 0–8 scale

**Subtle Avoidance Frequency Examination (SAFE; Cuming et al. 2009)**:
- 32 items, 5-point scale (never → always)
- Three factors: (1) active safety behaviors, (2) subtle behavioral restriction, (3) symptom concealment
- Developed by Rapee's group (Macquarie); used primarily in Australian research
- Responsive to treatment effects; good discrimination clinical vs. non-clinical

**Adolescent version (ASBQ)**: 29 items, 0–3 scale (Cronbach's α=0.74) — adapted from adult SBQ for 11–18 year-olds

Both instruments measure *frequency* rather than the *functional relationship* between the behavior and the feared outcome (which is what the definition requires).

### 4. How "Banning" Safety Behaviors Is Operationalized in Practice

Across studies, five distinct approaches to operationalizing SB elimination are identifiable:

**A. Counterbalanced exposure sessions (Wells 1995 template):**
Each patient's idiosyncratic SBs identified pre-session. One session = exposure alone; one session = exposure + "decrease" of pre-identified SBs. Specific instructions not published in accessible text, but imply therapist-guided in-session coaching.

**B. CBT group instructions to "drop safety behaviors" (Morgan & Raffle 1999):**
"Additional instructions to drop safety behaviours" added to standard CBT group treatment. No detail available on specific instruction content from abstract.

**C. Three-condition exposure experiment (Kim 2005):**
- Exposure + decreased SBs under *cognitive rationale* (explained why SBs maintain anxiety)
- Exposure + decreased SBs under *extinction rationale* (standard habituation framing)
- Exposure alone
Combined decreased-SB conditions outperformed exposure alone; cognitive rationale produced best results. The "cognitive rationale" operationalizes both SB elimination AND the belief-change mechanism — these are not separated.

**D. Clark cognitive therapy "SB experiment" (Clark et al. 2003/McManus et al. 2009):**
Within the Clark CT protocol, a specific "self-focused attention and safety behaviors experiment" in session 2: patient gives two short speeches/conversations, one with SBs and self-focus, one without. Experience the difference. Comparison produces insight; does not require the clinician to tell the patient the "right answer" about SBs.

**E. Social mishap exposures (Fang & Hofmann 2014):**
- Pre-exposure: collaboratively agreed behavioral goals per exposure trial (e.g., "maintain eye contact with every audience member"; "do not apologize")
- Instructions framed as *what to do* (positive direction) not just *what not to do*
- Verification: therapist observation + post-exposure review + sometimes videotape
- Explicit goal: give patient "no room to engage in any such avoidance behaviors"

**F. F-SET (Riccardi et al. 2016):**
- Therapist + patient identify patient's safety aids collaboratively
- Two elimination methods: (1) *fading* (gradual reduction), (2) *antiphobic exercises* (do the feared thing actively/exaggeratedly)
- For SAD: antiphobic = deliberately acting in ways that draw negative attention (stumbling, unusual clothing)

### 5. The Subtype Question: Not All Safety Behaviors Are Equal

Strong evidence that SB subtypes have different effects:

| Subtype | Effect on performer | Effect on interaction partner |
|---------|---------------------|-------------------------------|
| Avoidance (talk less, keep still, avoid self-disclosure) | Increased anxiety | Partner likes less, enjoys less, wants less future contact |
| Impression management (monitor appearance, try to convey good image) | Increased anxiety | Negligible effect on partner |

Implication: studies that "ban all safety behaviors" without distinguishing subtypes are treating a heterogeneous construct as unitary. Impression management behaviors may need different handling than avoidance.

### 6. The Availability vs. Utilization Distinction

Blakey & Abramowitz (2016) and the Blakey et al. 2019 RCT (not fully retrieved) distinguish:
- **Availability**: SBs present in the environment but patient asked not to use them
- **Utilization**: SBs actively used

The SBA condition in Blakey et al. claustrophobia study: *"Please only use these aids if you feel you must."* The SBU condition: participants actively used aids. Counterintuitively, SBU produced *faster* danger expectancy reduction — suggesting that having SBs available but not using them may signal danger more strongly than actively using them (inhibitory learning argument).

## Points of Consensus

- SBs maintain social anxiety by preventing disconfirmation of feared outcomes (Clark & Wells 1995; Rapee & Heimberg 1997) — broad consensus
- Dropping SBs during exposure produces better outcomes than exposure alone (Wells 1995; Morgan & Raffle 1999; Kim 2005) — multiple studies agree
- High social anxiety is associated with greater SB use — well established
- Both SBQ subscales (avoidance, impression management) show good internal consistency and discriminate clinical from non-clinical populations
- Avoidance-type SBs have stronger negative interpersonal consequences than impression management SBs

## Active Debates

1. **Are SBs always harmful during exposure?**
   - Traditional view (Wells 1995; Kim 2005): Yes — eliminate to maximize disconfirmation
   - Challenge (Milosevic & Radomsky 2008; Blakey & Abramowitz 2016): Not necessarily — SBs may facilitate approach behavior and not always impair fear reduction
   - Current best evidence: Effects depend on SB type, timing, and whether use contextualizes safety learning

2. **Should SBs be eliminated immediately or gradually?**
   - F-SET: Fading (gradual)
   - Wells/Clark/Morgan: Immediate elimination during exposure
   - **Blakey et al. (2019) RCT**: No significant difference between systematic fading (judicious use) and immediate elimination for spider phobia (N=60, d≈2.5 in both conditions). BUT: SBs were physical protective gear — not generalizable to SAD's interpersonal SBs.
   - Taylor & Alden (2011): SBs promoted social approach in SAD, suggesting early SB use may facilitate engagement even if it impairs performance quality — indirect support for gradual elimination approach.

3. **Do SBs impair learning or just impair context-generalization?**
   - Inhibitory learning view: SBs don't prevent fear reduction, they make it context-specific
   - Classic CBT view: SBs prevent belief change

4. **What distinguishes SBs from adaptive coping?**
   - Rachman et al.: some behaviors labeled SBs may be rational risk-reduction that does not maintain anxiety
   - No agreed operational criterion for distinguishing adaptive coping from maladaptive SBs

## Gaps in Literature

1. **Compliance verification**: Most studies rely on self-reported adherence to SB instructions. Blakey & Abramowitz (2016) explicitly confirmed compliance failures across the literature: some participants randomized to "no SB" conditions nevertheless performed SBs (Morgan & Raffle 1999), and vice versa. **Partial exception**: Blakey et al. (2019) coded 15% of sessions for treatment fidelity (interrater agreement 98.38%) — the only study with formal session-level compliance verification. However, this is for spider phobia, not SAD, and treatment fidelity coding (therapist adherence) is not the same as moment-to-moment SB use coding during exposure tasks.

2. **Confounded manipulations**: Standard experimental paradigm (McManus/Clark group) combines SBs with self-focused attention in a single manipulation. Pure SB-only manipulation designs are rare (Goodall 2019 is an exception for specific subtypes, but in analog population).

3. **Clinical replication**: Most detailed experimental data comes from analogue (non-clinical) populations. Clinical studies provide less procedural detail.

4. **Full item content**: SAFE full item list is not accessible without full text. The active safety behaviors factor (Factor 1) in particular is underspecified.

5. **Cognitive vs. behavioral SBs**: Most instruments and manipulations focus on overt behavioral SBs. Cognitive SBs (mental rehearsal, thought suppression, distraction) are theoretically important but harder to measure/manipulate.

6. **How "decreased" is defined**: Studies say "decrease safety behaviors" but rarely specify whether this means (a) complete elimination, (b) graded reduction, or (c) elimination of some behaviors while permitting others. Fading vs. immediate elimination is understudied.

> *"Focus on yourself and watch yourself during this conversation. Think about how you are coming across to the other person... be careful about what you say, deciding whether it is good enough to say before you say it."*

**From Goodall et al. (2019) — avoidance manipulation** (FULL TEXT, exact items):
> Participants instructed to perform: (1) *"avoid talking about yourself"*, (2) *"keep still"*, (3) *"talk less"*. Control condition: *"act as you normally would."*

**From Goodall et al. (2019) — impression management manipulation** (FULL TEXT, exact items):
> Participants instructed to perform: (1) *"picture how you appear to others"*, (2) *"check how you're coming across"*, (3) *"try to come across well"*

**From Fang & Hofmann (2014) — social mishap exposure protocol** (FULL TEXT, procedural description):
> *"It is essential that patients refrain from avoidance or safety behaviors... It is therefore advisable to provide detailed instructions to the patients to give them no room to engage in any such avoidance behaviors."* Behavioral goals collaboratively agreed before each exposure. Verification via therapist observation, post-exposure review, and videotape.

**From context-specific-2024 study — SBQ rating prompt** (FULL TEXT):
> SBs rated as behaviors used *"to make yourself feel safer or to try to prevent your feared outcome(s)"*

**From F-SET (Riccardi et al. 2016) — treatment framing** (from abstract/extraction):
> Safety aids conceptualized as *"behavioral and cognitive strategies that an individual engages in as a result of their anxiety, which may help relieve anxiety in the short term but likely contribute to anxiety in the long term."* Elimination via fading exercises and antiphobic exercises.

**From Wells et al. (1995) — experimental design** (from abstract, verbatim):
> *"All patients received one session of exposure alone and one session of exposure plus decrease in 'safety' behaviors in a counterbalanced within-subject design."* Exact instructions not accessible without full text.

**From Morgan & Raffle (1999) — CBT group** (from abstract):
> Treatment group received *"additional instructions to drop safety behaviours"* during exposure tasks. Exact wording not accessible.

**From Kim (2005) — three conditions** (from abstract):
> Three conditions: *"exposure with decreased safety behaviors under cognitive rationale"*; *"exposure with decreased safety behaviors under extinction rationale"*; *"exposure with no change in safety behavior."* Exact rationale/instructions not accessible.

### Q: Does the evidence favor SB elimination in SAD specifically, or is the field genuinely mixed?
**Asked**: 2026-03-16

**Answer**: For clinical SAD, the field is consistent: **every clinical treatment study** in the SAD domain found SB elimination beneficial or SBs harmful. The 9 studies from Blakey & Abramowitz (2016) Table 2 are: Garcia-Palacios & Botella (2003, n=1); Kim (2005, n=45); McManus et al. (2008, n=40); Morgan & Raffle (1999, n=30); Plasencia et al. (2011, n=93); Taylor & Alden (2010, n=80); Taylor & Alden (2011, n=80, nuanced: poorer performance but SBs promoted social approach); Wells et al. (1995, n=8); Wells & Papageorgiou (1998, n=40). Not a single SAD clinical study found SBs helpful.

The "mixed" meta-analytic finding (Meulders et al. 2016, n=23 studies: inconclusive) pools SAD with specific phobias. The positive SB evidence comes almost entirely from specific phobias (especially spider/snake), where SBs are physical protective gear rather than interpersonal impression management behaviors.

The one multi-session judicious use RCT (Blakey et al. 2019) produced a null result — but this was spider phobia (physical protective gear SBs), not SAD.

**Source**: blakey-abramowitz-2016, blakey-2019, meulders-2016

### Q: How was "judicious use" operationalized in the Blakey 2019 RCT?
**Asked**: 2026-03-16

**Answer**: E/JU condition: participants chose 2 items from a list of 8 physical protective items (eye goggles, dental visor face shield, long chemistry apron, long sleeve rain jacket, short work gloves, long chemistry gloves, boot/shoe covers, 12" clear plastic shield). Systematic fading over sessions: most helpful item used in sessions 1 & 2; second-most helpful item used in session 1 only; no SBs in session 3. Therapist explained SBs were helpful by making the situation less distressing and enhancing ability to remain.

This operationalization is the most ecologically valid test of Rachman et al. (2008) judicious use to date. But note: these SBs are physical protective gear, entirely unlike SAD's interpersonal/impression management SBs. SBs in both conditions were restricted to overt behaviors; mental SBs explicitly excluded.

**Source**: blakey-2019
