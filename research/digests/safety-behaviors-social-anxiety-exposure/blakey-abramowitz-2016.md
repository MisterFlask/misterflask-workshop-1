# Digest: Blakey & Abramowitz 2016 — Safety Behaviors Review from Inhibitory Learning Perspective

Source: [blakey-abramowitz-2016](../../sources/safety-behaviors-social-anxiety-exposure/blakey-abramowitz-2016.md)
Access: full-text
Last Updated: 2026-03-16

## Summary

Comprehensive systematic review (not meta-analysis) of the literature on safety behaviors during exposure therapy, analyzed through inhibitory learning theory (Craske et al. 2008). Reviews both sides: (1) why SBs interfere with exposure, and (2) why they might enhance it. Concludes that although SBs are not unconditionally deleterious, they generally tend to interfere and should be eliminated as soon as patients are willing. The most complete catalog of the empirical literature on SBs during exposure available.

## Key Findings

- **Definition**: Safety behaviors = "overt or covert actions performed to prevent, escape, or minimize a feared catastrophe and/or associated distress" (Telch & Lancaster, 2012)
- **Mechanisms of harm** (traditional): (1) cause misattributions of safety; (2) disrupt therapeutic information processing — communicate threat, increase threat perception, divert attention from disconfirmatory information
- **Mechanisms of harm** (inhibitory learning): (1) prevent maximal violation of negative expectancies; (2) prevent generalization of inhibitory associations; (3) prevent development of distress tolerance
- **Potential benefits**: (1) enhance acceptability/tolerability; (2) facilitate approach behavior; (3) promote self-efficacy and sense of control
- **Meta-analytic conclusion** (Meulders et al. 2016, cited within): aggregate data "inconclusive and could not provide strong evidence supporting either the removal or addition of safety behaviors during exposure"
- **Clinical recommendation**: "eliminate safety behaviors as quickly as anxious patients are willing"

| Taylor & Alden (2010) | 80 | Idiosyncratic (impression management) | Eliminating enhanced outcomes |
| Taylor & Alden (2011) | 80 | Idiosyncratic (impression management) | Poorer performance but SBs promoted social approach |

| Wells & Papageorgiou (1998) | 40 | Self-focused attention | Reducing self-focus enhanced outcomes |

**Key note**: Every SAD clinical study found that reducing/eliminating SBs improved outcomes. The positive SB findings are almost entirely from specific phobias, particularly spider/snake fears.

### Example Social Anxiety Safety Behaviors (Table 1)

Specific concerns and corresponding SBs listed:
- "I will stutter my words" → mentally rehearse before speaking; keep sentences short
- "Others will notice and negatively evaluate me for my somatic anxiety symptoms" → wear clothing that hides "problem areas"; keep anti-anxiety medication on hand
- "Others will think I'm a 'loner'" → text/use electronic devices in public; walk with others in public; frequently check social media

### Critical Methodological Issues Identified (Section 12)

1. **Brief exposures**: Studies used 3–30 min exposures; clinical protocols recommend 30–90 min
2. **Single-session designs**: Most studies single session; precludes testing "judicious use" (by definition, uses fading over multiple sessions)
3. **Manipulation confounds**: Some studies confounded SB use with treatment rationale (inconsistent rationales across conditions)
4. **Compliance failure**: "some participants randomized to 'no safety behavior' conditions nevertheless performed safety behaviors (e.g., Morgan & Raffle, 1999), and others randomized to 'with safety behavior' conditions did not always utilize safety behaviors (e.g., Rachman et al., 1986)" — **Future research should measure and ensure (or statistically correct for) instruction compliance**
5. **No follow-up or brief follow-up**: Most studies lack adequate follow-up
6. **"With/without" dichotomy is a poor test of judicious use**: Judicious use = strategic incorporation with eventual fading, not just "SBs permitted throughout"

## Operationalization of Key Concepts

| Concept | How Operationalized | Notes |
|---------|---------------------|-------|
| Safety behavior (definition) | "Overt or covert actions performed to prevent, escape, or minimize a feared catastrophe and/or associated distress" | Functional, not topographical |
| SB vs adaptive coping | SBs = functionally performed in absence of real threat; adaptive coping = not performed to prevent feared catastrophe | Thwaites & Freeston (2005) distinction |
| SB "availability" | SBs present in environment but patient not instructed to use | Sy et al. (2011) — availability alone can signal danger |
| SB "utilization" | Patient actively uses SBs | Distinct from availability |
| "Judicious use" | Strategic incorporation "in a limited manner and only for a limited period, especially in the early stages of treatment" (Rachman et al., 2008, p. 171) — includes eventual fading | Most "with SB" studies do NOT test this; they use SBs throughout |

**Critical operationalization note**: The most common experimental design (SBs throughout "with" condition vs. no SBs "without" condition) is NOT a test of judicious use. Judicious use requires fading. Most positive SB findings used non-clinical samples with specific phobias; these may not generalize to SAD.

## Methodology

- **Study Type**: Systematic qualitative review (not meta-analysis)
- **Inclusion criteria**: studies involving manipulation of behavior meeting SB definition, with at least one systematic outcome measure; anxiety-related conditions; published before October 2016
- **Databases**: PsycINFO and PubMed + reference list checking
- **Scope**: ~50+ primary studies reviewed; Table 2 catalogs ~40 quantitative studies

## Methodological Critique

### Strengths
- Most comprehensive qualitative synthesis available
- Covers both directions of argument (harmful vs. helpful)
- Provides full Table 2 catalog across studies with their SBs and findings
- Properly distinguishes judicious use from "SBs permitted throughout"

### Limitations
- Qualitative, not meta-analytic — cannot quantify effect sizes
- Published before Blakey et al. (2019) RCT which directly tested judicious use
- Does not separately analyze SAD vs. other anxiety disorders in any formal way

## Relevance to Research Question

This is the most comprehensive catalog of how SBs have been operationalized across the literature. Table 2 shows that virtually all SAD studies use "idiosyncratic" SB identification (not standardized lists), and most operationalize elimination as "instructions not to perform" without behavioral verification. The compliance failure note (p. 12) directly confirms the gap identified in our research question synthesis.
