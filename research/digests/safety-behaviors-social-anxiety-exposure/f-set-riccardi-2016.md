# Digest: Riccardi et al. 2016 — False Safety Behavior Elimination Therapy (F-SET)

Source: [f-set-riccardi-2016](../../sources/safety-behaviors-social-anxiety-exposure/f-set-riccardi-2016.md)
Access: abstract-only
Last Updated: 2026-03-14

## Summary

RCT of F-SET, a 5-session transdiagnostic treatment built entirely around identification and elimination of "false safety behaviors." Developed by Norman Schmidt's group at Florida State. Distinct conceptual frame from Clark/Wells CBT: calls safety behaviors "false safety aids" and emphasizes that they "relieve anxiety short-term but maintain it long-term." Treatment targets anxiety disorders broadly (SAD, GAD, panic).

## Key Findings

- F-SET outperformed waitlist on anxiety (d=2.01) and depression (d=2.16) — very large effects
- Change in avoidance strategies mediated symptom reduction (mediation analysis)
- Brief (5 sessions individual; earlier 10-session group format also tested)
- Protocol: (1) psychoeducation on anxiety nature; (2) discuss false vs. true fear structures; (3) identify patient's safety aids; (4) develop fading and antiphobic exercises; (5) eliminate avoidance strategies

## Operationalization of Key Concepts

| Concept | How Measured/Operationalized | Notes |
|---------|------------------------------|-------|
| "False safety behavior" | Behavioral and cognitive strategies that relieve anxiety short-term but likely contribute to it long-term | Functional definition; "false" = mistakenly believed to prevent feared outcomes |
| Safety aid identification | Therapist + patient collaboratively identify patient's specific safety aids | Patient-specific, not predefined list |
| Safety aid elimination | Combination of: (a) fading exercises (gradually eliminate SBs), (b) antiphobic exercises (do the opposite of what anxiety demands) | Two distinct methods |
| "Antiphobic" approach for SAD | Engaging in behaviors that deliberately draw attention (e.g., stumbling words, wearing unusual clothing, pretending to faint) | Opposite of SBs — exposure to feared outcomes directly |
| Fading | Gradual reduction of safety aid use across trials | Graded, not immediate elimination |
| Avoidance strategies change | Mediator measure: assessed via self-report before/after | Specific measure not detailed from abstract |
| Outcome measures | Standardized self-report, assessor severity ratings, diagnostic status | |

**Operationalization concerns**:
- "False safety behavior" is a rebranding of "safety behavior" with evaluative framing baked in (the "false" implies it doesn't actually prevent feared outcomes) — this is a therapeutic technique (challenging the belief that the SB works) rather than just an elimination procedure
- "Antiphobic" exercises are not traditional safety behavior elimination — they involve actively doing feared things in exaggerated form; more like interoceptive exposure or social mishap exposure
- N=28 is small; effect sizes may be inflated

## Methodology

- **Study Type**: RCT (F-SET vs. waitlist)
- **Sample**: N=28, mean age 28.5, 75% female; SAD + GAD + panic
- **Measures**: Standardized self-report, assessor ratings, diagnostic status; assessment at pre, post, 1-month follow-up
- **Duration**: 5 sessions individual

## Methodological Critique

### Strengths
- Mediation analysis provides mechanism evidence
- Large effect sizes
- Transdiagnostic approach allows broader application

### Limitations
- N=28: small; wide confidence intervals
- Waitlist (not active treatment) control
- Cannot separate "elimination of SBs" from general CBT effects (psychoeducation, exposure)
- "False safety behavior" framing conflates belief-level intervention with behavior-level intervention

## Relevance to Research Question

Shows one detailed, explicit framework for how SB *elimination* is operationalized as a treatment: patient-specific identification → fading → antiphobic exercises. The "fading" vs. "immediate elimination" distinction is clinically important and rarely made explicit in other studies.

## Connections to Other Sources

- Contrasts with Wells/Clark approach: F-SET identifies SBs collaboratively but uses "fading" rather than immediate elimination
- Social mishap exposures (Fang et al. 2014) share the "antiphobic" approach
- Milosevic & Radomsky (2008) argued for "judicious use" — F-SET's fading approach is a middle ground
