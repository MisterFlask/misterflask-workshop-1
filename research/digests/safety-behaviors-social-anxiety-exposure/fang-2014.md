# Digest: Fang & Hofmann 2014 — Social Mishap Exposures for SAD

Source: [fang-2014](../../sources/safety-behaviors-social-anxiety-exposure/fang-2014.md)
Access: full-text (open access)
Last Updated: 2026-03-14

## Summary

Clinical description of social mishap exposures as a treatment ingredient for SAD. Case-based paper describing treatment of "Mary" with social anxiety. Operationalizes SB elimination within the mishap exposure framework: exposures deliberately target the feared social outcomes (embarrassment, negative evaluation) that SBs are designed to prevent. SB elimination is integral to mishap exposure design.

## Key Findings

- "It is essential that patients refrain from avoidance or safety behaviors... It is therefore advisable to provide detailed instructions to the patients to give them no room to engage in any such avoidance behaviors."
- Behavioral goals are collaboratively agreed upon with the therapist *before each exposure*
- Post-exposure review confirms behavioral completion
- Videotape review used to demonstrate discrepancy between felt anxiety and visible anxiety

## Operationalization of Key Concepts

| Concept | How Measured/Operationalized | Notes |
|---------|------------------------------|-------|
| Safety behaviors in SAD | "Variety of avoidance and safety behaviors to reduce the risk of rejection" — prevent evaluation of feared outcomes | Consistent with Clark/Wells definition |
| SB elimination in mishap exposures | Collaboratively agreed behavioral goals before each exposure; detailed instructions "to give no room for avoidance behaviors" | Pre-exposure goal-setting |
| Verification of SB absence | (a) Therapist observation during exposure; (b) post-exposure review; (c) video review | Multi-method verification |
| Specific SBs targeted in Mary's case | Eye contact avoidance (limited contact during speeches); freezing (to minimize attention); over-apologizing; procrastination on phone calls | Patient-specific; derived collaboratively |
| Mishap exposure design | Exposure deliberately creates/allows the feared social mishap (embarrassment) to occur | Contrast with standard habituation-based exposure |
| Exposure examples | Request unusual hotel services; interrupt restaurant patrons; admit inability to read; wear visible bandages | Designed to produce social mishap without SBs |
| Antiphobic direction | Patient instructed to "maintain eye contact with every member of the audience at least once"; "no excessive apologizing" | Positive behavioral instruction, not just prohibition |

**CRITICAL OPERATIONALIZATION NOTE**: This paper makes explicit what many studies leave implicit — SB elimination is operationalized as:
1. **Pre-exposure**: Collaborative identification of patient's specific SBs for that situation
2. **Instruction**: Detailed behavioral instructions ruling out each specific SB
3. **During**: Therapist monitoring compliance
4. **Post**: Review of what actually happened (videotape or verbal review)

The "positive instruction" approach (tell patient what TO DO rather than only what NOT to do) is a key practical point.

## Methodology

- **Study Type**: Clinical case description (not RCT or experimental study)
- **Sample**: Single case ("Mary") — generalizability limited
- **Measures**: Described narrative, not quantified
- **Duration**: Not specified

## Methodological Critique

### Strengths
- Rare level of procedural detail about SB elimination in practice
- Positive behavioral instruction approach is practically useful
- Multi-method verification (observation, review, video)

### Limitations
- Single case: no statistical evidence of efficacy
- "Mary" is a composite/illustrative case — may not reflect actual treatment
- Cannot separate mishap exposure effects from SB elimination effects

## Relevance to Research Question

Best available account of how SB elimination is operationalized in clinical practice: pre-exposure collaborative goal-setting, detailed behavioral instructions, monitoring, and post-exposure review. The "no room for avoidance" language is important — suggests instructions must be specific enough to close off all escape routes.

## Connections to Other Sources

- Consistent with F-SET (Riccardi 2016) "antiphobic" approach: doing the opposite of what anxiety demands
- Contrast with Wells (1995) which used patient-specific SB identification but in a briefer experimental context
- Consistent with Kim (2005) finding that cognitive rationale (explaining *why* SBs maintain anxiety) improves outcomes
