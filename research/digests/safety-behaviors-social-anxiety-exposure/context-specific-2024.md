# Digest: Context-Specific Safety Behaviors Study 2024 — SAD vs. MDD

Source: [context-specific-2024](../../sources/safety-behaviors-social-anxiety-exposure/context-specific-2024.md)
Access: full-text (open access)
Last Updated: 2026-03-14

## Summary

Examined SB subtypes (avoidance vs. impression management) across two different social contexts (affiliation vs. performance) in SAD, MDD, and non-patient controls (n=132). Uses modified Safety Behaviour Questionnaire (SBQ). Key finding: impression management SBs are context-specific in SAD (higher in affiliation than speech), while avoidance SBs are consistently negative across contexts.

## Key Findings

- SBQ modified version used: 9-item impression management (α=.87), 6-item avoidance (α=.85); 0-8 scale
- Participants rated SBs "used to make yourself feel safer or to try to prevent your feared outcome(s)"
- Two tasks: social affiliation (~25 min with trained confederate) and social performance (5-min impromptu speech)
- SAD group used significantly more impression management SBs during affiliation vs. speech
- MDD group showed no contextual difference
- Avoidance SBs consistently related to negative outcomes across both contexts
- Impression management neutral in affiliation context, potentially beneficial in performance context

## Operationalization of Key Concepts

| Concept | How Measured/Operationalized | Notes |
|---------|------------------------------|-------|
| Safety behaviors | SBQ modified: 9-item IM + 6-item avoidance, 0-8 scale | Rating prompt: "used to make yourself feel safer or to try to prevent your feared outcome(s)" |
| Impression management (9 items) | Monitoring appearance, concealing anxiety, acting agreeable, acting to convey positive impressions, picturing appearance, maintaining behavioral control, acting normally, trying to come across well, "acted very agreeable" | α=.87 |
| Avoidance (6 items) | Avoided talking about yourself, said little or nothing, "talked as little as possible" | α=.85 |
| Social affiliation task | ~25-minute interaction with trained confederate | Naturalistic interaction |
| Social performance task | 5-minute impromptu speech | Evaluative performance context |
| Self-report timing | SBs rated *after* each task (retrospectively, about that task) | Post-task rating |

**Operationalization concerns**:
- Self-report after task may reflect what patients *intended* to do rather than what they did
- Rating prompt ("to make yourself feel safer") may not capture SBs patients are unaware of
- Modified SBQ vs. standard SBQ — items selected but full selection rationale not clear
- Confederate in affiliation task may behave differently than real social partner

## Methodology

- **Study Type**: Cross-sectional laboratory study with clinical and non-clinical groups
- **Sample**: n=132: SAD (n=69), MDD (n=30), non-patient controls (n=33); ages 18–39
- **Measures**: Modified SBQ (15 items), social anxiety and depression measures, task-specific SB ratings
- **Duration**: Single session; two tasks

## Methodological Critique

### Strengths
- Direct comparison SAD vs. MDD vs. controls
- Context-sensitive design (two task types)
- Good-sized clinical sample
- Adequate internal consistency

### Limitations
- Cross-sectional: cannot establish causality
- Self-report: patients rate their own SBs retrospectively
- Confederate may behave non-naturally
- No verification that rated SBs actually occurred

## Relevance to Research Question

Shows that SBs are context-specific: same patients use different SBs in different situations. Has direct implications for how "eliminating safety behaviors" should be operationalized — a blanket prohibition may not make sense if different subtypes operate differently in different contexts. Impression management may not need to be eliminated in all contexts.

## Connections to Other Sources

- Uses same SBQ subscales as PLOS ONE subtypes study (2019)
- Extends findings to clinical populations
- SBQ rating prompt ("to make yourself feel safer") directly operationalizes the functional definition from Blakey & Abramowitz (2016)
