# Digest: Goodall et al. 2019 — Sub-Types of Safety Behaviours

Source: [plos-subtypes-2019](../../sources/safety-behaviors-social-anxiety-exposure/plos-subtypes-2019.md)
Access: full-text (open access)
Last Updated: 2026-03-14

## Summary

Two-study paper distinguishing avoidance vs. impression management subtypes of safety behaviors. Study 1 (cross-sectional survey, n=487) examined relationship between SB subtypes and social anxiety. Study 2 (experimental dyadic, n=96 undergraduates) manipulated specific avoidance vs. impression management behaviors in dyadic conversation. Measured effects on self-reported anxiety, partner ratings, and independent assessor ratings.

## Key Findings

- Avoidance behaviors (n=8 items in SBQ): "talk less," "remain still," "avoid questions," "position oneself inconspicuously," "stay at group edges," "avoid self-disclosure," "minimize attention-drawing," "censor statements"
- Impression management behaviors (n=5 items in SBQ): "monitor how one appears," "attempt to convey positive impressions," "picture one's appearance," "maintain strict behavioral control," "act normally"
- Avoidance produced *broader* negative effects: increased performer anxiety, reduced partner enjoyment, decreased partner liking, lower assessor ratings
- Impression management increased only performer anxiety; partner and assessor effects were negligible
- Both subtypes increased within-situation anxiety for performer
- Self-rated SB adherence (Study 2): M=64.12%, SD=13.85%

## Operationalization of Key Concepts

| Concept | How Measured/Operationalized | Notes |
|---------|------------------------------|-------|
| SB subtypes (Study 1) | Social Behaviour Questionnaire (SBQ; Clark 2005): 28 items, 0-3 frequency scale, two-factor structure | Same measure as McManus/Hirsch studies |
| SBQ — avoidance | Factor loadings >0.40; "Talk less" highest loading (0.68) | 8-item subscale |
| SBQ — impression management | "Check that you are coming across well" highest loading (0.64) | 5-item subscale |
| Avoidance manipulation (Study 2) | Participants instructed to perform 3 specific avoidance behaviors: (1) "avoid talking about yourself," (2) "keep still," (3) "talk less" | 3-item subset of avoidance subscale |
| Impression management manipulation (Study 2) | Participants instructed to perform 3 specific IM behaviors: (1) "picture how you appear to others," (2) "check how you're coming across," (3) "try to come across well" | 3-item subset of IM subscale |
| Control condition | "When not required to perform safety behaviour, act as normally would" | No instructions |
| Compliance verification | Self-report: % of time felt they followed instruction (M=64%) | No behavioral verification |
| Outcome — performer | Self-rated anxiety (VAS), perceived partner liking | |
| Outcome — partner | Rated enjoyment of interaction, liking of performer, future interaction interest | Partner blind to condition |
| Outcome — observer | Independent assessors rated video recordings | Blind to condition |

**Operationalization concerns**:
- Compliance was self-reported; cannot verify participants actually performed the behaviors
- "Performing" an instruction like "try to come across well" is difficult to verify — cognitively complex
- 3 items out of 8/5 item subscale; selection may not represent the full subtype
- Undergraduate analogue sample; may not generalize to clinical SAD
- Confounds SB use with impression management instructions (impression management IS a behavior, but also resembles what many people do naturally)

## Methodology

- **Study Type**: Study 1 = cross-sectional survey; Study 2 = within-subjects experimental dyadic
- **Sample**: Study 1: n=487 online; Study 2: n=96 undergraduates in dyads
- **Measures**: SBQ (28-item, 0-3), VAS for anxiety, partner questionnaires, video-coded observer ratings
- **Duration**: Study 2 = single session, 2 × 5-minute conversations (within-subjects, counterbalanced)

## Methodological Critique

### Strengths
- Within-subjects design eliminates individual difference confounds
- Multi-method outcome: self, partner, and independent assessor
- First study to directly compare *differential* causal effects of SB subtypes experimentally
- Open access and detailed Methods

### Limitations
- Undergraduate analogue sample
- Compliance assessed only by self-report
- SB subtypes confounded with social function (avoidance reduces social contact; IM increases effort)
- Cannot separate SB effects from conversation-content differences (avoidance means less talking = less data)

## Relevance to Research Question

Provides the most methodologically detailed account of how safety behavior *manipulation* works in experimental studies: specific items are selected from the SBQ and participants are given those items as behavioral instructions. Shows both the benefits (specificity) and limits (construct validity, compliance verification) of this approach.

## Connections to Other Sources

- Uses same SBQ as McManus (2007) and Clark/Hirsch studies — good consistency
- Adolescent study (Selles 2021) used similar instruction format but different scale (ASBQ)
- Confirms that subtypes matter: avoidance is the more harmful type in interpersonal terms
