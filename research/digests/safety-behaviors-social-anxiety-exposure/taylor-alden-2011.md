# Digest: Taylor & Alden 2011 — Intra- and Interpersonal Consequences of Self-Protective Behavior in SAD

Source: [taylor-alden-2011](../../sources/safety-behaviors-social-anxiety-exposure/taylor-alden-2011.md)
Access: abstract-only
Last Updated: 2026-03-16

## Summary

Second paper from same n=80 SAD sample as Taylor & Alden (2010). Finding is theoretically nuanced: SBs were associated with poorer social performance quality but actually *promoted social approach behavior*. This is the study most directly relevant to the judicious use debate in a SAD-specific context.

## Key Findings

- SBs associated with poorer performance quality (consistent with standard SB-harm narrative)
- SBs promoted social approach behavior — contradicting the idea that SBs straightforwardly interfere with exposure
- This nuanced finding is from a clinical SAD sample (n=80)

## Methodology

- **Study Type**: Clinical study
- **Sample**: n=80, SAD; same sample as Taylor & Alden (2010)

## Methodological Critique

Abstract-only. The "SBs promoted social approach" finding is especially important to understand: Does this mean participants with SBs available/usable were more willing to enter the social situation? Or that SB use during the situation was associated with more approach behavior afterward? These have very different implications.

## Relevance to Research Question

Most directly relevant of the Taylor/Alden papers to the judicious use debate. If SBs promote approach to feared situations (even if they impair performance quality), this supports a role for SBs in facilitating early treatment engagement — consistent with Rachman et al. (2008) and the clinical recommendation in Blakey et al. (2019) that therapists need not be concerned if patients are initially unwilling to eliminate SBs.

## Connections to Other Sources

- Taylor & Alden (2010): Same sample; standard SB-harm finding
- Blakey et al. (2019): Clinical implication that initial SB use may be acceptable parallels this finding
- Blakey & Abramowitz (2016) Table 2: Note on this paper acknowledges the approach facilitation finding

## NEED FULL TEXT

> **Priority**: HIGH
>
> **What's Missing**: Exact operationalization of "social approach," whether approach was measured behaviorally or via self-report, how SBs were identified/manipulated, full results table.
>
> **Operationalization Gap**: "Promoted social approach" is ambiguous — need to know if this means willingness to enter situations vs. behavior during situations.
>
> **Why It Matters**: The approach-facilitation finding in SAD (not just specific phobias) is one of the few pieces of evidence supporting a nuanced use of SBs in SAD clinical work. Understanding what "approach" means here is critical.
