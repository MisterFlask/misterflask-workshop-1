# Research Question: Safety Behaviors in Social Anxiety Exposure Therapy

Created: 2026-03-14
Status: active
Slug: safety-behaviors-social-anxiety-exposure

## Core Question

How are safety behaviors operationalized in practice in studies on social anxiety disorder (SAD) exposure therapy? This includes: how they are defined, how they are measured/assessed, how they are manipulated experimentally, and what specific behaviors are studied.

## Context & Motivation

"Safety behaviors" (SBs) are actions taken to prevent or mitigate feared outcomes during anxiety-provoking situations. In social anxiety, classic examples include: avoiding eye contact, preparing what to say, holding a drink to prevent hand-trembling, wearing concealing clothing, rehearsing sentences before speaking. The dominant CBT model (Clark & Wells 1995; Rapee & Heimberg 1998) holds that SBs maintain anxiety by preventing disconfirmation of feared outcomes and drawing attention inward. However, some researchers (e.g., Rachman, Milosevic) have argued SBs can facilitate exposure. Understanding *how* SBs are operationalized is critical for evaluating whether studies are measuring the same construct and whether their conclusions generalize.

## Key Findings (Running)

**Updated 2026-03-14 after Phase 1 literature search:**

1. **Definitional consensus, operational divergence**: All major sources share a functional definition (SBs = actions preventing disconfirmation of feared outcomes), but operationalization varies widely: idiosyncratic patient-specific identification (Wells 1995), fixed item-sets from SBQ (Goodall 2019), verbatim scripted instructions (Selles 2021), or collaborative identification + fading (F-SET).

> "Safety behaviors are overt or covert actions performed to prevent, escape, or minimize a feared catastrophe and/or associated distress (Telch & Lancaster, 2012). Safety behaviors are functionally related to anxious beliefs and are logical, if unnecessary. To illustrate, a man with a fear of germs might wear gloves when using public transportation...Although topographically similar, safety behaviors are functionally distinct from adaptive coping...or non-pathological safety maneuvers..."
>
> — blakey-abramowitz-2016, Section 1, p. 2

2. **The standard experimental manipulation is confounded**: The dominant "with/without" conversation-task paradigm (McManus/Clark group) combines SB use with self-focused attention in a single instruction. Effects cannot be attributed to SBs alone.

> "We cannot draw conclusions about the extent to which effects are driven by self-focused attention and/or the use of safety behaviours, because both processes were manipulated together. This is because in reality it is difficult to conceive of an individual using common safety behaviours such as monitoring how they are coming across and rehearsing what they are going to say without focusing on themselves."
>
> — selles-2021, Limitations

3. **Two primary validated instruments**: (a) SBQ (Clark 2005): 28 items, 0–3 scale, two-factor (avoidance + impression management); (b) SAFE (Cuming 2009): 32 items, 5-point scale, three-factor (active SBs + subtle restriction + symptom concealment).

> "The avoidance factor comprised eight behaviors: 'Talk less,' 'Keep still,'...'Censor what you are going to say.' The impression management factor included five behaviors: 'Check that you are coming across well,'...'Try to act normal.' Both subscales demonstrated acceptable internal consistency (avoidance α = .76; impression management α = .75)."
>
> — plos-subtypes-2019, Measures (SBQ factor content)

[Full paragraph unavailable for SAFE — cuming-2009 is abstract-only]

4. **SB subtypes matter**: Avoidance SBs (talk less, keep still, avoid eye contact) have stronger negative interpersonal effects than impression management SBs. Studies that treat SBs as unitary may miss this.

> "Both safety behavior types increased subjective anxiety in performers. However, avoidance behaviors produced broader negative effects: participants thought they 'looked more anxious,' 'enjoyed the conversation less,' and were 'less keen on having a further conversation.' Additionally, conversational partners 'rated them as appearing more anxious and liked them less'...Impression management behaviors showed no comparable interpersonal deterioration."
>
> — plos-subtypes-2019, Results Study 2

5. **How "banning" is operationalized** — at least 6 distinct approaches identified: (a) Wells 1995 counterbalanced sessions; (b) Morgan group CBT instructions; (c) Kim 2005 three-condition with cognitive/extinction rationale; (d) Clark CT "SB experiment" using within-patient demonstration; (e) Fang social mishap exposures with collaborative pre-exposure goals; (f) F-SET fading + antiphobic approach.

> "It is essential that patients refrain from avoidance or safety behaviors, such as apologizing, or any other behavior that might lessen the patients' anxiety in the situation. For this reason, it is advisable to provide detailed instructions to the patients to give them no room to engage in any such avoidance behaviors."
>
> — fang-2014, Procedure (approach e; other approaches abstract-only)

6. **Cognitive rationale improves outcomes**: Kim (2005) found that explaining *why* SBs maintain anxiety (cognitive rationale) produced better outcomes than extinction-only framing, even when both conditions decreased SBs. The mechanism is not just behavioral.

[Full paragraph unavailable — kim-2005 is abstract-only]

7. **Compliance verification is weak across the literature**: Most studies do not verify that participants in "no SB" conditions actually refrained, or that participants in "with SB" conditions actually used SBs.

> "Considering that some participants randomized to 'no safety behavior' conditions nevertheless performed safety behaviors (e.g., Morgan & Raffle, 1999), and others randomized to 'with safety behavior' conditions did not always utilize safety behaviors (e.g., Rachman et al., 1986), future research should measure and ensure (or statistically correct for) instruction compliance."
>
> — blakey-abramowitz-2016, Section 12, p. 12

**Added 2026-03-16 from Blakey & Abramowitz 2016 full text:**

8. **SAD clinical studies are consistent**: Every clinical treatment study in the SAD domain (Wells 1995; McManus 2008; Morgan & Raffle 1999; Kim 2005; Plasencia et al. 2011; Taylor & Alden 2010/2011) found SB elimination beneficial or SBs harmful. The positive SB findings (Milosevic & Radomsky 2008; Bandura et al. 1974) are almost entirely from specific phobias.

> "It is important to identify the contexts in which safety behaviors may be benign or beneficial. The majority of research offering support for the judicious use of safety behaviors is circumscribed to specific phobias. In contrast, safety behaviors are often labeled as harmful in other exposure contexts (e.g., social phobia) or altogether unstudied (e.g., PTSD)."
>
> — blakey-abramowitz-2016, Section 14, p. 13

9. **"With/without" studies are poor tests of judicious use**: Judicious use = systematic fading over sessions, not just "SBs permitted throughout." Most prior studies used SBs throughout treatment, which is not what Rachman et al. (2008) proposed.

> "A final issue regards the degree to which safety behaviors are 'judiciously used' in research. The judicious use of safety behaviors refers to the careful and strategic use of safety behavior applied 'in a limited manner and only for a limited period, especially in the early stages of treatment' (Rachman et al., 2008, pp. 171). Several clinical treatment studies showed superior effects of safety behavior fading (see Table 2); however, most experimental studies either promoted ('yes') or prevented ('no') safety behavior use. In this sense, dichotomized 'with or without' studies may be poor tests of the judicious use of safety behaviors."
>
> — blakey-abramowitz-2016, Section 12, p. 12

10. **The only multi-session judicious use RCT (Blakey et al. 2019) found no difference**: Spider phobia, N=60, 4 sessions, systematic fading (2 SBs → 1 SB → 0 SBs): E/ESB and E/JU produced equally large effects (d≈2.5) with no group differences at 1-month follow-up. BUT: SBs used were physical protective gear (goggles, gloves) — not interpersonal impression management behaviors relevant to SAD.

> "Our primary hypothesis—that E/ESB participants would demonstrate greater improvement than E/JU participants along self-report and behavioral symptom measures at follow-up—was not supported. Participants in both conditions evidenced large (and comparable) improvements in self-reported and behavioral symptoms from pre-to posttreatment, which were maintained over the follow-up period. Thus, our findings lend support to the claim that safety behaviors do not necessarily interfere with exposure. It is important to bear in mind, however, that whereas previous experiments on this topic (with the exception of Levy & Radomsky, 2016) required participants to use safety behaviors during the entirety of exposure, safety behaviors were systematically faded over the course of multiple exposure sessions in the current study."
>
> — blakey-2019, Section 5, p. 7

11. **Safety misattribution confirmed**: Blakey et al. (2019) E/JU participants made significantly more SB-related exposure outcome attributions than expected by chance — consistent with Salkovskis (1991) misattribution hypothesis.

> "Our results are in line with the proposition that patients are prone to misattribute the non-occurrence of a feared exposure outcome to safety behavior use (Salkovskis, 1991). Specifically, participants in the E/JU condition made more safety behavior-related attributions than would be expected by chance, which suggests individuals performing safety behaviors during exposure do not entirely conclude that their fear-based expectancies are mistaken. Thus, a clinical implication is that therapists should explicitly frame a successful exposure outcome as discordant with the preconceived expectancy, making sure their patients 'give credit where credit is due'..."
>
> — blakey-2019, Section 5, p. 7

## Open Questions

- Are there validated, psychometrically-tested scales specifically for SBs in SAD, or is ad hoc behavioral observation/self-report the norm?
- How do different studies distinguish SBs from avoidance behaviors vs. cognitive strategies?
- Do experimental manipulation studies use drop-in (add SBs) or drop-out (eliminate SBs) designs, or both?
- How do researchers handle the distinction between subtle in-situation SBs and broader avoidance?
- Are SBs operationalized differently depending on whether the outcome of interest is anxiety, perceived social performance, or actual social outcomes (observer ratings)?

- Rachman et al. (2008) / Milosevic & Radomsky challenged the standard view, leading to debate about whether SBs can be helpful in early exposure—this has implications for how "SB use" is defined and measured in intervention studies.

> "As mentioned, not all research supports recommendations to eliminate safety behaviors at the start of exposure (see Table 2). Rachman et al. (2008) recently proposed the 'judicious use' of safety behaviors: the careful and strategic implementation of safety behaviors in the early and/or most challenging stages of treatment. Advocates of this method highlight positive consequences, such as enhanced treatment acceptability and approach behavior (e.g., Levy & Radomsky, 2014)."
>
> — blakey-abramowitz-2016, Section 8, p. 9
