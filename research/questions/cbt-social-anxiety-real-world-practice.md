# Research Question: How CBT for Social Anxiety Is Actually Practiced in the Real World

Created: 2025-01-16
Status: active
Slug: cbt-social-anxiety-real-world-practice

## Core Question

How do therapists actually deliver CBT for social anxiety disorder in routine clinical practice, as opposed to the manualized protocols used in RCTs? What components get emphasized, dropped, or modified? What's the gap between "theoretically optimal CBT" and "CBT in the wild"?

## Context & Motivation

This connects to the broader thesis around social anxiety treatment. If the blog argues that certain mechanisms (e.g., safety behavior elimination, exposure without safety behaviors, metacognitive approaches) are key to effective treatment, it matters whether practicing therapists actually implement these components.

Relevant blog posts:
- "Maybe Social Anxiety Is Just You Failing At Mind Control" - core thesis on approval-seeking as mechanism
- "Confidence Engineering" - MCT-inspired approach, questions whether standard CBT addresses the right targets
- The Slutcon postmortem suggests that even brief, non-clinical interventions can produce meaningful change when they target the right mechanism

Key sub-questions:
1. Do therapists actually conduct in-session exposure for social anxiety?
2. How rigorously do therapists address safety behaviors?
3. What proportion of "CBT" in practice is actually just supportive therapy with cognitive reframing?
4. Does training/supervision quality predict adherence to evidence-based components?

## Key Findings (Running)

### Exposure Therapy is Systematically Underused

**US Data (Freiheit 2004, Whiteside 2016):**
- Only 61% of therapists report providing exposure therapy for childhood anxiety
- 26% seldom/never use ERP for OCD
- 76% seldom/never use interoceptive exposure for panic
- Less than 1/3 regularly use exposure for social anxiety

**Dutch Data (van Minnen 2015):**
- Much better: 97.8% of CBT therapists use exposure
- BUT: exposure mostly happens as homework (89-91%), not in-session
- Therapist-directed in vivo exposure only used by 35-52%
- 22% of panic patients get no interoceptive exposure
- 15% of OCD patients get no ERP

### Primary Cause: Therapist's Own Anxiety and Negative Beliefs

**Whiteside 2016 key finding:**
- Negative beliefs about exposure = strongest predictor of non-use (d=1.37)
- Therapists fear exposure will "exacerbate anxiety, cause harm, damage therapeutic alliance"
- Therapists high on anxiety sensitivity report more barriers, exclude more clients

**Waller 2009 framework:**
- "Therapist drift" = shift from "doing therapies" to "talking therapies"
- Caused by therapists' own "cognitive distortions, emotional reactions, and safety behaviors"
- Therapist avoids patient distress → avoids behavioral interventions → patient doesn't improve

### Training Matters But Doesn't Fully Solve It

**Whiteside 2016:**
- PhD psychologists: 86% use exposure
- Master's-level counselors: 49%
- Pure CBT orientation: 76% use exposure
- Non-CBT orientations: 36%

**van Minnen 2015:**
- Even among certified CBT therapists, 24-56% want more training
- Workplace lacking protocols (55%), materials (22%)

### What Therapists Do Instead

- Cognitive restructuring (used by 67-84% of therapists)
- "Talking therapy" - supportive discussion without behavioral intervention
- Relaxation techniques (sometimes used as safety behavior!)
- Allow safety behaviors during exposure (40% of exposure therapists do this for panic)

## Open Questions

- **Safety behavior elimination specifically**: Do therapists actively target safety behaviors, or just do "exposure" while allowing them? The van Minnen finding that 40% allow safety behaviors during panic exposure is alarming.
- **Session coding studies**: The surveys tell us what therapists *say* they do. Are there studies that actually code session recordings?
- **SAD-specific data**: Most data is general anxiety or OCD/panic. Social anxiety may have unique patterns given it's more ambiguous what "exposure" means.
- **Metacognitive components**: Does anyone even attempt to address threat monitoring / rumination in practice?

## Conflicts & Tensions

**The Dutch vs US discrepancy**: Dutch CBT therapists report much higher exposure use (98% vs ~60%). Possible explanations:
1. Dutch sample was CBT association members only (selection bias toward committed CBT practitioners)
2. Training systems differ (Dutch CBT training may be more behavioral)
3. 7-year gap between surveys
4. Self-report bias - everyone *says* they do exposure

**Effectiveness studies show CBT "works" in routine practice**: Morina 2023 found effect sizes of d=0.9-1.2 in training clinics. But this may be because training clinics have more supervision/adherence than typical community practice.
